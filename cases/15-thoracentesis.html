Pleural Space Anatomy

The pleural space is a potential space between the visceral pleura (covering the lung surface) and parietal pleura (lining the chest wall, diaphragm, and mediastinum). Normal volume: ~15 mL per hemithorax. Excess fluid accumulates when formation exceeds absorption — by increased capillary hydrostatic pressure, decreased oncotic pressure, increased capillary permeability, or impaired lymphatic drainage.

Pleural Fluid Formation Rates

  • Normal production: ~0.1 mL/kg/hour (cleared by lymphatics)
  • Radiographically visible on PA CXR: >200–300 mL (blunting of costophrenic angle)
  • Lateral decubitus CXR: detects as little as 50–100 mL
  • Ultrasound: detects as little as 20 mL

Indications & Contraindications

Diagnostic Indications

  • New, unexplained pleural effusion (especially unilateral)
  • Any effusion >1 cm on lateral decubitus CXR or ultrasound in hospitalized patient with uncertain etiology
  • Suspected malignant, exudative, parapneumonic, or tuberculous effusion
  • Infection/empyema evaluation (pH, glucose, LDH, culture)
  • Suspected hemothorax or chylothorax

Therapeutic Indications

  • Symptomatic relief of dyspnea from large effusion
  • Respiratory failure from tension hydrothorax
  • Drainage of complicated parapneumonic effusion / empyema
  • Reexpansion of collapsed lung
  • Malignant effusion palliation (consider PleurX catheter for recurrent effusions)

Relative Contraindications (No Absolute Contraindications)

  • Coagulopathy: INR >2.0 or platelets <50,000 — consider FFP or platelet transfusion, or use ultrasound guidance; risk is lower than often perceived
  • Therapeutic anticoagulation: hold per drug half-life if non-urgent; proceed under US guidance if urgent
  • Small effusion (<1 cm on US): high pneumothorax risk; consider ultrasound-guided approach by experienced provider
  • Mechanical ventilation: pneumothorax risk higher; proceed if indicated, with caution
  • Uncooperative patient: use sedation or defer if not urgent
  • Single lung/contralateral hemithorax non-functional: any pneumothorax is catastrophic — maximum caution

Technique & Ultrasound Guidance

Ultrasound Guidance: Standard of Care

Real-time ultrasound guidance reduces pneumothorax risk by 70% compared to landmark-based thoracentesis (Gordon et al., CHEST 2010). Ultrasound also identifies effusion size, loculations, distance to lung, and safe insertion point. Point-of-care ultrasound is now the standard of care for thoracentesis.

Patient Positioning

  • Preferred: Seated upright, leaning forward with arms resting on overbed table — fluid gravitates to posterior costophrenic sulcus
  • Supine/lateral decubitus: For patients unable to sit (mechanically ventilated, orthopedic limitations) — use US to identify fluid collection

Landmark Approach (Without Real-Time US)

  1. Confirm effusion on US — mark safe insertion point with indelible marker
  2. Two intercostal spaces below upper level of dullness on percussion (and US-confirmed fluid)
  3. Avoid below 9th rib (diaphragm), above rib (neurovascular bundle under inferior rib margin)
  4. Standard site: posterior, between posterior axillary line and midline, 7th–9th intercostal space
  5. Insert needle just superior to the upper margin of the lower rib (to avoid the neurovascular bundle)

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Informed consent; verify imaging; confirm laterality
  2. Full sterile technique (gown, gloves, drapes)
  3. Identify insertion site with US; mark skin; maintain sterile US probe cover
  4. Local anesthesia: inject 1% lidocaine in skin → subcutaneous → rib periosteum → pleural space (aspirate air/fluid to confirm before full anesthetic injection)
  5. Needle insertion (18–21 gauge; typically syringe + stopcock system or commercial kit)
  6. On fluid return: advance catheter over needle (Seldinger or catheter-over-needle technique)
  7. Connect to evacuated collection system or syringe/stopcock for samples
  8. Collect samples: LDH, total protein, glucose, pH (in ABG syringe, no heparin), cell count + differential, Gram stain/culture, cytology; specific additional tests based on clinical scenario
  9. Remove catheter; apply sterile dressing
  10. Post-procedure CXR: assess for pneumothorax, lung re-expansion

Volume Limits for Therapeutic Drainage

Safely drain up to 1–1.5 liters per session. Draining more risks re-expansion pulmonary edema (RPE) — especially when the lung has been compressed >3–7 days. If draining a large effusion: use manometry (stop if pleural pressure <−20 cmH₂O) or stop at 1.5 L regardless. Cough or chest tightness = stop. RPE occurs in ~0.5% of large-volume thoracenteses and can be fatal.

Light's Criteria: Exudate vs. Transudate

Light's Criteria (1972) — Still Gold Standard

A pleural effusion is an exudate if ≥1 of:

  • Pleural fluid protein / Serum protein >0.5
  • Pleural fluid LDH / Serum LDH >0.6
  • Pleural fluid LDH >2/3 upper limit of normal serum LDH

Sensitivity 98%, Specificity 83% for exudate. ~25% of transudates are misclassified as exudates — apply serum-to-fluid albumin gradient correction if exudate by Light's but CHF/cirrhosis/nephrosis suspected (albumin gradient >1.2 g/dL = true transudate).

Transudate vs. Exudate — Clinical Implications

Transudate (Systemic Process)

  • Heart failure (most common) — bilateral > unilateral; responds to diuresis
  • Cirrhosis (hepatic hydrothorax)
  • Nephrotic syndrome
  • Hypoalbuminemia
  • Peritoneal dialysis
  • Myxedema (hypothyroidism)
  • Constrictive pericarditis

Exudate (Local Process)

  • Parapneumonic effusion / Empyema
  • Malignancy (lung, breast, lymphoma, mesothelioma)
  • PE (bloody exudate; up to 30% of PEs)
  • Tuberculosis
  • Connective tissue disease (RA, SLE)
  • Pancreatitis (left-sided)
  • Chylothorax (triglycerides >110)
  • Postcardiac injury syndrome (Dressler's)

When Cytology is Negative but Malignancy Suspected

  • Repeat thoracentesis (sensitivity increases to ~80% with second specimen)
  • Medical thoracoscopy (pleuroscopy) — biopsy under direct visualization; sensitivity >95%
  • CT-guided pleural biopsy — for thickened pleura
  • Pleural biopsy (Abrams needle) — largely replaced by thoracoscopy; still used in TB (high sensitivity for granulomas)

Clinical Pearls

🔊Ultrasound before thoracentesis is non-negotiable. Landmark-guided thoracentesis without US is associated with 6× higher pneumothorax risk. Even if you confirm the site with US and then step away, position changes can shift the fluid significantly. Real-time guidance is ideal; pre-marking with US is the minimum standard.
💡Light's criteria misclassify ~25% of transudates (especially CHF on diuretics, which increases pleural fluid protein relative to serum). When CHF/cirrhosis/nephrosis is clinically suspected but effusion meets exudate criteria: calculate the serum-to-pleural albumin gradient. If >1.2 g/dL → true transudate despite Light's.
⚠️Stop at 1–1.5 liters. Re-expansion pulmonary edema (RPE) occurs when long-collapsed lung re-expands too rapidly — a hydrostatic/permeability injury to the re-expanded parenchyma. Symptoms: cough, chest tightness, frothy sputum during or after drainage. Treatment: oxygen, CPAP/BiPAP; mortality ~rare but reported. Prevention: volume limit + pleural pressure monitoring.
🩺pH <7.20 in a parapneumonic effusion mandates chest tube drainage — do NOT send pH in a gel-separator tube (the separator material inactivates the hydrogen ions). Send pleural fluid pH in a heparinized ABG syringe, same as blood gas, immediately transported on ice to the blood gas analyzer. Delay or wrong collection tube yields falsely high pH.
🔬Cytology requires volume. A single 10 mL aliquot for malignant cytology is inadequate. Send 60–100 mL per specimen. Sensitivity for malignant cytology: ~60% with one sample, ~80% with two samples, >90% with three samples or thoracoscopic biopsy. If malignancy is strongly suspected and two thoracenteses are negative → medical thoracoscopy.
