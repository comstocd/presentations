PICC & Central Venous Catheter Complications

Central venous catheters are among the most common invasive devices in hospitalized patients and among the most common sources of preventable harm. CLABSI is one of the leading causes of hospital-acquired infection. Mechanical complications occur at insertion and throughout the life of the line. Every clinician managing inpatients must know how to recognize, prevent, and manage catheter-related complications.

Midline ≠ PICC

A midline catheter terminates in the axillary or subclavian vein — it is not a central venous catheter. It cannot be used for vesicants (vancomycin, potassium >60 mEq/L, hypertonic saline, vasopressors, chemotherapy, TPN). Confusing a midline with a PICC and infusing these agents causes chemical phlebitis and tissue necrosis. Verify tip position before infusing anything that requires central access.

Indications & Site Selection

Indications for Central Venous Access

  • Hemodynamic monitoring (CVP, ScvO2, PAC)
  • Administration of vesicants, hyperosmolar solutions, or medications requiring central delivery (vasopressors, TPN, potassium >60 mEq/L, hypertonic saline >3%)
  • Prolonged IV antibiotic therapy (IV antibiotics >1 week are the most common PICC indication)
  • Absence of adequate peripheral venous access
  • Hemodialysis / apheresis / plasmapheresis
  • Rapid fluid/blood product administration (large-bore CVC)
  • Long-term chemotherapy or immunotherapy

Ultrasound Guidance Is Standard of Care

Real-time ultrasound guidance for CVC insertion (particularly IJ) reduces insertion failures, arterial punctures, pneumothorax, and number of attempts. AHRQ and multiple guidelines mandate ultrasound guidance for IJ CVCs. For subclavian access, in-plane ultrasound reduces complications vs. landmark technique. The only scenario where landmark technique alone may be appropriate is a true emergency without time for US setup.

Tip Position Matters: The Cavoatrial Junction

The ideal CVC tip position is the cavoatrial junction (CAJ) or distal SVC, approximately 2–3 cm above the carina on CXR. Tips too proximal (mid-SVC) increase thrombosis risk. Tips in the right atrium increase arrhythmia and perforation risk. Tips in the right ventricle cause arrhythmias and endocardial damage. Tips against the SVC wall cause SVC perforation and cardiac tamponade (rare but fatal). Confirm position on CXR before use.

CLABSI: Catheter-Related Bloodstream Infection

CLABSI: A Leading Preventable Cause of Death

Tens of thousands of CLABSIs occur in US ICUs annually — pre-bundle implementation estimates reached ~30,000/year, and rates have declined significantly with widespread bundle adoption (NHSN data show substantial reductions since 2008), though CLABSIs remain a major source of preventable harm. Associated mortality is 12–25% per episode in published series, though attributable mortality varies when controlling for underlying illness severity. Attributable costs have been estimated at $45,000–$55,000 per infection in older analyses. A CLABSI is a sentinel event at most institutions.

CLABSI vs. CRBSI: Definitions

  • CRBSI (catheter-related bloodstream infection): Clinical definition requiring microbiologic evidence linking the catheter as the source (differential time to positivity, catheter tip culture). Used for clinical decision-making.
  • CLABSI (central line-associated bloodstream infection): CDC/NHSN surveillance definition. BSI in a patient with a central line in place for >2 days, with no other identified source. Used for reporting and quality metrics. CLABSI is a broader definition and may include infections that are not truly catheter-related.

Pathogenesis

  • Extraluminal route (most common, early): Skin organisms migrate along the outer catheter surface from the insertion site. Dominant in the first 2 weeks.
  • Intraluminal route (most common, late): Hub contamination → organisms colonize inner lumen → biofilm formation. Dominant after 2 weeks, especially with frequent hub access.
  • Hematogenous seeding: Remote infection seeds the catheter (less common).
  • Contaminated infusate: Rare; typically pharmacy or compounding error.

Diagnosis

  • Draw ≥2 sets of blood cultures: one from the catheter lumen and one from a peripheral vein (or two peripheral sites if catheter draw is not possible)
  • Differential time to positivity (DTP): Catheter draw becomes positive ≥2 hours before peripheral draw → supports CRBSI (sensitivity ~81%, specificity ~92%)
  • Catheter tip culture (semiquantitative; ≥15 CFU = significant) — only if line is removed
  • Do not culture catheter tips routinely on removal; only if CRBSI is suspected

Antibiotic Lock Therapy

Used to salvage tunneled catheters or ports in patients where line removal is not feasible (lack of venous access, coagulopathy, patient refusal). Concentrated antibiotic solution is instilled into the catheter lumen and allowed to dwell for hours. Used adjunctively with systemic antibiotics; not appropriate for S. aureus, Candida, tunnel infections, or port pocket infections. Requires ID guidance.

Catheter-Related Thrombosis

Upper Extremity DVT (UEDVT) & PICC-Related Thrombosis

Catheter-related upper extremity DVT (UEDVT) is increasingly recognized as PICC use has grown. The risk is substantially higher with PICCs than with non-tunneled CVCs. Approximately 2–6% of PICC insertions are complicated by symptomatic DVT; subclinical DVT rates are much higher (up to 38% in some series).

Risk Factors for Catheter-Related UEDVT
  • PICC (vs. other CVC types) — single greatest risk factor for UEDVT
  • Multiple lumens
  • Larger catheter diameter relative to vein diameter
  • Insertion in basilic > brachial > cephalic vein
  • Active malignancy
  • Prior UEDVT or catheter in same vessel
  • Hypercoagulable state
  • Infection / CLABSI
  • Tip malposition (too proximal)
Clinical Presentation
  • Upper arm or shoulder swelling, erythema, warmth
  • Arm or hand edema
  • Pain along the course of the vein
  • Collateral venous dilation visible on shoulder/chest
  • Catheter malfunction (resistance to flushing, inability to aspirate blood)
  • May be asymptomatic — found incidentally on imaging
  • PE is a recognized complication of UEDVT (though less common than with lower extremity DVT)

Diagnosis

  • Compression duplex ultrasound of the upper extremity — first-line; sensitive for axillary, subclavian, and brachial veins; less sensitive for central veins (IJ, brachiocephalic, SVC)
  • CT venography — if central vein thrombosis suspected (SVC syndrome) or US inconclusive
  • MR venography — alternative to CT; no iodinated contrast

Management of Catheter-Related UEDVT

  • If catheter is no longer needed: Remove it and anticoagulate for 3 months
  • If catheter is still needed and functioning: Anticoagulate while catheter remains in place; remove when no longer needed; anticoagulate for minimum 3 months or until catheter is removed (whichever is longer)
  • Anticoagulation agent: LMWH or DOAC (rivaroxaban or apixaban) preferred over warfarin per CHEST guidelines; LMWH preferred in active malignancy
  • Catheter removal alone without anticoagulation is not sufficient — thrombosis may progress to SVC or pulmonary circulation
  • Thrombolysis and thrombectomy are rarely needed for UEDVT; reserved for phlegmasia or SVC syndrome with hemodynamic compromise

SVC Syndrome

Obstruction of the superior vena cava causing impaired venous drainage from the head, neck, and upper extremities. Causes: malignancy (most common), thrombosis (catheter-related increasingly recognized), fibrosing mediastinitis. Features: facial plethora and edema, upper extremity swelling, dilated collateral veins on chest wall, dyspnea, headache worsened by bending forward. Management: treat underlying cause; anticoagulation for thrombotic SVC; SVC stenting for malignant obstruction.

Line Removal: When & How

When to Remove

  • Line is no longer clinically indicated — the most important reason and the most commonly delayed
  • CLABSI with S. aureus or Candida (always remove)
  • Tunnel infection or port pocket infection
  • Symptomatic catheter-related thrombosis that cannot be managed conservatively
  • Catheter fracture, malposition unrepairable under fluoroscopy, or persistent occlusion
  • Patient request

Lines Should Be Removed as Soon as No Longer Needed

The single most effective intervention to reduce CLABSI is removing central lines when they are no longer needed. Daily review of line necessity is mandated in most bundle protocols. "The line is still in just in case" is not a clinical indication. For every additional day a central line is in place, the CLABSI risk accumulates. Cumulative CLABSI risk increases progressively with line dwell time; prompt removal when no longer indicated is both safe and protective.

How to Remove a CVC Safely

  • Patient supine or Trendelenburg (increases venous pressure → reduces air embolism risk)
  • Have patient perform Valsalva (or hold breath and bear down) at the moment of removal → increases intrathoracic pressure → reduces risk of air entering the vein
  • Remove smoothly and steadily; do not jerk
  • Immediately apply occlusive dressing to the site; maintain pressure for ≥5 minutes
  • Keep patient supine for 30 minutes after removal
  • Examine catheter to ensure it is intact (no fracture)
  • For coagulopathic patients: extend compression time; consider absorbable hemostatic dressing

Air Embolism on Removal

Air embolism can occur at the time of CVC removal, not just insertion. The tract left by the catheter creates a direct path to the venous system. Air embolism during removal is prevented by: (1) Trendelenburg position, (2) Valsalva maneuver during withdrawal, and (3) immediate occlusive dressing. Deaths from air embolism after CVC removal are reported and preventable.

Prevention Bundle

CDC / IHI CLABSI Prevention Bundle: All Five Elements Required
  1. Hand hygiene before and after any catheter manipulation. Alcohol-based hand rub or soap and water. Non-negotiable.
  2. Maximal sterile barrier precautions at insertion: sterile gloves, gown, mask, cap, and large sterile drape covering the patient from head to toe. No exceptions for "quick" insertions.
  3. Chlorhexidine skin antisepsis (≥0.5% chlorhexidine with alcohol): apply with back-and-forth scrubbing motion; allow to dry completely before needle insertion (~30 seconds). Superior to povidone-iodine.
  4. Optimal catheter site selection: subclavian preferred over IJ; femoral last resort; avoid femoral in ambulatory or ICU patients whenever possible.
  5. Daily review of line necessity and prompt removal when no longer needed. Document reason for continued need each day.

Additional Prevention Strategies

  • Chlorhexidine-impregnated dressings (Tegaderm CHG): reduce CLABSI rates in high-risk patients; change every 7 days or when loose/soiled
  • Antimicrobial-impregnated catheters (chlorhexidine/silver sulfadiazine or minocycline/rifampin): recommended when CLABSI rates remain high despite bundle compliance; not a substitute for bundle
  • Antiseptic caps (SwabCap, disinfecting cap on hub): reduce intraluminal contamination; should be changed with every access
  • Needleless connectors: proper use and regular change (every 96 hours or with set change); neutral or negative-pressure connectors preferred over positive-pressure
  • Avoid routine line changes over a guidewire: does not reduce CLABSI and increases mechanical complications; change site if infection suspected, not same site over guidewire
  • Chlorhexidine bathing for ICU patients: daily bathing with 2% chlorhexidine cloths reduces CLABSI and other HAIs

PICC-Specific Considerations

PICC: When Is It Appropriate?

PICCs are frequently over-ordered and under-scrutinized. The Michigan Appropriateness Guide for Intravenous Catheters (MAGIC) criteria provide evidence-based guidance:

MAGIC Criteria: Appropriate PICC Indications
  • IV antibiotic therapy ≥6 days
  • IV therapy requiring central access (vesicants, TPN, hyperosmolar solutions)
  • Difficult peripheral venous access with anticipated IV therapy ≥5 days
  • Prolonged hospitalization expected with multiple IV medication needs

Inappropriate: Single antibiotic course <6 days manageable peripherally; patient with adequate peripheral access; convenience alone; anticipated hospital stay <5 days with peripheral access available. Using a PICC to avoid the inconvenience of peripheral IV changes is not a clinical indication and exposes the patient to DVT and CLABSI risk unnecessarily.

PICC and Renal Patients: A Critical Consideration

Never Place a PICC in a Patient with CKD Stage 3–5 or ESRD Without Nephrology Input

PICCs cause venous stenosis and thrombosis of the basilic, brachial, subclavian, and axillary veins. These are the vessels required for future arteriovenous fistula creation. A single PICC can permanently destroy the arm for dialysis access. In any patient with CKD Stage 3 or worse, or any patient at risk of progression to ESRD, a PICC should only be placed after nephrology consultation confirms no other option exists. Prefer peripheral IVs, midlines (if central access is not required), or a femoral CVC (for short-term use). The ipsilateral arm to a functioning AV fistula or graft is absolutely contraindicated for any IV access.

PICC Dressing and Maintenance

  • Transparent semipermeable dressing: change every 7 days and when loose/wet/soiled
  • Gauze dressing: change every 48 hours (or when loose/soiled)
  • Chlorhexidine-impregnated dressing preferred in high-risk patients
  • Securement device (StatLock or similar): reduces catheter migration and dislodgement; change with dressing
  • Flush with 10 mL NS before and after each use; flush with heparin (10 units/mL, 3 mL) when not in use per institutional protocol
  • Document arm circumference at insertion and weekly to monitor for UEDVT

Dialysis Catheters

Hemodialysis catheters are central venous catheters with large lumens (11–14 Fr) designed to accommodate high flow rates (300–450 mL/min). They carry the highest CLABSI rate of all CVC types, due to frequent access (every dialysis session), high-risk patient population (ESRD, immunocompromised, diabetic), and biofilm formation.

Site Preferences for HD Catheters

  • Right IJ (preferred): Most direct path to SVC; lowest thrombosis rate; avoids subclavian stenosis
  • Left IJ: Acceptable; longer path, more catheter manipulation
  • Femoral: Acceptable for temporary, non-ambulatory patients; high infection and thrombosis rate; limits ambulation
  • Subclavian: Avoid in ESRD. Subclavian catheterization causes subclavian vein stenosis in 40–50% of cases, permanently destroying the ipsilateral arm for AV fistula creation

HD Catheter Infections

Exit site infections (erythema, tenderness, purulence at skin insertion) can often be treated with antibiotics without line removal if no bacteremia. Tunnel infections (erythema or tenderness extending >2 cm from exit site along tunnel track) and bacteremia require catheter removal. S. aureus bacteremia from an HD catheter always requires catheter removal, echocardiography, and minimum 14–28 days of antibiotics depending on complications.

Antibiotic Lock Therapy in HD Catheters

For uncomplicated CoNS or gram-negative bacteremia in a tunneled HD catheter where removal is not immediately feasible, antibiotic lock therapy (instilling concentrated antibiotic into each lumen between HD sessions) combined with systemic antibiotics can achieve catheter salvage in ~70% of cases. This is not appropriate for S. aureus, Candida, or tunnel infections. Requires ID guidance and close follow-up with repeated blood cultures.

Clinical Pearls

The most effective CLABSI prevention is removing the line. Every morning, ask one question about every central line: "Does this patient still need this?" If the answer is no or uncertain, remove it. Daily line necessity review is a bundle element, a Joint Commission requirement, and the single intervention most consistently associated with CLABSI reduction. Lines left in "just in case" are lines causing harm.
S. aureus bacteremia from a central line is never uncomplicated. It always requires: (1) line removal, (2) echocardiography (TTE minimum; TEE if TTE non-diagnostic or concern for endocarditis), (3) minimum 14 days IV antibiotics from the first negative blood culture, (4) repeat blood cultures 48–72 hours after starting antibiotics to confirm clearance, and (5) workup for metastatic seeding (spine MRI if back pain, ophthalmology if eye symptoms). A 7-day course of antibiotics for "uncomplicated" S. aureus CLABSI is inadequate and associated with relapse and metastatic infection.
Never place a PICC in a CKD patient without nephrology input. The axillary, subclavian, and brachial veins are the patient's future AV fistula. One PICC can close that door permanently. For a patient who will need dialysis in 2 years, a PICC placed today for a 2-week antibiotic course may be the reason they never get a functional fistula and are dialyzed through a tunneled catheter indefinitely — the worst access outcome.
A midline is not a PICC. Do not infuse vesicants, vasopressors, TPN, hypertonic saline, or potassium >60 mEq/L through a midline. The tip is in the axillary or subclavian vein, not the central circulation. Extravasation of these agents from a peripheral-to-subclavian vein causes severe phlebitis and tissue necrosis. Verify tip position before any infusion requiring central access.
Valsalva on line removal prevents air embolism. The track left by a central catheter is a direct conduit for air to enter the central venous system. Trendelenburg position plus Valsalva at the moment of withdrawal increases intrathoracic pressure and venous pressure, preventing air entrainment. Apply occlusive dressing immediately. Keep the patient supine for 30 minutes. Document the catheter is intact. This is a procedure with real mortality risk if done carelessly.
Candida bloodstream infection always requires line removal and fundoscopic exam. Candida disseminates hematogenously to the retina, heart valves, kidneys, bone, and meninges. Endophthalmitis has been reported in up to 5–15% of candidemia patients in published series and causes blindness if missed. Every patient with candidemia needs an ophthalmology consultation within 1 week of diagnosis. Echinocandin (anidulafungin, micafungin, caspofungin) is first-line; de-escalate to fluconazole if susceptible and clinically stable after 5–7 days. Uncomplicated candidemia requires minimum 14 days from first negative blood culture; deep-seated infection requires longer.
