Case Presentation

Patient: E.S. — 74-Year-Old Male with Down Syndrome

  • Nonverbal, wheelchair-bound, residing in state-run group home
  • Normally feeds himself slowly on mechanically altered diet (¼-inch ground meat), fluid-restricted
  • Sister serves as healthcare proxy; she accompanies him to appointments
  • July 11: Appeared congested, coughing (noted by sister during vascular appointment)
  • Morning of admission: Yelling, appearing altered, SpO₂ 80s on baseline 1.5L NC
  • BP at group home: 119/40 → EMS transport

Reduced Oral Intake × 1 Month

This is a critical "red flag" that may signal terminal decline: reduced PO intake, increased assistance with feeding, progressive aspiration risk. In a patient with this degree of comorbidity, this trajectory often represents the beginning of the dying process, not a reversible acute illness.

Medical Complexity

Problem List

  • Down syndrome + intellectual disability (nonverbal)
  • End-stage renal disease — hemodialysis MWF (left bicep fistula)
  • Chronic hypoxia: baseline 1.5L NC

  • Atrial flutter on apixaban
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Microscopic polyangiitis (MPO-ANCA positive)
  • CPPD (calcium pyrophosphate deposition disease)
  • Drug-induced lupus (ANA 1:320, histone Ab, positive P-ANCA)

  • GERD

Assessment: Acute-on-Chronic Respiratory Failure

Presentation most consistent with aspiration pneumonia in the context of dysphagia (no recent speech therapy evaluation) + chronic aspiration risk (ID, mechanically altered diet, reduced alertness). Possible contribution from volume overload (elevated BNP, though chronically elevated). Worsening O₂ requirements (1.5L → 3L NC).

Aspiration Pneumonia in High-Risk Patients

Risk Factors in This Patient

  • Intellectual disability / nonverbal status → impaired cough reflex, reduced aspiration awareness
  • GERD — acid exposure to airway
  • Mechanically altered diet — risk of microaspiration despite texture modification
  • No recent speech therapy evaluation — swallowing function unknown
  • Hemodialysis sessions → volume shifts, increased pulmonary congestion risk
  • Prone to respiratory infections (Down syndrome: anatomic airways differences, immune dysregulation)

Management of Aspiration Pneumonia

  • Speech therapy evaluation (bedside swallow study; may need videofluoroscopic swallowing study)
  • Antibiotics: aspiration pneumonia covers oropharyngeal flora — Piperacillin-tazobactam or Ampicillin-sulbactam (if not resistant). Duration 5–7 days.
  • Consider PEG tube if aspiration risk severe and consistent with goals of care — but in this case, goals-of-care discussion must precede any invasive intervention
  • Elevate HOB 30–45°
  • Optimize fluid balance via dialysis

Capacity, Surrogate Decision-Making & NYS Law

Medical Decision-Making Capacity vs. Legal Competence

Capacity is a clinical assessment; competence is a legal determination by a court. Capacity is decision-specific and can fluctuate. A patient with intellectual disability does not automatically lack capacity — assessment should be individualized.

For a nonverbal patient with severe intellectual disability: capacity for complex medical decisions is typically absent, requiring a legal healthcare proxy or surrogate decision-maker.

New York State: Healthcare Proxy Law (Article 29-C)

  • Patient may designate a healthcare agent via a Healthcare Proxy form while competent
  • If no proxy designated: Family Health Care Decisions Act (FHCDA, 2010) provides a priority hierarchy for surrogate decision-makers: spouse/domestic partner → adult child → parent → sibling → close friend
  • In this case: sister is the healthcare proxy and decision-maker
  • Surrogate must apply the substituted judgment standard (what the patient would want if they could decide) — if unknown, uses best interests standard

Special NYS Requirements for Individuals with Developmental Disabilities

FHCDA Special Provisions for Developmental Disability (DD)
  • For individuals with DD lacking capacity who reside in licensed facilities: additional oversight for decisions to withhold/withdraw life-sustaining treatment
  • May require second physician confirmation of diagnosis and prognosis
  • Some decisions require ethics consultation or Surrogate Decision Making Committee (SDMC)
  • The goal is to protect a vulnerable population from inadequate care while still respecting their dignity and wishes through their proxy

MOLST in New York State

Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (MOLST)

The MOLST (DOH-5003) is a physician medical order (not an advance directive) that immediately translates patient/surrogate wishes into actionable medical orders across all care settings.

  • Required for anyone with a serious health condition who wants to limit life-sustaining treatment across care settings
  • Travels with the patient (home, group home, hospital, nursing facility, ambulance)
  • Addresses: CPR, mechanical ventilation, artificial nutrition, hospitalization, dialysis, and antibiotics
  • For individuals with DD lacking capacity: NYS law requires heightened process — may require court authorization or SDMC review for certain MOLST decisions (e.g., DNR, withdrawal of dialysis)
  • Must be reviewed and updated at each care transition

Weekend Palliative Consult Delay

The admitting note identifies goals-of-care/palliative needs as a priority but notes: "it is the weekend — I will ask my colleague to reach them on Monday." This highlights a systemic gap: palliative care consultation should be available 7 days a week for complex patients approaching end of life. A 2-day delay in this clinical context could mean the patient deteriorates before the discussion can occur.

Palliative Care Principles in This Case

When to Involve Palliative Care

  • Complex symptom burden (dialysis-related distress, sacral ulcer pain)
  • Anticipating end of life (functional decline, multiple organ failure, ESRD + HF + chronic respiratory failure)
  • Goals-of-care conversations needed with surrogate
  • MOLST discussion required (nonverbal, lacks decision-making capacity)
  • Concurrent management of acute illness with comfort measures

Key Goals-of-Care Discussion Points (Sister as Proxy)

  • What did the patient value when he could express preferences? What brought him joy?
  • Is the goal curative, life-prolonging, or comfort-focused?
  • Understanding of prognosis — ESRD + diastolic HF + chronic respiratory failure + autoimmune disease in a 74-year-old with DS: median survival measured in months to 1–2 years without a major acute trigger
  • Would he want intubation if needed? Dialysis to continue?
  • Pain management during dialysis — nephrology noted he frequently cries during treatments; this warrants aggressive symptom management regardless of code status

Symptom Management (Regardless of Goals)

  • Dyspnea: low-dose opioids (morphine 2–4 mg IV/SC q4h PRN) — most effective symptom-directed treatment
  • Anxiety: lorazepam or midazolam; gabapentin for neuropathic pain
  • Sacral ulcer: wound care, pressure redistribution, topical analgesics
  • Dialysis distress: pre-session anxiolytics, comfort positioning, music therapy

Dialysis Withdrawal Considerations

Withdrawal of dialysis is one of the most common decisions in nephrology palliative care. After stopping dialysis in ESRD, death typically occurs within 8–12 days (range 1–23 days) depending on residual renal function. This is a legitimate, legally and ethically supported choice by a patient or their surrogate.

RPA/ASN Guidelines on Withdrawal of Dialysis

  • Withholding or withdrawing dialysis is ethically equivalent to withdrawing any other medical intervention
  • Shared decision-making is required; surrogate decision-making is valid when patient lacks capacity
  • Palliative care involvement is strongly recommended to manage uremic symptoms at end of life
  • Uremic pruritus, pain, dyspnea, and agitation — symptom management after dialysis withdrawal is essential
  • Hospice enrollment is appropriate after dialysis withdrawal decision is made

Clinical Pearls

⚖️Palliative care is NOT synonymous with "giving up" or comfort-only care. It is appropriate at any stage of illness alongside disease-directed treatment. Early integration of palliative care reduces symptom burden, improves quality of life, and may paradoxically increase survival in certain patient populations (Temel et al., NEJM 2010).
🧠Intellectual disability (ID) does not equal lack of decision-making capacity. Capacity is decision-specific. Even with ID, many individuals can participate meaningfully in some health decisions. However, in severe/profound ID with nonverbal status (as in this case), formal capacity assessment confirms the need for surrogate decision-making.
💡In New York State, the MOLST is a physician-signed medical order that is immediately actionable — EMS personnel must comply with a valid MOLST instructing DNR, even in the field. A MOLST is more powerful than a Healthcare Proxy in an emergency because it does not require physician interpretation.
🩺Down syndrome: individuals with DS have a median life expectancy of ~60 years; many now live into their 70s. They have high rates of Alzheimer's disease (virtually universal by age 60), hypothyroidism, atlantoaxial instability, congenital heart disease, and autoimmune disease. The overlap of DS + ESRD + HF + microscopic polyangiitis in a 74-year-old represents extraordinary medical complexity.
❤️Crying during dialysis in a nonverbal patient is a form of pain communication. The moral imperative to assess and treat pain in non-communicative patients is equal to (or greater than) the obligation in communicative patients. Validated pain scales for patients who cannot self-report (FLACC, Abbey Pain Scale) should be routinely used.
