Acute Kidney Injury

AKI complicates approximately 10–15% of hospitalizations and up to 50% of ICU admissions, though reported rates vary considerably by cohort, AKI definition, and clinical setting. It is associated with increased mortality, progression to CKD, cardiovascular events, and prolonged hospitalization. The framework of pre-renal, intrinsic, and post-renal etiologies guides diagnosis; urinalysis with urine microscopy — whether performed by the clinician or the laboratory — is the highest-yield initial diagnostic test. Many cases of AKI are preventable or mitigable with nephrotoxin avoidance and early recognition. All of it demands a cause.

Definition & KDIGO Staging

KDIGO 2012 Definition of AKI (Any One of the Following)
  • Increase in serum creatinine ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours
  • Increase in serum creatinine to ≥1.5× baseline within 7 days
  • Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr for ≥6 hours

Baseline creatinine: lowest creatinine in the preceding 3 months. If unavailable, use the admission creatinine or back-calculate using the MDRD equation assuming eGFR of 75 mL/min/1.73m².

Creatinine Is a Late and Imperfect Marker

Serum creatinine does not rise until 50–70% of GFR is lost. A creatinine of 1.5 mg/dL in an elderly, sarcopenic woman may represent severe AKI (her baseline may be 0.6 mg/dL). Conversely, a creatinine of 2.0 mg/dL in a muscular young man may be his baseline. Always compare to prior values. Urine output is an earlier, more sensitive indicator of AKI — oliguria often precedes creatinine rise by hours.

Classification: Pre-renal, Intrinsic, Post-renal

The classic three-compartment framework remains the backbone of AKI diagnosis. Most AKI is pre-renal; most preventable AKI is also pre-renal. The goal of initial evaluation is to rapidly classify the AKI and identify reversible causes.

Pre-renal AKI

Pre-renal AKI results from decreased effective arterial blood volume reaching the kidneys. The kidney parenchyma is intact; the dysfunction is functional and reversible with restoration of perfusion.

Causes

True Volume Depletion
  • Hemorrhage (GI bleed, trauma, surgical)
  • GI losses (vomiting, diarrhea, ileostomy output)
  • Renal losses (diuretics, osmotic diuresis in DKA/HHS)
  • Third-spacing (pancreatitis, burns, bowel obstruction)
  • Poor oral intake, insensible losses (fever, sweating)
Reduced Effective Arterial Volume (RAAS-Mediated)
  • Heart failure (low cardiac output state)
  • Cirrhosis (splanchnic vasodilation → reduced effective volume; HRS)
  • Nephrotic syndrome (hypoalbuminemia → reduced oncotic pressure)
  • Sepsis (systemic vasodilation)

Pre-renal vs. Intrinsic: The Critical Distinction

If pre-renal AKI is not identified and treated promptly, prolonged renal hypoperfusion causes ischemic acute tubular necrosis (ATN) — intrinsic AKI. The distinction matters because pre-renal responds to fluid and the underlying cause; ATN does not respond to fluids and requires time to recover.

Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS): A Special Pre-renal Entity

HRS is a functional pre-renal AKI in cirrhosis driven by extreme splanchnic vasodilation → compensatory renal vasoconstriction → severe reduction in GFR. It is a diagnosis of exclusion: other causes of AKI (volume depletion, nephrotoxins, infection, intrinsic kidney disease) must first be addressed. The EASL/AASLD diagnostic framework requires: AKI in cirrhosis, no improvement after 2 days of diuretic withdrawal AND volume challenge with albumin 1 g/kg/day (max 100 g/day), absence of shock, no current or recent nephrotoxic drug exposure, and no structural kidney disease (normal urinalysis and imaging). HRS-AKI (formerly Type 1 HRS) is rapidly progressive with median survival of weeks without treatment. Treatment: albumin + vasoconstrictors (terlipressin where available, or norepinephrine + octreotide + midodrine). Definitive treatment is liver transplantation. Covered in depth in Case 22 (Autoimmune Hepatitis).

AIN: A Frequently Missed and Treatable Diagnosis

Drug-induced AIN is far more common than classically taught. The triad of fever, rash, and eosinophilia occurs in fewer than 10% of AIN cases. The most common presentation is simply a rising creatinine in a patient who started a new medication. PPIs, NSAIDs, beta-lactams, fluoroquinolones, and sulfonamides are among the most frequently implicated agents — with PPIs increasingly recognized in recent case series, though patterns vary by institution and patient population. Urine WBC casts support the diagnosis; eosinophiluria is insensitive and non-specific (Hansel stain required). Kidney biopsy is the gold standard. Treatment: remove offending agent; steroids if no improvement in 1–2 weeks (benefit most established for drug-induced AIN).

Post-renal AKI

Obstruction of urinary outflow increases back-pressure throughout the collecting system, transmitted retrograde to Bowman's space, reducing GFR. For bilateral AKI to develop from obstruction, either both ureters must be obstructed, or there is a solitary functioning kidney.

Causes by Level of Obstruction

Check a Post-Void Residual Early in Any AKI With Oliguria

Bladder outlet obstruction is the most common post-renal cause of AKI and takes 30 seconds to screen for with a bedside bladder scan or in-and-out catheterization. A post-void residual >300 mL is abnormal and strongly supports urinary retention; 150–300 mL is borderline and requires clinical correlation. Elevated PVR alone is not a diagnosis — it must be interpreted alongside symptoms (weak stream, nocturia, overflow incontinence), physical exam (distended bladder, enlarged prostate), and the overall clinical picture. In any male with AKI and a history of BPH or weak stream, screen PVR before ordering a renal ultrasound. If obstructed: place a Foley catheter. AKI from bladder outlet obstruction often reverses substantially within 24–72 hours of catheter placement.

Diagnostic Approach

History: The Most Important Diagnostic Tool

  • Volume assessment: Fluid intake vs. output; recent GI losses (vomiting, diarrhea); blood loss; diuretic use; heart failure or cirrhosis history
  • Medication review: NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors/ARBs, aminoglycosides, vancomycin, contrast, IV contrast timing, diuretics, PPIs, beta-lactams, chemotherapy
  • Urinary symptoms: Decreased urine output, hematuria, dysuria, weak stream, nocturia, urinary retention
  • Systemic symptoms: Rash, fever, arthralgia (AIN, vasculitis, GN); hemoptysis (Goodpasture's, ANCA vasculitis); skin findings (cholesterol emboli — livedo reticularis, blue toes)
  • Recent procedures: Cardiac catheterization (contrast nephropathy, cholesterol emboli); surgery (ischemic ATN, medication exposure); IV contrast timing
  • Baseline kidney function: Prior creatinine values; known CKD; history of prior AKI

Urinalysis & Urine Microscopy

Urine Microscopy Is the Highest-Yield Test in AKI Evaluation

Urine microscopy — whether performed manually by the clinician or by the laboratory — is the single most informative test in AKI evaluation. It takes minutes, is low-cost, and directly identifies the pathological process. In centers where clinician-performed microscopy is feasible, reviewing a fresh spin yourself adds immediacy and avoids processing delays. Every patient with unexplained or non-obvious AKI should have a fresh urine specimen examined under microscopy. The finding of specific casts localizes the injury to a specific compartment with high specificity.

Urine Indices: FENa, FEUrea, BUN:Cr

Fractional Excretion of Sodium (FENa)

Formula: FENa = (UNa × PCr) / (PNa × UCr) × 100

FENaInterpretationCaveat
<1%Suggests pre-renal (avid Na retention)Also seen in early GN, contrast nephropathy, myoglobinuria, HRS, early obstruction, and sepsis-associated AKI; not reliably discriminating across all clinical contexts
>2%Suggests intrinsic renal (ATN; tubular Na wasting)Falsely elevated with diuretics; also elevated in baseline CKD due to reduced tubular reabsorption capacity

Key limitations: FENa is unreliable in patients who have received diuretics, those with CKD (impaired baseline tubular Na handling), sepsis, contrast-associated AKI, and mixed etiologies. It is most useful in straightforward volume depletion vs. established ATN — not as a universal discriminator. Always integrate with clinical assessment.

Fractional Excretion of Urea (FEUrea) — Use When Diuretics Have Been Given

Formula: FEUrea = (Uurea × PCr) / (Purea × UCr) × 100

FEUreaInterpretation

Urea reabsorption is not affected by loop diuretics, making FEUrea more reliable than FENa in patients on diuretics. However, FEUrea is less well-validated and may be less reliable in protein malnutrition or urea cycle disorders.

Imaging

Renal Ultrasound: The First-Line Imaging Study in AKI

  • Hydronephrosis: Dilation of the renal pelvis and calyces — suggests obstruction (post-renal AKI). Graded mild/moderate/severe. Note: in early or acute obstruction, hydronephrosis may not yet be present (20–30% of obstructive AKI cases will have normal US initially).
  • Kidney size: Small, echogenic kidneys suggest underlying CKD (irreversible atrophy and fibrosis). Normal or enlarged kidneys in AKI suggest acute process.
  • Resistive index (RI): Doppler-derived; RI >0.7 suggests elevated intrarenal vascular resistance; not routinely used but may help distinguish ATN from pre-renal in some centers.
  • Nephrolithiasis: Echogenic foci with shadowing; identifies stones causing obstruction.

General Management

Treat the Underlying Cause

No AKI should be managed without identifying and addressing the precipitant. Fluids for volume depletion, antibiotics for pyelonephritis, Foley for obstruction, removal of offending nephrotoxin, steroids for AIN. General supportive measures:

PriorityInterventionDetail
1Optimize volume statusCorrect hypovolemia with IV fluids (balanced crystalloids preferred); avoid fluid overload (associated with worse outcomes); reassess volume status regularly
2Remove nephrotoxinsStop NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, vancomycin (or switch to AUC-guided monitoring), IV contrast (avoid), nephrotoxic chemotherapy; review all medications daily
3Monitor urine outputFoley catheter in moderate-severe AKI; hourly urine output; target >0.5 mL/kg/hr
4Electrolyte managementMonitor K, HCO3, phosphate, and calcium daily; treat hyperkalemia aggressively; sodium bicarbonate for metabolic acidosis if pH <7.20 or HCO3 <15
5Medication dosingDose-adjust all renally cleared medications per current eGFR; avoid iodinated contrast and gadolinium; check renal dosing daily as GFR changes
6Nutritional supportDo not restrict protein intake in AKI patients not on dialysis; malnutrition worsens outcomes; target 1.0–1.5 g/kg/day protein; consult dietitian
7Blood pressure managementTarget MAP ≥65 mmHg (higher in chronic hypertension); avoid hypotension; ACE inhibitors/ARBs may need to be held in acute setting

ACE Inhibitors / ARBs in AKI

When to Hold ACE Inhibitors and ARBs

ACE inhibitors and ARBs reduce efferent arteriolar tone, which is the kidney's last defense of GFR in hypoperfused states. In volume-depleted patients, heart failure, bilateral renal artery stenosis, or any hemodynamically unstable AKI, ACEi/ARBs should be held. They can be cautiously restarted once AKI has resolved and volume status is optimized — they have long-term renoprotective effects in CKD and diabetic nephropathy. The creatinine may rise 10–20% when restarted, which is acceptable; a rise >30–35% should prompt investigation for renal artery stenosis.

Renal Replacement Therapy

Indications for Emergent RRT (AEIOU)

AEIOU Mnemonic: Emergent Dialysis Indications
  • A — Acidosis: Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1–7.2) refractory to medical management
  • E — Electrolytes: Severe hyperkalemia (K >6.5 or with EKG changes) refractory to medical treatment
  • I — Intoxication: Dialyzable toxins (methanol, ethylene glycol, lithium, salicylates, metformin in lactic acidosis)
  • O — Overload: Volume overload with pulmonary edema refractory to diuretics
  • U — Uremia: Uremic encephalopathy, uremic pericarditis, uremic bleeding (platelet dysfunction)

Timing of RRT: Early vs. Late

The AKIKI and IDEAL-ICU trials found no benefit of early (Stage 2 AKI) vs. delayed (Stage 3 or development of AEIOU indications) RRT initiation in critically ill patients. Many patients avoid RRT entirely if given time to recover with supportive care. Initiate RRT when AEIOU criteria are met or when AKI is progressing without sign of recovery. Do not initiate RRT preventively in stable patients.

Uremic Emergencies

  • Uremic pericarditis: Fibrinous pericarditis from uremic toxins; chest pain; pericardial rub; risk of tamponade; indication for urgent dialysis (not anticoagulants)
  • Uremic encephalopathy: Confusion, asterixis, myoclonus, seizures; responds to dialysis; distinguish from other causes of AMS
  • Uremic bleeding: Platelet dysfunction (impaired aggregation); prolonged bleeding time; treat with DDAVP 0.3 mcg/kg IV; cryoprecipitate; conjugated estrogens for longer-term effect; dialysis

Recovery & Long-term Outcomes

AKI-to-CKD Transition

AKI is not a self-limited event. It is now recognized as a major risk factor for:

  • CKD development: 25–30% of patients with AKI develop new or worsening CKD; risk is proportional to AKI severity and duration
  • Recurrent AKI: Each episode of AKI increases risk of subsequent episodes (sensitization)
  • ESRD: ~8–10% of patients with severe AKI progress to ESRD within 2–3 years
  • Cardiovascular events: AKI independently increases risk of MI, heart failure, stroke, and cardiovascular death
  • Mortality: Even mild AKI is associated with increased 30-day and 1-year mortality after adjustment for comorbidities

Discharge Planning After AKI

Every patient discharged after AKI needs: (1) a follow-up creatinine check within 7–14 days, (2) nephrology referral if AKI was severe (Stage 2–3) or baseline CKD is present, (3) education about nephrotoxin avoidance (NSAIDs especially), (4) BP management, and (5) medication reconciliation with dose adjustments documented. "AKI, resolved" on a discharge summary without a follow-up plan is incomplete care.

Special Entities

Contrast-Induced AKI (CI-AKI)

Modern Evidence Has Revised the Risk Calculus

Older teaching significantly overstated the nephrotoxicity of IV iodinated contrast. Contemporary evidence, including multiple large retrospective cohort studies and meta-analyses (McDonald et al., Davenport et al.), suggests that the incidence of CI-AKI with modern low- and iso-osmolar contrast agents is substantially lower than previously reported — and may not be significantly higher than AKI occurring in similar patients who do not receive contrast. Much of the historical signal reflected confounding by indication (sicker patients get contrast). This does not mean contrast is risk-free, but it does mean that withholding clinically indicated contrast studies to "protect the kidneys" in most patients with mild-moderate CKD is not supported by current evidence and may cause harm by delaying diagnosis.

  • CI-AKI definition: rise in creatinine ≥0.3 mg/dL or ≥50% from baseline within 48–72 hours of IV contrast exposure
  • Genuinely high-risk patients (where contrast is still a real concern): eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m², particularly with concurrent volume depletion, CHF, or diabetes with advanced CKD; intra-arterial contrast (higher renal concentration than IV)
  • In patients with eGFR 30–60: risk is low with modern agents; individualize decision; benefit of the contrast study usually outweighs risk
  • Prevention in genuinely high-risk patients: IV isotonic saline hydration before and after the procedure; use the lowest effective contrast volume; prefer low- or iso-osmolar agents; avoid concomitant nephrotoxins
  • Metformin: Current ACR guidance recommends holding metformin at the time of contrast administration in patients with eGFR <30, or if eGFR is 30–60 and the patient is having intra-arterial contrast or has other AKI risk factors; restart 48 hours after if renal function stable. Routine 48-hour hold in all CKD patients is no longer universally recommended — risk of lactic acidosis is from the AKI itself, not the contrast.
  • N-acetylcysteine: multiple large RCTs (PRESERVE trial) have not shown benefit; not recommended as standard prevention

Rhabdomyolysis-Induced AKI

  • CPK >5,000–10,000 U/L is associated with AKI risk; CPK >15,000–20,000 substantially increases risk
  • Causes: trauma, extreme exertion, seizures, myositis, statins, alcohol, cocaine, antipsychotics (NMS), hypothermia, prolonged immobility (found down)
  • Presentation: muscle pain and weakness; dark cola-colored urine; elevated CPK; dipstick heme-positive urine with few RBCs on microscopy (myoglobinuria)
  • Management: aggressive IV fluid resuscitation (goal UO >200–300 mL/hr until urine clears); isotonic saline; monitor K, Ca, PO4 closely (hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia all occur)
  • Calcium: do not supplement calcium unless symptomatic hypocalcemia — calcium deposits in injured muscle and rebounds as muscle heals (“calcium paradox”)

Myeloma Cast Nephropathy

  • AKI from precipitation of monoclonal light chains (Bence-Jones proteins) in renal tubules; casts obstruct tubular flow
  • Suspect in elderly patient with AKI and elevated total protein but low albumin (suggesting M-spike); check serum protein electrophoresis (SPEP), urine electrophoresis (UPEP), serum free light chains
  • Treatment: aggressive hydration; avoid nephrotoxins; urgent chemotherapy to reduce light chain production; plasma exchange (controversial); avoid IV contrast

Clinical Pearls

Urine microscopy is the highest-yield test in AKI evaluation. Muddy brown casts = ATN. RBC casts = highly suggestive of glomerulonephritis. WBC casts = AIN or pyelonephritis. Bland sediment = pre-renal or post-renal. Every patient with unexplained AKI should have a fresh urine specimen examined under microscopy — either by the clinician directly or sent fresh to the laboratory. The key is that the specimen is fresh and the result is reviewed, not just auto-reported. Sending urine to the lab without reviewing the microscopy findings is an incomplete evaluation.
Screen for urinary retention early in any AKI with oliguria. Bladder outlet obstruction is the most common post-renal cause of AKI and takes 30 seconds to screen for with a bedside bladder scan or in-and-out catheterization. A post-void residual >300 mL in the right clinical context strongly supports urinary retention, but PVR must be interpreted alongside symptoms (weak stream, nocturia) and exam findings. In any elderly male with AKI, a history of BPH, or any patient on anticholinergics, assess PVR before ordering a renal ultrasound. If obstructed: place a Foley catheter. AKI from bladder outlet obstruction often reverses substantially within 24–72 hours.
FENa is unreliable in patients on diuretics. Diuretics cause natriuresis regardless of volume status, making FENa falsely high even in pre-renal AKI. Use FEUrea (<35% = pre-renal) when diuretics have been given within the past 12–24 hours. Clinical context — skin turgor, mucous membranes, orthostatics, fluid balance — is more reliable than any urine index in isolation.
NSAIDs in cirrhosis or CHF is a prescription for AKI. In states where RAAS is maximally activated (cirrhosis, CHF, volume depletion, CKD), prostaglandins are the only remaining defense of renal afferent arteriolar perfusion. A single dose of ketorolac, ibuprofen, or naproxen eliminates this defense and can precipitate AKI or HRS within hours. Avoid all NSAIDs in these patients and substitute acetaminophen at appropriate doses.
AKI is not over when the creatinine normalizes. Recovery of creatinine to baseline does not mean the kidney has fully recovered or that risk has returned to baseline. Patients who sustain AKI have significantly increased risk of CKD progression, recurrent AKI, cardiovascular events, and death for years afterward. Every AKI discharge should include follow-up creatinine within 7–14 days and nephrology referral for moderate-severe episodes.
Calcium gluconate first, always, in hyperkalemia with EKG changes. The sequence in severe hyperkalemia is: (1) calcium gluconate to stabilize the membrane, (2) insulin + dextrose to shift K into cells, (3) albuterol as adjunct, (4) remove K from the body (furosemide if urine output, binders, or dialysis). Calcium gluconate does not lower the potassium — it buys time. If the EKG shows peaked T-waves, wide QRS, or sine waves, calcium is the first drug every time.
