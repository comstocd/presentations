Necrotizing Fasciitis

An EMT presents with what appears to be a recurrent cyst at an ingrown hair site — within 24 hours she has spreading cellulitis, cannot walk, and is in septic shock with AKI. Time to debridement is the only thing that saves her life.

Case Narrative

Opening Statement (Patient's Own Words)

"The story started with an ingrown hair... I thought 'oh great, I didn't clean it well enough and it's coming back...' but it actually felt a little different."

Day 1

Ingrown hair site ruptures → brief relief. EMT felt "run down" at end of shift → goes home. Fever develops. Hot showers for joint aches. Notices bright red skin at cyst site.

Day 2–3

Within 24 hours: Swelling, erythema extending across lower abdomen to right hip. Cannot walk 20 ft (bathroom to bed). "Doubled over in pain." Calls husband to come home from work.

Urgent Care Visit

MD unable to examine abdomen due to pain intensity. IV placed; morphine given × 3 doses without adequate relief. Physician states: "I've given the max I can here." Transfers to hospital by personal car (patient refuses ambulance — her coworkers are the EMS crew).

Emergency Department

Dilaudid administered. CT abdomen performed. Started on 3 antibiotics. Cultures taken from cyst site. Admitted for sepsis. Also developing AKI ("starting to go into kidney failure").

What makes this case harrowing?

Pain out of proportion to skin findings is the cardinal sign of NF. This patient received max-dose opioids at urgent care and remained in agony — not because of inadequate analgesia, but because fascial plane destruction and ischemia produce a pain level that no opioid dose can fully address. Recognizing this pattern should trigger immediate surgical consultation.

What is Necrotizing Fasciitis?

Necrotizing fasciitis (NF) is a rapidly progressive, life-threatening deep soft tissue infection affecting the fascial planes and subcutaneous fat. It is characterized by:

  • Infection spreads along fascial planes (relatively avascular → poor antibiotic penetration)
  • Muscle typically spared due to better blood supply
  • Fulminant progression: hours to systemic septic shock
  • Mortality: 20–40% even with optimal management; approaches 100% without surgery
  • Incidence: ~0.4–1 case per 100,000 person-years — rare but devastatingly lethal

Microbiology & Classification

Type I — Polymicrobial (Most Common, ~80–90%)

  • Mixed anaerobic + aerobic organisms
  • Anaerobes: Clostridium, Bacteroides, Peptostreptococcus
  • Aerobes: Enterobacteriaceae, Staphylococcus aureus
  • Typically involves trunk, perineum (Fournier's gangrene), post-surgical wounds
  • Risk factors: diabetes, obesity, immunosuppression, cirrhosis

Type II — Monomicrobial (10–15%)

  • Group A Streptococcus (S. pyogenes) — "flesh-eating bacteria"
  • Often healthy hosts without predisposing conditions
  • May be complicated by streptococcal toxic shock syndrome (TSS)
  • Increasingly: Vibrio vulnificus (saltwater exposure, liver disease)
  • MRSA — necrotizing fasciitis with Panton-Valentine leukocidin (PVL) strains

Mechanisms of Tissue Destruction

  • Bacterial toxins (streptococcal pyrogenic exotoxins — superantigens triggering massive cytokine release)
  • Enzymatic destruction of fascial planes (hyaluronidase, proteases)
  • Gas production by anaerobes → crepitus (pathognomonic when present)
  • Vascular thrombosis → tissue ischemia → rapid spread

Score <6: Low risk (NPV 96%). Score 6–7: Intermediate. Score ≥8: High risk (PPV 93%). A high score supports early surgical exploration — but a low score does NOT exclude NF when clinical suspicion is high.

Imaging

  • CT with IV contrast: Best modality — shows gas tracking along fascial planes (pathognomonic), fascial thickening, fluid collections. Sensitivity ~90%, specificity ~95%. However: if clinical suspicion is very high, do not delay surgery for CT.
  • MRI: Most sensitive for extent of fascial involvement — rarely practical in emergency settings
  • Plain films: May show soft tissue gas — specific but insensitive (only ~50%)
  • Ultrasound: Shows thickened fascia, fluid tracking, dirty shadowing from gas — useful at bedside

⚠️ The Cardinal Signs of NF

  • Pain out of proportion to external skin findings — most important early sign
  • Rapid progression of erythema beyond initial borders
  • Tense, wooden-hard, edematous skin
  • Skin discoloration (dusky, violaceous, then necrosis)
  • Crepitus (gas in tissues) — late but pathognomonic
  • Bullae or skin necrosis — late sign, indicates extensive fascial destruction
  • Systemic toxicity (fever, hypotension, tachycardia, confusion) disproportionate to apparent wound

Management: Time-Critical Protocol

Time to Surgery is the Single Most Important Prognostic Factor

Every hour of delay in surgical debridement increases mortality. Studies show mortality doubles when surgery is delayed beyond 12–24 hours from presentation. Surgical exploration is both diagnostic and therapeutic — frozen section pathology can confirm NF intraoperatively.

Simultaneous Resuscitation

  • IV access × 2, aggressive fluid resuscitation (septic shock: 30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus; then vasopressors if MAP <65)
  • Blood cultures × 2 before antibiotics
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics immediately (do not delay for OR)
  • ICU-level care
  • Correct coagulopathy, transfuse for Hgb <7 (or <8 if hemodynamically compromised)

Surgical Management

  • Aggressive surgical debridement of all necrotic tissue — may require multiple return trips to OR (q24–48h "second-look" procedures)
  • Healthy, bleeding tissue at margins = adequate debridement
  • Do not hesitate to perform wide excision — inadequate debridement = treatment failure
  • Wound VAC/negative pressure wound therapy after debridement
  • Reconstructive surgery/skin grafting once infection controlled and wound granulating

Adjunctive: Hyperbaric Oxygen (HBO)

Controversial — not universally available; some retrospective data suggest benefit for Type I NF (anaerobic organisms suppressed by high oxygen). Not a substitute for surgery. Consider at centers with HBO capability after initial debridement.

IVIG

For Group A Streptococcal NF with toxic shock syndrome — IVIG may neutralize superantigens. Evidence is limited (case series and small RCTs); reasonable adjunct in severe GAS-TSS.

Clinical Pearls

🔪"When in doubt, cut it out." The surgical principle in NF: if clinical suspicion is high, operative exploration is safer than watchful waiting. Surgical findings (gray necrotic tissue, lack of bleeding, "dishwater" fluid tracking along fascial planes) confirm diagnosis intraoperatively.
💡Clindamycin is essential in GAS NF because of the Eagle effect: at high bacterial densities (stationary growth phase), beta-lactams are less effective. Clindamycin inhibits ribosomal protein synthesis — killing stationary-phase bacteria AND suppressing exotoxin production. Always add to GAS-directed therapy.
⚠️A normal skin exam does NOT rule out NF. The infection is in the deep fascial planes — the overlying skin may appear nearly normal in the first 12–24 hours. Extreme tenderness on palpation beyond visible erythema borders is a key early clue.
🩺The "finger test": In the OR, a small incision over the suspicious area — if tissue dissects easily without bleeding along fascial planes, NF is confirmed. This surgical diagnostic maneuver is rapid and definitive.
🧬Fournier's gangrene is a specific type of NF affecting the perineum and genitalia — polymicrobial (Type I), often in diabetic men. It carries 20–40% mortality. Urgent urology + colorectal + surgery consultation is required for adequate debridement of perirectal, scrotal, and perineal planes.
