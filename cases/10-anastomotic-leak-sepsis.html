Anastomotic Leak, Fecal Peritonitis & Septic Shock

58-year-old female post-low anterior resection (LAR) for rectal cancer — develops an anastomotic leak with fecal contamination, progressing to septic shock, ARDS, and multiorgan dysfunction requiring emergent re-operation and ICU-level care.

Case Presentation

Room 6 — What Went Wrong After LAR?

Low anterior resection (LAR) is a sphincter-preserving operation for rectal cancer. The anastomosis between colon and residual rectum is the Achilles' heel — anastomotic leaks occur in 5–25% of cases (higher with lower anastomoses, near the anal verge). When they occur: fecal spillage into the peritoneum → chemical peritonitis → bacterial peritonitis → sepsis → septic shock → MODS. This is the catastrophic trajectory of this case.

Post-operative Days 1–4

Expected post-op course after LAR. Patient had rectal cancer requiring low anterior resection. Defunctioning loop ileostomy placed to protect anastomosis (standard of care for low anastomoses).

Post-operative Day 5–6

Fever develops. Tachycardia. Increasing abdominal pain and distension. Ileus vs early bowel obstruction considered. Leukocytosis.

Deterioration

Patient becomes hemodynamically unstable. CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast: anastomotic dehiscence with extraluminal air and feculent fluid collection in the pelvis. Free perforation vs contained leak.

Septic Shock

MAP drops below 65 despite IV fluid boluses. Vasopressors initiated. Lactate 4.8 mmol/L. Emergent return to OR. Findings: fecal peritonitis, anastomotic breakdown. Hartmann's procedure (end colostomy + rectal stump closure) performed. Abdominal washout.

Post-operative ICU

ARDS develops (bilateral infiltrates, PaO₂/FiO₂ <200 on mechanical ventilation). AKI. Hepatic dysfunction. Persistent vasopressor requirement. Multiple-organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS).

Risk Factors for Anastomotic Dehiscence

Patient-Level

  • Male sex (reduced pelvic working space)
  • Obesity (BMI >30)
  • Neoadjuvant radiation therapy
  • Smoking, alcohol use
  • Malnutrition (albumin <3)
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Immunosuppression
  • ASA class III–IV
  • Emergency surgery

Technical / Intraoperative

  • Tension on anastomosis
  • Inadequate blood supply to anastomotic ends
  • Low anastomosis (<5 cm from anal verge)
  • Inadequate bowel preparation (fecal loading)
  • Intraoperative blood loss >500 mL
  • Prolonged operative time
  • Laparotomy vs laparoscopy (debated)

Classification of Anastomotic Leak

ISREC/IASLS Grading
  • Grade A: Subclinical leak on imaging; no change in management needed
  • Grade B: Leak requiring active therapeutic intervention (CT-guided drainage, antibiotics) but not re-laparotomy
  • Grade C: Leak requiring re-laparotomy — this patient

Septic Shock: Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2021

Sepsis-3 Definitions (Singer et al., JAMA 2016)
  • Infection: Suspected or confirmed pathogenic microorganism
  • Sepsis: Life-threatening organ dysfunction (SOFA score ↑≥2) due to dysregulated host response to infection
  • Septic shock: Sepsis + vasopressor requirement to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg AND serum lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation. Hospital mortality >40%.
  • qSOFA screening (≥2 of: RR≥22, altered mentation, SBP≤100) identifies high-risk patients outside ICU

Hour-1 Bundle (Surviving Sepsis 2021)

  1. Lactate: Measure lactate. Re-measure if >2 mmol/L.
  2. Blood cultures: Obtain before antibiotics (minimum 2 sets from different sites)
  3. Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics within 1 hour of recognition (for septic shock; within 3 hours for sepsis without shock)
  4. Fluid resuscitation: 30 mL/kg IV crystalloid bolus for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/L
  5. Vasopressors: Start norepinephrine if MAP <65 despite initial fluid challenge; do not wait until fluid resuscitation "complete"

Fluid Resuscitation: Balanced Crystalloids Preferred

Surviving Sepsis 2021 and SMART/SALT-ED trials: balanced crystalloids (Lactated Ringer's, Plasma-Lyte) are preferred over normal saline (0.9% NaCl) for large-volume resuscitation. NS causes hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis, which can worsen renal function and complicate acid-base interpretation. In septic shock with abdominal source, resuscitate to clinical endpoints (MAP ≥65, UO ≥0.5 mL/kg/h, lactate clearance >10%) rather than a fixed volume.

Surgical Management of Grade C Anastomotic Leak

Hartmann's Procedure (Most Common for Fecal Peritonitis)

  1. Takedown of the leaking anastomosis
  2. End colostomy (proximal colon brought to abdominal wall)
  3. Rectal stump closure (stapled or sutured; Hartmann's pouch)
  4. Thorough peritoneal lavage (≥10 L warm NS)
  5. Drain placement
  6. Delayed primary closure vs. open abdomen (VAC dressing) for severe contamination

Damage Control Surgery (DCS)

In hemodynamically unstable patients (as here): DCS principles — abbreviated initial operation (source control only), ICU resuscitation, and planned return to OR in 24–48 hours once the patient is stabilized ("damage control resuscitation").

Reversal Considerations

Hartmann's reversal (colostomy takedown and reanastomosis) can be considered 3–6 months later in suitable candidates. Reversal rates: only 40–70% of patients ultimately undergo reversal. Factors favoring non-reversal: age, comorbidities, poor functional status, patient preference, recurrent cancer.

ICU Management: Multiorgan Support

ARDS Management (ARDSNET Protocol)

  • Low tidal volume ventilation: 6 mL/kg IBW (reduces ventilator-induced lung injury; ARDSNet trial: ↓mortality 22% → 31% vs 12 mL/kg)
  • PEEP optimization: PEEP table based on FiO₂ requirement; avoid excessive PEEP driving plateau pressure >30 cmH₂O
  • Prone positioning: For moderate-severe ARDS (PaO₂/FiO₂ <150); PROSEVA trial — 16 hr/day proning ↓28-day mortality 16% vs 32.8%
  • Conservative fluid strategy after resuscitation (FACTT trial); aggressive diuresis once hemodynamics stable
  • Target SpO₂ 88–95%: permissive hypoxemia acceptable; avoid hyperoxia

AKI Support

  • Renal replacement therapy (CRRT preferred in hemodynamically unstable patients)
  • AKIKI trial: no benefit to early vs delayed RRT initiation — use clinical triggers (refractory hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis, fluid overload)
  • Nephroprotective measures: avoid contrast, adjust drug dosing, optimize MAP

Other ICU Priorities

  • Stress ulcer prophylaxis (PPI or H2RA in mechanically ventilated patients)
  • DVT prophylaxis (mechanical compression; anticoagulation when hemostasis confirmed)
  • Blood glucose control 140–180 mg/dL (NICE-SUGAR trial)
  • Nutritional support: early enteral nutrition within 24–48 hours of ICU admission if hemodynamically stable
  • SIADH/electrolyte management

Antimicrobial Therapy for Intra-Abdominal Infection

IDSA/SIS 2017: Complicated Intra-Abdominal Infection

  • Empiric (community-onset, moderate-high severity): Piperacillin-tazobactam 3.375 g IV q6h (or 4.5 g q8h extended infusion) OR Meropenem 1 g IV q8h for high-risk patients
  • Add: Vancomycin if MRSA risk or healthcare-associated infection
  • Duration: 4–7 days if adequate source control achieved (not 14 days historically taught — STOP-IT trial)
  • De-escalate based on culture results (typically: mixed enteric flora — E. coli, Bacteroides fragilis)
  • Anaerobic coverage is essential for colonic source (metronidazole or pip-tazo)

Clinical Pearls

⚠️A protective loop ileostomy does NOT prevent anastomotic leak — it prevents fecal peritonitis by diverting stool away from the anastomosis. An anastomotic leak can still occur (and is detectable on CT), but the clinical consequence is less catastrophic. Every patient undergoing LAR for rectal cancer should have a diverting stoma discussed preoperatively.
💡Lactate >4 mmol/L defines septic shock by Sepsis-3. It reflects tissue hypoperfusion and anaerobic metabolism. Serial lactate measurement (every 2 hours) provides a dynamic marker of resuscitation adequacy — a decline of ≥10–20% per hour indicates improving perfusion. Failure to clear lactate predicts mortality.
🫁Berlin Definition of ARDS (2012): Acute onset (<1 week) of bilateral pulmonary infiltrates not fully explained by cardiac failure/fluid overload, with PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤300 on PEEP ≥5 cmH₂O. Sepsis is the most common trigger (40% of ARDS). Low tidal volume ventilation (6 mL/kg IBW) remains the single most evidence-based intervention reducing ARDS mortality.
🔬STOP-IT trial (NEJM 2015): For intra-abdominal infections with adequate source control, 4-day antibiotic courses had identical outcomes to 8-day courses. Prolonged antibiotics do not improve outcomes and select for resistance. Source control (drainage, debridement, repair) is the definitive treatment — antibiotics are adjunctive.
🔪In septic shock from an abdominal source, vasopressors should be initiated alongside — not after — IV fluids if the patient is not responding. The old paradigm of "fill first, pressors second" is superseded by early NE initiation, which reduces the total volume required and may improve outcomes (CENSER trial).
