Deep Surgical Site Infection After Rotator Cuff Repair

65-year-old male with deep SSI presenting 7 weeks after rotator cuff repair — MRI shows hardware-adjacent abscess and complete cuff re-tear. Illustrates the diagnostic and management challenges of prosthetic joint infection and deep SSI.

Presentation

Clinical Scenario

65-year-old male with a history of arthroscopic rotator cuff repair 7 weeks prior, now presenting with progressive right shoulder pain, erythema, swelling, and wound drainage. Initially dismissed as normal post-op inflammation at a 2-week wound check. Re-presents 7 weeks post-op with frank pus from the incision site, fever, and shoulder immobility from pain.

Cutibacterium acnes: The Silent Infector

C. acnes is a slow-growing, low-virulence anaerobe that is a dominant colonizer of the shoulder dermis and sebaceous glands. It causes indolent, late-onset infections that are easily missed — cultures must be held for 10–14 days (unlike standard 5-day protocols) to recover this organism. A negative culture at 5 days does NOT rule out C. acnes shoulder PJI. This organism is intrinsically resistant to metronidazole but susceptible to penicillin, amoxicillin-clavulanate, clindamycin, and doxycycline.

MRI Findings

MRI Right Shoulder with Contrast

  • Complete full-thickness re-tear of the repaired rotator cuff (supraspinatus + infraspinatus)
  • Moderate shoulder joint effusion
  • Peritendinous and subdeltoid fluid collections with rim enhancement → abscess formation
  • Hardware (suture anchors) in situ — no overt hardware failure on MRI, but hardware-adjacent enhancement
  • Surrounding soft tissue edema and enhancement consistent with deep infection

MRI vs CT in Shoulder SSI

  • MRI with contrast: Best for soft tissue characterization, abscess delineation, rotator cuff integrity
  • CT with contrast: Better for hardware evaluation, cortical erosion
  • Ultrasound: Rapid bedside assessment; can guide aspiration of joint fluid or abscess

Management

IDSA 2012 PJI Treatment Algorithm

Key Decision: Retain Hardware or Remove?
  • DAIR (Debridement, Antibiotics, Irrigation, Retention): Only viable if <30 days from implantation OR <3 weeks of symptoms AND stable, well-fixed hardware AND susceptible organism. Success rate ~60–80% if criteria met.
  • Hardware removal (this patient): Late presentation (>30 days from implant) + failed initial treatment + biofilm established → hardware removal is required for cure in most cases. With rotator cuff suture anchors, removal may also mandate cuff re-repair at a later date.
  • Two-stage revision: Common for hip/knee PJI; less applicable for rotator cuff (anchors are simpler to remove); consider if significant bone loss or complex anatomy.

Surgical Steps in This Case

  1. Surgical debridement and washout of the shoulder joint and subdeltoid space
  2. Removal of all suture anchors (hardware removal)
  3. Intraoperative tissue cultures × 3–5 samples (including from hardware-bone interface)
  4. Send tissue for histopathology (PMNs >5/hpf in periprosthetic tissue = PJI)
  5. Wound VAC or delayed primary closure
  6. Rotator cuff re-repair deferred until infection cleared (typically 3–6 months)

Perioperative Antibiotics

  • Hold antibiotics until intraoperative cultures taken (if patient stable)
  • If septic or unstable → antibiotics immediately after cultures drawn
  • Empiric: Vancomycin IV (MRSA + CoNS coverage) ± Ceftriaxone (broad gram-positive/negative)
  • Transition based on culture results

Rifampin: The Biofilm Buster

Rifampin has unique activity against biofilm-embedded staphylococci and is included in virtually all PJI staphylococcal regimens requiring hardware retention (IDSA recommendation). However: NEVER use rifampin as monotherapy (rapid resistance emergence); always combine. Key drug-drug interactions: induces CYP450 (reduces efficacy of warfarin, statins, many others). C. acnes is intrinsically resistant to rifampin — do not use for C. acnes PJI.

Clinical Pearls

🦠Culture all shoulder PJI specimens for at least 14 days before calling negative. Standard 5-day lab protocols will miss Cutibacterium acnes in up to 70% of cases. When ordering shoulder PJI cultures, explicitly request extended incubation. This is the most common reason for "culture-negative" shoulder PJI.
💡The IDSA PJI diagnostic criteria require ≥1 major criterion OR ≥3 minor criteria. Major: two positive periprosthetic cultures with the same organism, OR sinus tract communicating with the joint. Minor: elevated ESR/CRP, elevated synovial WBC, elevated synovial PMN%, positive histology (>5 PMN/hpf), single positive culture. Know these criteria — they drive the diagnostic workup.
⚠️Diabetes is a potent risk factor for SSI and should be optimized before elective orthopedic surgery. A preoperative HbA1c <7–7.5% is associated with significantly lower infection rates. This patient's HbA1c of 8.2% at the time of surgery likely contributed to his infection risk.
🩻A complete cuff re-tear in the setting of deep infection is almost always infection-related (mechanical failure from bacteria degrading the suture/anchor interface + inflammation), not a "surgical failure." Attempting re-repair before clearing infection is futile — the repair will fail again. Infection clearance must precede any reconstructive procedure.
💊OPAT (Outpatient Parenteral Antibiotic Therapy) is appropriate for stable patients completing 2–6 weeks of IV antibiotics for PJI. This requires: reliable IV access, patient/caregiver compliance, home nursing arrangements, and close monitoring of drug levels (vancomycin AUC/MIC) and organ function. IDSA OPAT guidelines apply.
