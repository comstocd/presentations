Syncope 2/2 Sinus Node Dysfunction in a Diabetic Patient on Hemodialysis

79-year-old female with ESRD on HD, T2DM, paroxysmal AF on anticoagulation, HFpEF, and CAD presenting with recurrent syncope during dialysis — culminating in a 20-second monitored asystolic pause and dual-chamber pacemaker implantation.

Presentation

ED Triage & Chief Complaint

  • Arrived via EMS from Vestal Dialysis — syncopal episode at the start of dialysis
  • BG in ambulance: 177 mg/dL · BP at triage: 112/57 · HR: 85 · RR: 26 · SpO₂: 100%
  • Alert, answering questions; lightheadedness, frontal headache, nausea

History of Present Illness

  • 79-year-old female with ESRD on HD (since 2022), paroxysmal AF on apixaban, insulin-dependent T2DM, CHF (HFpEF), CAD (single-vessel LAD — found on cath in March), HTN, HLD, hypothyroidism, RA, OSA, anemia of chronic disease
  • Multiple recent hospitalizations for recurrent syncope during dialysis and hypervolemia
  • April 2–5: admitted for acute hypoxemic respiratory failure 2/2 volume overload — required extra HD and increased diuretics
  • April 27: dialysis triggered dyspnea, weakness, nausea, chest pain → dialysis stopped → sent to ED
  • March cardiac catheterization: small-to-moderate basal anteroseptal reversible perfusion defect, transient ischemic dilation of LV; conservative management attempted but patient returned with ongoing chest discomfort → invasive angiography confirmed single-vessel CAD (LAD)
  • Multiple HD access surgeries: implanted/removed catheters, fistulogram with balloon angioplasty (2023) for malfunctioning fistula

Why This Case Matters

Dialysis-associated syncope is common, but this patient has three converging mechanisms that demand urgent cardiac evaluation: (1) diabetic cardiac autonomic neuropathy compounded by (2) uremic autonomic dysfunction, both predisposing to (3) hemodynamically significant sinus node dysfunction — ultimately confirmed by a documented 20-second asystolic pause. The "DM" in the title is not incidental — it is mechanistically central.

Note on Troponin Interpretation in ESRD

Troponin elevation in ESRD is common at baseline and does not reliably distinguish Type 1 MI from chronic elevation. Serial troponins (delta troponin) and clinical context are essential. Here, the troponin of 0.18 in the setting of known single-vessel CAD, demand from recurrent hemodynamic instability, and mild elevation raises concern for Type 2 MI (demand ischemia) rather than plaque rupture — particularly given preserved LVEF on echo.

Imaging

  • Chest X-ray: Cardiomegaly and cephalization of pulmonary vasculature (dilation of upper lobe pulmonary veins ≥ lower lobe veins, reflecting elevated left atrial pressure — an early radiographic sign of pulmonary edema)
  • Echocardiogram: EF 60–65%, Grade II diastolic dysfunction, mild dilation of left and right atria — consistent with HFpEF
  • Head CT: Unremarkable — no acute intracranial pathology

The Key Inpatient Event

April 29 — Monitored Asystolic Pause During In-Hospital Dialysis

Nursing note: Patient off unit at dialysis. RN notified that cardiac monitor showed asystole. Heart rate: asystole for approximately 20 seconds, followed by rate in the high 20s to low 30s. Patient complained of dizziness, shortness of breath, vomiting, and was diaphoretic. Simultaneously, BP was markedly elevated (systolic >250 mmHg). Anti-nausea medication administered. Patient hemodynamically stabilized.

Cardiology consult note: "She did not lose consciousness but had an approximate 19-second pause on monitor. By the time dialysis staff contacted the nursing staff on the cardiac floor, the patient's heart rate had picked back up. After the pause she was in a junctional rhythm in the 30s for some time, then back into what appeared to be atrial fibrillation in the 60s."

A documented 19–20 second asystolic pause with symptomatic bradycardia (junctional escape in the 20–30s) is a Class I indication for permanent pacemaker implantation per the 2018 ACC/AHA/HRS Bradycardia Guidelines. This is sinus node dysfunction with clinically significant pauses — the definitive endpoint of this patient's diagnostic evaluation.

Pathophysiology: Why DM is Central to This Case

Diabetic Cardiac Autonomic Neuropathy (CAN)

CAN is a direct, mechanistically relevant complication of long-standing T2DM that explains this patient's susceptibility to sinus node dysfunction. It is not a coincidental comorbidity.

CAN: Key Facts
  • Affects up to 60% of patients with long-standing T2DM depending on diagnostic criteria used, including subclinical forms detected on autonomic testing; symptomatic CAN is less common (Vinik et al., Diabetes Care 2003)
  • Temporal sequence: Parasympathetic denervation occurs first (initially causing resting tachycardia) → followed by sympathetic denervation → net result: fixed heart rate, blunted chronotropic response, susceptibility to bradyarrhythmias
  • Associated with: prolonged QTc, impaired heart rate variability (HRV), silent MI, sudden cardiac death
  • Associated with increased all-cause mortality in observational studies (~2–3× risk; Pop-Busui et al., Diabetes Care 2017); causality uncertain given confounding by diabetes severity and comorbidities
  • Diagnosis: Cardiovascular reflex tests (Ewing battery) — HR response to Valsalva, deep breathing, standing; HRV analysis
  • No approved pharmacotherapy; risk factor optimization (glycemic control, BP control, statins) may slow progression

Double Hit: CAN + Uremic Autonomic Neuropathy

Compounding Autonomic Dysfunction in ESRD

Uremic autonomic neuropathy (UAN) independently impairs baroreflex sensitivity, sympathetic outflow, and cardiac chronotropy — effects that compound diabetic CAN. In this patient, the combination creates profound autonomic failure: inability to mount appropriate heart rate responses to volume shifts during dialysis, inability to vasoconstrict against rapid fluid removal, and a structurally vulnerable sinus node that may be predisposed to exit block or arrest under hemodynamic stress. The DM is not background noise — it is the predisposing substrate for the sinus node dysfunction that ultimately required pacemaker implantation.

Hypothyroidism: A Reversible Confounder

Hypothyroidism causes bradycardia and can independently precipitate sinus node dysfunction. Per ACC/AHA guidelines, reversible causes of bradycardia must be excluded before pacemaker implantation. In this patient, hypothyroidism was a known diagnosis and presumably managed — but TSH should be confirmed at target before attributing bradycardia solely to intrinsic sinus node disease. Similarly, dialysis-associated electrolyte shifts (particularly potassium) can transiently worsen bradyarrhythmias and are a listed contraindication to pacing in the absence of correction.

Teaching Point: IDH Definition & Management

Intradialytic hypotension is defined as a drop in systolic BP ≥20 mmHg or to <90 mmHg associated with symptoms during HD. It is the most common complication of dialysis and is independently associated with increased all-cause mortality. Management strategies include: cooled dialysate, sodium profiling, volume profiling, midodrine, reduced ultrafiltration rate, and transitioning to more frequent HD modalities (SLED, nocturnal HD). Carnitine supplementation has been explored but evidence supporting its routine use for IDH is limited and it is not a standard recommendation in major guidelines.

Sinus Node Dysfunction

Spectrum of Sinus Node Dysfunction
  • Sinus bradycardia — HR <60 bpm at rest
  • Sinus node exit block — impulse generated but fails to exit the node; P-P interval suddenly doubles
  • Sinus arrest / pause — sinus node ceases firing entirely; escape rhythm takes over (junctional or ventricular)
  • Bradycardia-tachycardia (tachy-brady) syndrome — alternating bradycardia with AF or flutter; AF terminates → long pause → symptomatic bradycardia (classic presentation, highly relevant here given paroxysmal AF)
  • Chronotropic incompetence — failure to achieve age-predicted maximum HR with exercise (≥80% of expected)

This patient's progression: paroxysmal AF → ectopic atrial bradycardia → sinus arrest with junctional escape — encompasses nearly the full spectrum.

SND Is the #1 Pacemaker Indication in the Elderly

SND is among the most common pacemaker indications, accounting for roughly half of implants in some registries. It is primarily a disease of aging — progressive fibrosis of the sinus node and surrounding atrial tissue — accelerated by diabetes, hypertension, and structural heart disease, all present in this patient.

Pacemaker Indications & Modes

Class I Indications (ACC/AHA 2018)
  • Sinus node dysfunction with documented correlation of symptoms and bradycardia
  • Symptomatic sinus bradycardia as result of required drug therapy
  • Sinus node dysfunction with symptomatic chronotropic incompetence
  • Acquired 3rd-degree or advanced 2nd-degree AV block
  • Mobitz II 2nd-degree AV block (with or without symptoms)

This patient meets Class I criteria: documented symptomatic sinus node dysfunction with 20-second asystolic pause and junctional escape in the 20–30s range.

Common Pacemaker Complications
  • Pacemaker syndrome — AV dyssynchrony causing symptoms (fatigue, palpitations, near-syncope); avoided by maintaining AV synchrony (dual-chamber or atrial pacing)
  • Lead displacement — most common early complication (1–2%)
  • Pocket hematoma — increased risk with anticoagulation (this patient on apixaban)
  • Infection — 0.5–1%; requires device extraction
  • Pacemaker-mediated tachycardia — retrograde conduction triggers endless loop; prevented by programming
  • Phrenic nerve stimulation — diaphragmatic pacing if RV lead too proximal

Mode Selection Trial (MOST): Key Findings

2,010 elderly patients with SND were randomized to DDDR vs. VVIR pacing. DDDR did not reduce the primary endpoint of death or non-fatal stroke, but in secondary analyses significantly reduced AF burden and HF hospitalizations over 4 years, with improvements in quality of life indices. For this patient with paroxysmal AF and HFpEF, DDDR is the appropriate choice — confirmed by the implanting team who programmed DDD mode at 70–130 bpm, with sensed and paced AV delay of 220 ms.

Operative Summary

  • Dual-chamber pacemaker implanted via left subclavian vein approach (double-wire technique given venous access difficulty)
  • RV lead: right ventricular apex, active fixation
  • RA lead: right atrial appendage, active fixation
  • Pocket: left prepectoral, subclavicular
  • Initial settings: DDD 70–130 bpm; paced and sensed AV delay 220 ms
  • Post-op CXR: no parenchymal or pleural disease; cardiomegaly with pacer lead; atherosclerotic calcification of thoracic aorta

Post-Pacemaker: Review AV-Nodal Blocking Agents

This patient is on apixaban for paroxysmal AF. Post-pacemaker, any rate-controlling agents for AF (beta-blockers, non-DHP calcium channel blockers, digoxin) should be carefully reviewed. With dual-chamber pacing providing rate support, the threshold for AV-nodal agents shifts — excessive nodal blockade in a pacemaker-dependent patient can still cause symptomatic bradycardia below the programmed lower rate limit if sinus node dysfunction is severe (pacemaker syndrome, lead undersensing).

Outcome

  • Dual-chamber pacemaker successfully implanted; no immediate procedural complications
  • Post-op rhythm: DDD pacing as programmed at 70 bpm lower rate limit
  • Patient subsequently evaluated for ability to safely resume hemodialysis with cardiac rate support in place
  • UTI treated (E. coli — antibiotic selection per sensitivities)
  • Concurrent goals: optimization of dialysate potassium concentration, review of ultrafiltration rate, management of HFpEF and volume balance
  • Follow-up: device interrogation at 2 weeks and 3 months; nephrology to continue HD with cardiac monitoring

Clinical Pearls

CAN + ESRD = double hit on autonomic function. Diabetic CAN affects up to 60% of patients with long-standing T2DM when subclinical forms are included; parasympathetic denervation precedes sympathetic, creating a fixed, low heart rate vulnerable to sinus node exit block. Uremic autonomic neuropathy compounds this independently. Together they create profound autonomic failure — explaining why this patient's sinus node decompensated during the hemodynamic stress of dialysis.
Tachy-brady syndrome is the most dangerous form of SND. Paroxysmal AF that self-terminates is followed by a prolonged sinus pause as the depressed sinus node struggles to recover. This is the mechanism most consistent with this patient's events: AF → pause → junctional escape → hemodynamic compromise. Pacemaker eliminates the pause; AF rate control still required separately.
Troponin elevation in ESRD requires serial measurement and clinical context. Chronic troponin elevation occurs in 20–75% of ESRD patients at baseline due to LVH, reduced clearance, and subclinical ischemia. A single elevated troponin is not diagnostic of ACS. For high-sensitivity troponin assays, a delta rise ≥20% within 1–3 hours supports acute MI (per ESC 0/1h or 0/2h rapid pathway); conventional troponin assays use different delta thresholds and timing. Always integrate with clinical presentation and EKG changes.
Exclude reversible causes before pacemaker implantation. Hypothyroidism, electrolyte abnormalities (hyperkalemia from ESRD), medications (amiodarone, beta-blockers, digoxin, diltiazem), sleep apnea, and Lyme disease are all reversible causes of bradycardia and AV block. Pacemaker is inappropriate if the bradycardia resolves with treatment of the underlying cause. In this patient, hypothyroidism and dialysate electrolyte management required documentation before attributing the pause to intrinsic SND alone.
DDDR pacing reduces AF burden in SND patients. The Mode Selection Trial (MOST) did not show a reduction in the primary endpoint of death or stroke with DDDR vs. VVIR, but secondary analyses demonstrated significantly reduced AF incidence, HF hospitalizations, and improved quality of life. Given this patient's paroxysmal AF and HFpEF, dual-chamber rate-adaptive pacing is the appropriate choice — and what was implanted.
Intradialytic hypotension kills silently. IDH (SBP drop ≥20 mmHg or to <90 mmHg with symptoms) occurs in 20–30% of HD sessions and is independently associated with increased all-cause mortality, myocardial stunning, and cerebral ischemia. Autonomic dysfunction, high ultrafiltration rate, low blood volume, and diastolic dysfunction (preload-dependent physiology) are all operative in this patient. Post-pacemaker, IDH management (cooled dialysate, sodium profiling, midodrine, slower ultrafiltration) remains essential.
