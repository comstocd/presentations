Decompensated Autoimmune Hepatitis: SBP, Staph Bacteremia, ARDS, and Hemorrhagic Ascites

48-year-old female with advanced autoimmune hepatitis, refractory ascites, and prior hepatorenal syndrome presenting with fever, encephalopathy, and abdominal pain. Complicated by coagulase-negative Staph bacteremia, ARDS, difficult intubation, GI bleeding from gastric AVMs, bloody paracenteses, and AKI — with evolving transplant candidacy questions driven by complex hepatic vascular anatomy.

This patient's MELD 3.0 of 21 reflects higher urgency than the original score suggests. MELD determines organ allocation priority — higher score = higher priority on waitlist.

SBP Diagnosis: Criteria & This Patient
  • Classic SBP: PMN count ≥250 /μL in ascitic fluid — treat with ceftriaxone regardless of culture result
  • Bacterioascites: Positive culture but PMN <250 /μL — treat if symptomatic
  • Culture-negative neutrocytic ascites (CNNA): PMN ≥250 but culture negative — still treat as SBP
  • This patient: PMN <250 but symptomatic (fever, abdominal pain, encephalopathy) + concurrent bacteremia. GI recommended ceftriaxone 2g IV daily ×5 days given clinical picture, plus albumin 25g IV q6h — appropriate given bacteremia even without classic SBP criteria

Albumin in SBP: AASLD guidelines recommend IV albumin (1.5 g/kg on day 1, 1 g/kg on day 3) with antibiotics for SBP to prevent hepatorenal syndrome. Albumin reduces 3-month mortality from ~30% to ~10% in this setting (Sort et al., NEJM 1999).

Second Paracentesis (10/31) — Bloody Fluid

Yielded 2,400 mL of light red fluid (previously clear/yellow). CT confirmed increased ascites density compatible with internal bleeding mixing with ascites — likely from gastric AVM bleeding tracking into the peritoneal cavity. Subsequent paracentesis (11/2) yielded 4L of bloody fluid. Patient received 6U PRBC, 2U plasma, and 5U platelets within 36 hours.

Bloody Ascites: Differential

Bloody ascitic fluid (RBC >50,000/μL) suggests: hepatocellular carcinoma (most common), traumatic tap, coagulopathy with bleeding into ascites, peritoneal carcinomatosis, or vascular rupture. In this patient, concurrent gastric AVM bleeding with drainage into the peritoneal cavity is the most plausible explanation given the timing (GI bleeding confirmed by FOBT and drop in H&H) and her history of gastric AVMs ablated in June 2023.

CoNS: True Bacteremia or Contaminant?

Evaluating CoNS Bacteremia
  • CoNS (e.g., S. epidermidis) is the most common blood culture contaminant
  • Features favoring true bacteremia: ≥2 positive bottles, short time to positivity, clinical deterioration, immunocompromised host, indwelling hardware
  • Features favoring contaminant: Single bottle, late positivity, no clinical correlation, no hardware
  • This patient: 2-of-2 bottles positive, clinically septic, and per ID consult, a Mynx closure device placed at the right femoral artery puncture site (from the angiogram at Rochester on 10/4) may have served as a nidus for infection — strongly favoring true bacteremia, not contamination
  • Final speciation as MSSA (not CoNS) confirms a clinically significant pathogen

Daptomycin: Key Teaching Point

Daptomycin is inactivated by pulmonary surfactant and must not be used for pneumonia, regardless of organism susceptibility. It is appropriate for bacteremia, endocarditis, and skin/soft tissue infections. In patients with concurrent pulmonary infection and gram-positive bacteremia, a beta-lactam or vancomycin (if MRSA) must cover the pulmonary source, while daptomycin can cover the bacteremia. CPK should be monitored weekly given risk of skeletal muscle toxicity.

Respiratory Failure, Difficult Intubation & ARDS

Sequence of Respiratory Deterioration

  • 10/26: Volume overload from albumin/IV fluids → BiPAP initiated; furosemide 40 mg BID; albumin stopped
  • 10/27 (01:18): Failed BiPAP; intubated for airway protection (etomidate 20 mg + rocuronium 100 mg)
  • 10/27 CXR: ETT in right mainstem bronchus 3 cm below carina → complete left hemithorax opacification → repositioned
  • 10/30: Ventilated AC/VC+; Vt 350 mL (per ARDSNet 6–8 mL/kg IBW; IBW ~45 kg), RR 20, PEEP 8, FiO2 0.35; ABG 7.34/39/74/21.5
  • 11/3: Extubated after 4 hours of SBT at 1320; placed on BiPAP, then Salter 6 L; SpO2 100% on FiO2 40%

Difficult Airway: Lessons from This Intubation

First attempt with 8.0 ETT failed to pass cords. Second attempt with 7.5 ETT met resistance; patient desaturated to 70% but was mask-ventilable. Bougie failed due to anterior cords and poor angle. Success achieved with hyperangulated stylette + 7.5 ETT. Key teaching: (1) Always have the next size down available; (2) mask ventilation capability is the critical safety net — baggable patient buys time; (3) anterior cords + airway edema (common in ascites/anasarca) predict difficult laryngoscopy; (4) video laryngoscopy would have been the preferred next step if available. Anasarca distorts airway anatomy and tissue compliance — anticipate difficulty in any patient with severe ascites or generalized edema.

Ventilator Management

ARDSNet Low-Tidal-Volume Protocol
  • Target Vt 6–8 mL/kg ideal body weight (IBW) to avoid volutrauma/barotrauma
  • IBW for females = 45.5 + 2.3 × (height in inches − 60) — this patient IBW ~45 kg; Vt target 270–360 mL
  • Vt 350 mL used here — appropriate (7.8 mL/kg IBW)
  • Permissive hypercapnia acceptable (pH ≥7.20) to avoid high driving pressures
  • PEEP titrated to maintain oxygenation while minimizing FiO2 toxicity (>0.60 sustained)
  • Prone positioning for PaO2/FiO2 <150 (PROSEVA trial) — not documented here but should be considered if oxygenation worsens

ABG on 10/27: pH 7.42/PaCO2 29/PaO2 121/HCO3 21 on FiO2 0.70 — respiratory alkalosis with adequate oxygenation at that FiO2. ABG on 10/30: pH 7.34/PaCO2 39/PaO2 74/HCO3 21.5 on FiO2 0.35 — mild hypoxemia, compensated, appropriate for weaning trajectory.

Autoimmune Hepatitis: Background

AIH: Key Features
  • Chronic inflammatory liver disease mediated by autoreactive T cells against hepatocyte antigens
  • Predominantly affects women (70%); bimodal age distribution (10–30 and 45–70)
  • Diagnosis: elevated transaminases, elevated IgG, positive ANA/ASMA (Type 1) or anti-LKM1 (Type 2), liver biopsy showing interface hepatitis with plasma cell infiltrate
  • First-line treatment: Prednisone ± azathioprine (this patient allergic to azathioprine → limited options)
  • Second-line: Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF), tacrolimus, cyclosporine
  • Goal: biochemical remission (normal ALT, IgG); histologic remission before consideration of withdrawal
  • Cirrhosis develops in ~40% if undertreated; once cirrhotic, standard transplant evaluation criteria apply
  • AIH post-transplant recurrence occurs in ~20–30%; lifelong immunosuppression required

Azathioprine Allergy: Clinical Implications

Azathioprine is the standard steroid-sparing agent in AIH. Allergy (hives) forces alternative immunosuppression. Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) is the most commonly used alternative — effective in ~80% of azathioprine-intolerant patients. This patient was maintained on prednisone alone, which is associated with greater steroid side effects and potentially less complete remission. The combination of steroid-only therapy, prior HRS, and now decompensation with bacteremia represents a challenging clinical trajectory.

SAAG: A Clinically Essential Calculation

Serum-Ascites Albumin Gradient (SAAG)

SAAG = Serum albumin − Ascitic fluid albumin

This patient: serum albumin 2.2 g/dL − ascitic albumin <0.4 g/dL = SAAG ≥1.8 g/dL

SAAGInterpretationCauses
≥1.1 g/dLPortal hypertension (transudate)Cirrhosis, heart failure, Budd-Chiari, portal vein thrombosis
<1.1 g/dLNo portal hypertension (exudate)Malignancy, TB peritonitis, pancreatitis, nephrotic syndrome

SAAG has replaced the old "transudate vs. exudate" terminology for ascites and is more accurate (97% sensitivity for portal hypertension). It does not diagnose the specific etiology but is the essential first step in ascites evaluation.

NSAIDs Are Absolutely Contraindicated in Cirrhosis

In cirrhotic patients, systemic vasodilation causes compensatory renal vasoconstriction. Renal perfusion is maintained almost entirely by prostaglandin-mediated afferent arteriolar dilation. NSAIDs (including ketorolac) eliminate this compensatory mechanism, precipitating acute tubular necrosis or HRS. Even a single dose can trigger AKI in advanced cirrhosis. The ketorolac administered on 10/24 was a preventable nephrotoxic exposure in this patient.

Nephrology Recommendations

  • D5W + 150 mEq NaHCO3 at 75 mL/hr (urinary alkalinization for ATN/pigment nephropathy)
  • Avoid all NSAIDs, COX-2 inhibitors, aminoglycosides, and IV contrast
  • Dose all medications per current GFR
  • No dialysis required; renal function improving by 11/3 (CrCl 26)

Transplant Candidacy & Vascular Anatomy

A Complex Transplant Picture

At initial GI consultation, this patient was deemed not a liver transplant candidate at Rochester due to severe vascular disease (portal vein thrombosis, hepatic artery aneurysm, multiple splenic artery aneurysms, hepatic artery-to-portal vein fistula). However, GI subsequently contacted Columbia-Presbyterian, who indicated this is not an absolute contraindication at their center and would consider transplant evaluation as an outpatient after resolution of the acute illness.

Vascular Anatomy Issues

  • Bilobed hepatic artery aneurysm with hepatic artery-to-portal vein fistula: coil embolization performed at Rochester 10/4/23; aneurysm obliterated, fistula closed. However, the vascular anatomy at the hilum remains complex.
  • Multiple splenic artery aneurysms: Rupture risk; complicate surgical planning
  • Portal vein thrombosis: Requires assessment of extent (partial vs. complete, segmental vs. main trunk); extensive PVT significantly complicates vascular anastomosis during transplantation
  • Severely dilated left atrium: Raises concern for portopulmonary hypertension (PoPH) and atrial fibrillation risk perioperatively. PoPH (mean PAP >25 mmHg at rest with PVR elevation) is a relative contraindication to transplant if mean PAP >35 mmHg despite treatment.

Clinical Pearls

MELD 3.0 corrects a decades-long sex disparity. Female patients with cirrhosis historically had higher waitlist mortality than males at equivalent MELD scores because the original model did not account for sex or albumin. MELD 3.0, adopted by UNOS in 2022, adds female sex (+1.33 points) and albumin as variables. This patient's score jumped from 18 (original) to 21 (MELD 3.0), more accurately reflecting her risk.
NSAIDs are absolutely contraindicated in cirrhosis. Even a single dose of ketorolac can precipitate AKI or HRS by eliminating prostaglandin-mediated afferent arteriolar dilation — the last line of renal autoregulation in a patient with splanchnic vasodilation. This was a preventable nephrotoxic exposure in this case.
Daptomycin is inactivated by surfactant and cannot treat pneumonia. Despite excellent gram-positive coverage, daptomycin binds pulmonary surfactant and is rendered ineffective in the lungs. Using it as the sole antibiotic in a patient with concurrent bacteremia and pneumonia is a prescribing error. Always pair with a separate pulmonary-active agent (beta-lactam, vancomycin).
Albumin infusion after large-volume paracentesis is standard of care. LVP of >5 L without albumin replacement (6–8 g/L of fluid removed) causes post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction (PPCD) — a hemodynamic derangement that worsens portal hypertension and increases risk of HRS and death. Albumin is the only proven preventive therapy. In SBP, albumin separately reduces HRS risk and 3-month mortality (Sort NEJM 1999).
Anasarca predicts a difficult airway. Fluid overload causes airway edema at the level of the supraglottis, vocal cords, and subglottis, reducing the lumen and increasing tissue compliance problems. Have smaller ETT sizes ready, call for video laryngoscopy early, and ensure a mask-ventilable patient before any attempt. This intubation required three passes and a hyperangulated stylette — a sobering reminder that "routine intubations" in decompensated liver disease are not routine.
Portal vein thrombosis does not absolutely preclude liver transplantation. Historically considered a contraindication, PVT management has evolved significantly. The extent (partial vs. complete), chronicity, and cavernous transformation guide surgical planning. Expert centers can perform venous reconstruction using iliac vein graft, jump grafts from the superior mesenteric vein, or cavoportal hemitransposition. Referral to a high-volume transplant center for vascular assessment is essential before declaring a patient ineligible.
