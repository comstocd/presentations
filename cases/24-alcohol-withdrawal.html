Alcohol Use Disorder & Withdrawal

Alcohol withdrawal can be life-threatening and is among the withdrawal syndromes most likely to cause death. Delirium tremens carries a substantial untreated mortality risk; with modern management, mortality is much lower but not negligible. Early recognition, correct risk stratification, symptom-triggered benzodiazepine protocols, and thiamine in at-risk patients are the competencies that prevent death.

Epidemiology & Risk Factors

  • ~15 million Americans have alcohol use disorder (AUD)
  • A substantial fraction of individuals with AUD who stop drinking abruptly will develop withdrawal symptoms; exact rates vary by population and definition
  • A smaller minority will develop severe withdrawal, including seizures or delirium tremens
  • Alcohol-related admissions account for a significant proportion of hospital and ICU utilization; inpatient withdrawal is common regardless of primary admission diagnosis

Kindling: Why Each Withdrawal Is Worse

Repeated cycles of alcohol use and withdrawal progressively sensitize neuronal circuits, lowering the threshold for seizures and autonomic instability with each subsequent episode. A patient with five prior withdrawals who "never needed treatment before" may develop DTs on the sixth. Always take prior withdrawal history seriously, even if previous episodes were mild.

Pathophysiology

Alcohol acts as a CNS depressant through two primary mechanisms:

GABA Potentiation

Alcohol enhances GABA-A receptor activity → increased chloride influx → neuronal inhibition.

Chronic exposure: GABA-A receptors downregulate and become subsensitive.

On cessation: Reduced GABAergic inhibition → CNS excitability.

NMDA Antagonism

Alcohol inhibits NMDA glutamate receptors → reduced excitatory neurotransmission.

Chronic exposure: NMDA receptors upregulate and become supersensitive.

On cessation: Unopposed glutamate excitation → CNS hyperexcitability.

The net result of withdrawal is CNS hyperexcitability: anxiety, tremor, seizures, autonomic instability (tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis, fever), and in severe cases, delirium tremens. The same GABA/NMDA imbalance explains why benzodiazepines (GABA-A agonists) are the treatment of choice — they directly address the underlying neurochemical deficiency.

Clinical Timeline of Withdrawal

Key Reference Point: Last Drink

Withdrawal timing is generally estimated from the last drink, though the history may be uncertain and onset can vary. Always ask explicitly: "When did you have your last drink?" A patient who last drank 36 hours ago may be at or near peak risk on arrival. One who last drank 8 hours ago may appear well but is still in the early escalation phase.

Withdrawal Seizures: Critical Teaching Points

  • Typically generalized tonic-clonic, brief (1–3 min), and self-limited
  • May occur as the first sign of withdrawal — before other symptoms are evident
  • Can occur even with a positive BAL as it falls rapidly in a dependent patient
  • Focal features, prolonged seizures, or status epilepticus should prompt workup for structural cause (hemorrhage, meningitis) — do not attribute to withdrawal alone
  • A single withdrawal seizure does not require long-term anti-epileptic therapy; benzodiazepines prevent recurrence
  • After a withdrawal seizure, the patient is at high risk for DTs within the next 12–24 hours

CIWA-Ar: Assessment & Scoring

The Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol — Revised (CIWA-Ar) is a validated tool for symptom-guided treatment in cooperative patients. It includes 10 items with a maximum score of 67.

CIWA-Ar Score Interpretation
ScoreSeverityManagement
<8Minimal / absent withdrawalMonitoring; no pharmacotherapy typically needed
8–15Mild withdrawalSymptom-triggered benzos; oral route acceptable
16–20Moderate withdrawalBenzos q1–2h; reassess frequently; consider IV route

Reassess CIWA-Ar every 1–4 hours depending on severity. Symptom-triggered dosing (treat when CIWA ≥8–10) is often preferred in appropriate patients and can reduce total benzodiazepine exposure and treatment duration compared to fixed-schedule dosing (Mayo-Smith meta-analysis). It requires reliable, frequent nursing assessment of a cooperative patient.

CIWA-Ar Limitations

CIWA-Ar requires patient cooperation and verbal response — it cannot be used in intubated, heavily sedated, or non-verbal patients. In these situations, use clinical judgment and vital sign trends. CIWA-Ar does not directly include vital signs, so clinical assessment must supplement the score — a patient with HR 130 and BP 190/110 but a CIWA of 6 still needs treatment.

PAWSS Score (Prediction of Alcohol Withdrawal Severity Scale)

A validated 10-item tool to identify patients at increased risk for complicated withdrawal at admission. Items include prior severe withdrawal, daily alcohol consumption, serum BAL at admission, and presence of active medical illness. Useful for triage decisions on presentation. Validation studies reported high sensitivity and specificity, though these figures should be interpreted in context.

LOT (Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam) in Liver Disease

Diazepam and chlordiazepoxide have long half-lives and are more likely to accumulate in liver disease, potentially causing prolonged sedation, respiratory depression, and encephalopathy. Lorazepam or oxazepam are often preferred in significant liver disease because they are less affected by hepatic oxidative metabolism — they undergo direct glucuronidation. Individualize agent selection based on the degree of hepatic impairment and clinical context.

Dosing Strategies

Symptom-Triggered (Preferred)

Give benzodiazepine only when CIWA-Ar ≥8–10.

Example: Lorazepam 1–2 mg IV/PO q1h PRN CIWA ≥10.

  • Requires reliable nursing assessment every 1–4 hours
  • Reduces total benzodiazepine dose vs. fixed schedule
  • Shorter treatment duration
  • Requires cooperative, assessable patient
Fixed-Schedule + PRN

Scheduled doses with PRN for breakthrough symptoms.

Example: Chlordiazepoxide 50 mg q6h ×4 doses, then 25 mg q6h ×8 doses; lorazepam 1–2 mg q1h PRN CIWA ≥10.

  • Useful when CIWA-Ar cannot be reliably performed
  • Higher total benzodiazepine use
  • Preferred in prior severe withdrawal, high-risk patients

Front-Loading (Severe Withdrawal / DTs)

Severe withdrawal or DTs may require repeated benzodiazepine dosing titrated to clinical effect, with close monitoring for oversedation and airway compromise. The goal is adequate sedation and suppression of autonomic hyperactivity, not a fixed dose or CIWA target. Some patients with DTs require very large benzodiazepine doses under monitored conditions. Do not under-treat out of concern for respiratory depression — in true DTs, inadequately treated withdrawal poses greater immediate risk than cautious oversedation.

Delirium Tremens

Delirium Tremens: A Medical Emergency

DTs carry a substantial untreated mortality risk; with modern management, mortality is much lower but not negligible. Death occurs from hyperthermia, cardiovascular collapse, respiratory failure, and aspiration. Any patient with clinical DTs should be in a monitored setting with IV access and ability to escalate to ICU.

Diagnostic Features of DTs

  • Confusion and disorientation — distinguishes DTs from alcoholic hallucinosis (which has intact sensorium)
  • Vivid hallucinations — classically visual (insects, animals, small objects); tactile and auditory also common
  • Marked autonomic hyperactivity — tachycardia, hypertension, fever, and profuse diaphoresis; severity varies
  • Agitation — may be extreme; patient may pull lines, fall, injure themselves or staff
  • Onset typically 48–96 hours after last drink; peak at 72 hours

Benzo-Refractory DTs: The Phenobarbital Protocol

When benzodiazepine requirements escalate beyond 40–60 mg diazepam equivalent per hour without adequate sedation, phenobarbital is the next step. It acts on a different site of the GABA-A receptor and does not cross-tolerate with benzodiazepines in resistant cases. Loading dose 10–15 mg/kg IV (slow infusion), then 130 mg IV q15–20 min PRN. Propofol infusion is the fallback for intubated patients in refractory DTs.

Thiamine & Wernicke's Encephalopathy

Give Thiamine Early in At-Risk Patients

In thiamine-deficient patients, glucose without thiamine may worsen or unmask Wernicke's encephalopathy by exhausting remaining thiamine reserves in glucose metabolism. Give thiamine early — ideally before or concurrent with glucose-containing fluids — in any patient with AUD, malnutrition, or suspected thiamine deficiency. Do not delay resuscitation for thiamine, but prioritize it as soon as practically possible.

Wernicke's Encephalopathy (WE)

Classic Triad (Present in Only a Minority of Cases)
  • Encephalopathy — confusion, disorientation, impaired consciousness
  • Oculomotor dysfunction — nystagmus, lateral gaze palsy, ophthalmoplegia; diplopia
  • Ataxia — wide-based gait; inability to stand or walk without support

Key teaching: The classic triad is present in a minority of cases. Most WE presents with only encephalopathy or subtle oculomotor findings. Maintain a low threshold for thiamine supplementation in any AUD patient with altered mental status — missing WE leads to irreversible Korsakoff syndrome.

Korsakoff Syndrome

The chronic, irreversible sequel of untreated or inadequately treated WE. Characterized by severe anterograde amnesia (inability to form new memories), retrograde amnesia, and confabulation (unconscious fabrication of memories to fill gaps). Untreated Wernicke's encephalopathy can progress to Korsakoff syndrome in a significant proportion of patients. Korsakoff amnesia is largely irreversible.

Why IV/IM, Not Oral?

Oral thiamine absorption is severely impaired in AUD due to alcohol-induced damage to intestinal transport mechanisms. IV and IM routes bypass this and achieve adequate tissue levels rapidly. Do not rely on oral thiamine to treat suspected WE or as the sole route in any acutely ill AUD patient. IV is preferred over IM due to faster and more reliable delivery.

Alcohol Use Disorder: Long-Term Management

The hospitalization for withdrawal is a critical opportunity to initiate AUD treatment. Discharge planning that includes pharmacotherapy and counseling referral improves the chance of sustained treatment engagement. Do not miss this window.

Non-Pharmacologic Interventions

  • Brief motivational intervention at bedside during admission — even 5–10 minutes improves 6-month outcomes (FRAMES model)
  • Referral to addiction counseling / outpatient AUD treatment at discharge
  • Alcoholics Anonymous / SMART Recovery — peer support programs that help some patients maintain recovery; outcomes vary and these should be offered as complements to, not substitutes for, evidence-based treatment
  • Social work involvement — housing instability, domestic violence, and psychiatric comorbidity are common; address holistically

Clinical Pearls

Give thiamine early in at-risk patients. Any AUD patient receiving IV fluids, dextrose, or a meal should receive thiamine as early as feasible. In practice: order thiamine 500 mg IV on arrival and give it before or with the first bag of D5W or before feeding. Wernicke's encephalopathy precipitated by glucose in a thiamine-deficient patient is iatrogenic and preventable. Korsakoff syndrome is not.
Withdrawal can begin with a positive BAL. A patient with a BAL of 200 mg/dL who typically drinks to 400 mg/dL is already in relative withdrawal. Do not wait for the BAL to reach zero before initiating treatment. Assess CIWA-Ar and treat based on clinical signs, not the number on the toxicology report.
After a withdrawal seizure, DT risk is elevated. A patient who seizes from alcohol withdrawal is at increased risk of progressing to DTs within the next 6–24 hours. Start benzodiazepines immediately after the postictal period, monitor closely, and strongly consider ICU or step-down level of care. Do not discharge after a single witnessed withdrawal seizure without inpatient monitoring and observation through the at-risk window.
Beta-blockers and clonidine reduce autonomic symptoms but do not treat the underlying withdrawal. They lower heart rate and blood pressure, which can make the CIWA-Ar score appear lower while CNS excitability continues unchecked. This creates false reassurance. They can be used as adjuncts for BP/HR control when clinically necessary, but never as monotherapy for withdrawal management. A patient on atenolol with a "CIWA of 4" and HR of 68 may have very active withdrawal that the beta-blocker is obscuring.
Lorazepam and oxazepam in liver disease. Diazepam and chlordiazepoxide have long half-lives that are further extended in hepatic failure due to impaired CYP450 metabolism. They accumulate, causing oversedation, hepatic encephalopathy, and respiratory failure. Use lorazepam or oxazepam (glucuronidated, no active metabolites) in any patient with cirrhosis, alcoholic hepatitis, or significant liver dysfunction. The dose is the same; the kinetics are predictable.
The hospitalization is a treatment opportunity. Patients with AUD who are admitted for any reason have a "teachable moment" during which they are sober, often frightened, and potentially receptive to change. Even a brief bedside motivational intervention (5–10 minutes) and warm handoff to addiction services at discharge significantly improves 6-month abstinence rates. Discharge without addressing AUD is an incomplete hospitalization.
