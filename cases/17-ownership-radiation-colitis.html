"Ownership": Radiation Colitis, Small Bowel Obstruction, and a Case Nobody Owned

84-year-old male with prior pelvic radiation and a ureteral stent — placed for radiation-induced stricture — who presented with acute colitis and progressively deteriorated over 22 days through missed etiology, delayed nutrition, multi-consultant fragmentation, and ultimately required emergent laparotomy, lysis of adhesions, and loop colostomy. Discharged DNI/DNR with PE, severe protein-calorie malnutrition, and pneumonia.

Case Presentation

Chief Complaint

84-year-old male. Lower abdominal pain, cramping, and diarrhea beginning the night prior to admission. Reported blood in stool per family.

History of Present Illness

The patient presented to the emergency department with left-sided abdominal pain radiating to the right, worsened by movement or coughing, and associated diarrhea. He had never experienced this pain before. He denied nausea, vomiting, fever, chills, sick contacts, or foreign travel. CT abdomen/pelvis was performed and was consistent with colitis. Incidentally noted: ureteral stent with persistent hydronephrosis. He received IV antibiotics and IV fluids in the ED. Blood cultures were drawn. Lactic acid was elevated. WBC was 12,000.

★ Stop Here — The PMH Contains the Diagnosis

The ureteral stent was placed specifically because pelvic radiation caused a ureteral stricture. The sigmoid colon sits immediately anterior to the prostate bed — directly in the radiation field. A patient with radiation-induced ureteral damage almost certainly has radiation-induced bowel damage as well. This was the diagnosis from Day 1.

The Hidden Clue — Present in the Chart from Day 1

What Was in the Chart

  • Prostate cancer s/p prostatectomy + pelvic radiation (2020)
  • Left ureteral stent placed for radiation-induced ureteral stricture
  • Persistent hydronephrosis from that stricture
  • CT: severe long-segment colitis, transverse → sigmoid (radiation field distribution)
  • Prior colonoscopy 2015: incomplete due to stricture
  • Negative stool cultures by Day 3

What Should Have Been Concluded

  • Radiation to the pelvis 2020 → ureteral stricture = established pelvic radiation injury
  • Sigmoid/rectum directly in the prostate radiation field
  • Colitis distribution perfectly matches radiation field
  • Radiation-induced stricture/fibrosis = mechanism for later obstruction
  • Infectious etiology less likely from the outset
  • Expected trajectory: chronic fibrosis, stricture, possible surgical intervention

Intraoperative Findings — Day 10 (The Truth)

"There were severe inflammatory changes in the pelvis. Ulcers in the mucosa of the rectosigmoid; the mucosa was severely inflamed. This was consistent with radiation injury. The whole wall of the sigmoid colon and the rectum was severely fibrosed, reminiscent of a 'lead pipe'." — Operative note, 9/15/2023

Hospital Timeline — 22 Days

Day 1 — 9/6/2023

Admission. CT: severe long-segment colitis transverse → sigmoid. WBC 12K, elevated lactate, AKI. Hospitalist: NPO, IVF, IV Zosyn, GI consult called. Urology notified (hydronephrosis/stent). Stool cultures, C. diff, GI PCR ordered. Lisinopril and statin held.

Day 1 — GI Consult (1st)

GI assessment: "differential includes infectious gastroenteritis vs. possible colonic ischemia." Recommendations: advance diet, IV fluids, stool PCR, further plans pending hospital course.

Day 3

Deterioration. New non-bloody vomiting, now rectal bleeding. XR abdomen: dilated small bowel loops up to 5.6 cm (ileus vs. SBO). Repeat CT: partial SBO, new bilateral pleural effusions and increased ascites. HR now 109. Surgical consult requested.

Day 3 — Surgery Consult (1st)

"Still passing gas and having frequent bowel movements… not an obstructive picture." No acute surgical intervention. Plan for repeat CT and possible upper GI endoscopy.

Day 3 — GI Consult (2nd)

Vomited dark material. Repeat CT showed dilated small bowel with significant fluid in stomach. NG tube could not be passed. Patient intubated in OR for airway protection → upper GI endoscopy performed. 500 cc black fluid evacuated. Lobulated mass lesion at GE junction biopsied → benign gastric polyp.

Days 4–5

Colitis resolving per CT. GI bleeding stable. IV antibiotics continued. Surgical service notes colitis improving, partial SBO/ileus likely secondary to colitis. No acute surgical intervention planned.

Day 6

Worsening. Patient very distended, complaining of SOB. Very scant BM with mucus. Repeat XR ordered. CT shows bowel obstruction. Surgery consult re-requested. Lasix started for bilateral pleural effusions and mild ascites with uncontrolled HTN. NGT placed, confirmed on XR, drained ~700 cc brown liquid.

Day 7 — Overnight

Patient tries to pull out NG tube. Haloperidol 2.5 mg administered. NG tube removed by patient.

Day 7 — Surgery Follow-up (2nd)

"Significant abdominal distention. Passing gas and bowel movements however breathing is compromised due to small bowel air distention." Patient refusing NG tube replacement. Gastric biopsy returns benign. Plan: continue NPO, consider TPN, consider NG tube replacement.

Day 8

Patient unable to tolerate anything PO. Cannot pass NGT — patient refuses. Plan for TPN discussed with primary team. PICC line placed.

Day 9

Respiratory compromise. RR ~28, SpO₂ 94% on 4L NC. Abdomen tense. Last BM 2 days ago. NGT attempts repeated twice — failed, patient refusing. Patient states he will permit endoscopic placement in OR. CT 9/13: contrast only in esophagus and stomach — high-grade SBO. CT 9/14 (no contrast): dilated jejunal loops to 4.4 cm, no contrast to colon, transition point LLQ. TPN not yet started. PICC placed.

Day 10 — Surgery (Operative)

Emergent exploratory laparotomy. Adhesions RLQ (prior prostatectomy/hernia repair) — lysed → bowel decompressed. "Lead pipe" sigmoid/rectum consistent with radiation injury. Flexible sigmoidoscopy performed intraoperatively. Loop colostomy via RUQ opening. 5 sheets of Seprafilm placed. Patient tolerated procedure well.

Day 22 — Discharge

Discharged alive. Code status: DNI/DNR.

Teaching Point 1 — Radiation-Induced Bowel Injury

Incidence
5–15% significant injury after pelvic radiation
Most vulnerable
Rectum and sigmoid (fixed, in-field)
Onset
Acute: 0–6 wk · Chronic: months to years
Mechanism (chronic)
Obliterative endarteritis → ischemia → fibrosis → stricture

Acute Phase (0–6 weeks)

  • Direct mucosal cytotoxicity
  • Crypt cell death, barrier loss
  • Diarrhea, tenesmus, rectal urgency
  • Usually self-limited

Chronic Phase (months–years) — This Patient

  • Obliterative endarteritis of small vessels
  • Progressive ischemia and submucosal fibrosis
  • "Lead pipe" colon — grossly fibrotic, rigid wall
  • Stricture → obstruction; ulceration; fistula

Why the Ureteral Stent Was the Key Clue

Radiation-induced ureteral stricture occurs in ~1–3% of patients after pelvic RT. The ureter runs through the same pelvic radiation field as the rectosigmoid. A stent placed for radiation-induced ureteral stricture is direct histologic surrogate evidence that pelvic radiation caused clinically significant fibrotic injury. The bowel — exposed to the same field — should be presumed involved.

Teaching Point 2 — Small Bowel Obstruction: When to Operate

Non-Operative Trial — Appropriate When:

  • Passing flatus or BM = partial/incomplete obstruction
  • NPO + IV fluids + NG tube decompression
  • Trial period: 48–72 hours
  • Gastrografin challenge: sensitivity 96%, specificity 98% for predicting need for surgery (Cochrane)
  • Serial abdominal exams — trending toward improvement

Operative Indications — Act When:

  • No contrast passage to colon on CT = high-grade/complete SBO
  • Peritonitis, fever, leukocytosis (concern for strangulation)
  • Failure of non-operative management at 48–72h
  • Closed-loop obstruction
  • Worsening respiratory compromise from abdominal distension

Case Application: What Should Have Triggered Earlier Surgery

  • Day 9 CT: Contrast only in esophagus/stomach — high-grade SBO confirmed
  • RR 28, SpO₂ 94% on 4L: Respiratory compromise from abdominal distension
  • 9 days of progressive obstruction failing non-operative measures
  • NG tube refused by patient for 2+ days — non-operative management was not achievable
  • Surgery performed Day 10 was correct — the question is whether Day 7–8 was already the threshold

The NG Tube Refusal — An Ethical Dimension

The patient removed his NG tube overnight on Day 7 and refused re-insertion. He offered to accept endoscopic placement in the OR. Per ACS Ethics guidelines, a capacitated patient may refuse even life-sustaining treatment. However this creates dual obligations: (1) assess decision-making capacity given concurrent Haloperidol administration for agitation and (2) expedite the surgical conversation rather than continuing to defer.

📚Sources: EAST Practice Management Guidelines for SBO (Level I); Di Saverio et al. World J Emerg Surg 2017; Cochrane Review — water-soluble contrast for adhesive SBO; ACS Ethics Manual

This Patient: An 8-Day Gap

  • NPO from Day 1 (admission)
  • Age 84, cancer history, prolonged hospitalization = high nutritional risk
  • ASPEN criteria met by Day 3–5 at the latest
  • TPN not started until Day 9 — a gap of at least 4–6 days beyond indication
  • Discharge Dx: severe protein-calorie malnutrition — directly attributable to delayed nutritional support
  • PICC line placed Day 8 "for TPN" — TPN had not yet been started when surgical consent was obtained

Refeeding Syndrome Prevention

In any patient who has been NPO or severely malnourished, TPN must be initiated at reduced rate (25–50% of goal) for the first 24–48 hours. Electrolytes — particularly phosphate, potassium, and magnesium — must be corrected before initiation. Refeeding syndrome (hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia + fluid shifts) can be life-threatening. This is why the Day 8 surgical note says "correct electrolytes and start TPN" — the sequencing is correct.

📚Sources: ASPEN/SCCM Clinical Guidelines for Nutrition Support — JPEN 2016;40(2):159–211; ESPEN Clinical Nutrition Guidelines 2023; UpToDate — Nutrition Support in Hospitalized Adults

Teaching Point 4 — VTE Prophylaxis & PICC-Related Thrombosis

Discharge Diagnosis: PE from Acute RUE DVT

PICC placed Day 9 for TPN. PE diagnosed by Day 22. Upper extremity DVT from central venous catheters: incidence 2–7% per CHEST guidelines.

Risk Factors in This Patient

  • PICC line (central catheter — highest UE DVT risk)
  • Active malignancy history (prostate cancer)
  • Major intra-abdominal surgery (laparotomy Day 10)
  • Prolonged immobility
  • Severe malnutrition (hypercoagulable state)
  • Age 84

Teaching Point 5 — The Ownership Problem

"Who Is the Captain of This Ship?"

When nobody owns a complex patient, the patient owns the outcome. This case had 7+ consultant visits across 3 services with no designated quarterback — and a patient who progressively deteriorated over 9 days while each service assessed and deferred.

ServiceDayKey DecisionGap
HospitalistDay 1NPO, IVF, Abx, consulted GI & UrologyDid not centralize radiation etiology; delayed TPN by 6+ days
GI (1st consult)Day 1Advance diet, stool PCR, "further plans pending"No definitive plan; no committed follow-up timeline
Surgery (1st consult)Day 3Passing gas → not obstructiveCorrect, but no proactive re-consult threshold defined
GI (2nd consult)Day 3EGD — evacuated gastric fluid, biopsied GE junction massValuable but no ownership of longitudinal plan
Surgery (2nd consult)Day 6NGT placed; partial obstruction, continue conservativeDid not escalate despite 3 more days of worsening
Surgery (3rd consult)Day 9High-grade SBO confirmed; operated Day 10Correct final action — arguably 2–3 days delayed

What the Quarterback Model Requires

  • Synthesize all consultant notes daily — one physician integrates the full picture
  • Set the agenda — what is the single most critical question today?
  • Define escalation thresholds — if obstruction not improving in 48h, re-consult surgery proactively
  • Lead GOC conversations — not delegate to specialists
  • Drive nutrition — don't wait for TPN to emerge from "plans pending"
  • Own the note — hospitalist notes should read like a synthesis, not a relay
📚Sources: Gandhi TK et al. NEJM 2006 (failure to synthesize multi-source information); Landrigan CP et al. NEJM 2004 (handoff fragmentation & adverse events); Joint Commission Sentinel Event data

Goals of Care & Capacity Assessment

When Should the DNI/DNR Discussion Have Happened?

The patient was discharged DNI/DNR. This represents the right endpoint — the question is whether these discussions were appropriately early or reactively late in an 84-year-old with radiation-damaged bowel, PE, malnutrition, and a progressive 22-day hospitalization.

Triggers for Early GOC Discussion

  • At admission in any seriously ill elderly patient with complex comorbidities
  • When diagnosis changes significantly (radiation-induced obstruction, upper GI mass)
  • When patient refuses life-sustaining treatment (NG tube) — capacity must be assessed first
  • Before any high-risk operative consent
  • Joint Commission: GOC documentation required for all inpatients

Capacity vs. Competency — The NG Tube Refusal

Day 7 note: Patient attempting to pull out NG tube → Haloperidol 2.5 mg administered. This is a delirium red flag.

  • Delirium does not automatically eliminate decision-making capacity but raises the threshold for formal assessment
  • Tools: MacCAT-T (MacArthur Competence Assessment Tool) or Aid to Capacity Evaluation (ACE)
  • If capacity absent: healthcare proxy / surrogate must be activated
  • If capacity present: patient may refuse treatment — but this accelerates the need for explicit GOC discussion
  • The chart does not document a formal capacity assessment at this juncture
📚Sources: Appelbaum PS. "Assessment of patients' competence to consent to treatment." NEJM 2007;357(18):1834–1840; ACS Statement on Advance Directives (DNR/DNI in the Operative Setting) 2014

Post-Discharge Prognosis

No post-discharge follow-up data is available from this presentation. Clinical Pearls

💡The PMH contains the diagnosis. A ureteral stent placed for radiation-induced stricture is evidence of significant pelvic radiation injury. The sigmoid colon sits in the same radiation field. When colitis appears in a patient with prior pelvic radiation, radiation-induced colitis must be the leading — not incidental — diagnosis until proven otherwise.
🔬Negative cultures = pivot time. When stool PCR and cultures return negative in an acute colitis patient, the differential must be actively revised. Infectious etiology is not simply "less likely" — it should be formally reconsidered and the leading diagnosis updated in the chart accordingly.
⚠️TPN has criteria — use them. ASPEN defines clear timing thresholds (3–7 days depending on nutritional risk). An elderly patient with malnutrition risk who is NPO beyond 3–5 days meets criteria. "Plans pending" is not a reason to delay nutritional support.
🏥Complexity demands a quarterback. When ≥3 services are actively involved in a patient's care, designate a primary physician who synthesizes, sets the daily agenda, drives escalation, and owns the GOC conversation. Distributed responsibility is equivalent to no responsibility.
💉Every PICC is a thrombosis risk. In a patient with prior malignancy undergoing major abdominal surgery, VTE risk is maximal. LMWH prophylaxis, single-lumen catheters, and daily reassessment of line necessity are non-negotiable standards per CHEST 2022 and ASCO 2023.
🧠Delirium + treatment refusal = formal capacity assessment. When a patient on Haloperidol for agitation refuses a life-sustaining intervention (NG tube), the response is not to document "patient refusing" and move on. It is to formally assess capacity, involve the surrogate if capacity is impaired, and use the decision as a catalyst for GOC conversations.
