Presentation & Timeline

Presenting Scenario

56-year-old male with alcohol use disorder (1–2 pints vodka/day), recurrent alcohol-induced pancreatitis/gastritis, HTN, and hyperlipidemia. Last drink 1/10/2025. Presents 1/11 with nausea and vomiting. Found: SpO₂ 84% on RA, leukocytosis, AKI, and Ca 16.7 mg/dL (critically elevated). Lipase 172 U/L (mildly elevated).

1/10/2025 — Last drink

Patient reportedly consuming 1–2 pints of vodka daily. Abrupt cessation precipitates withdrawal syndrome. Risk factors for severe withdrawal: heavy, prolonged use; prior episodes of DTs.

1/11/2025, 10:39 AM — ED Arrival

Hypoxemia SpO₂ 84%, nausea/vomiting, Ca 16.7 mg/dL, AKI, leukocytosis. CIWA assessment started. Lipase 172.

1/12/2025, 4:06 PM — Nursing Note

A&Ox4, flat affect. CIWA score 4. Starts asking repetitive questions. Hallucinations beginning.

1/13/2025, 11:09 PM — Delirium Tremens

Full DT picture: visual hallucinations (cats/dogs, "electrician," people in hallways), disorientation (states date as "July 7th, 1977"), agitation, pulling IV, attempting to leave, tactile hallucinations (itching). Placed with sitter. Escalating benzodiazepine requirements despite prior Ativan.

Alcohol Withdrawal Syndrome

Pathophysiology

Chronic alcohol use upregulates NMDA glutamate receptors and downregulates GABA-A receptors as compensatory adaptations to ethanol's CNS depressant effects. Abrupt cessation removes ethanol's inhibitory effect → CNS hyperexcitability: seizures, autonomic instability, delirium.

CIWA-Ar Scale — Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol

10 items scored 0–7 (nausea/vomiting, tremor, paroxysmal sweats, anxiety, agitation, tactile disturbances, auditory disturbances, visual disturbances, headache, orientation). Maximum score 67.

  • CIWA <10: Mild — monitoring, supportive care
  • CIWA 10–15: Moderate — consider benzodiazepines
  • CIWA ≥15: Severe — benzodiazepines required
  • CIWA ≥20 or DTs: ICU-level monitoring; may need phenobarbital adjunct

This patient escalated from CIWA 4 → severe DTs requiring escalating benzodiazepines. CIWA-based symptom-triggered dosing is superior to fixed-schedule dosing (reduces total benzodiazepine requirements and duration of treatment; Sullivan et al., NEJM 1989).

Treatment: Benzodiazepines as First-Line

  • Lorazepam (Ativan) — preferred in hepatic impairment (no active metabolites; direct glucuronidation). Dose: 2–4 mg IV q5–15 min PRN for CIWA ≥8–10; can titrate to effect.
  • Diazepam — long-acting, frontloading strategy. Preferred in patients with normal hepatic function. Reduces breakthrough seizures.
  • Chlordiazepoxide — oral, long-acting, lower abuse potential.
  • Phenobarbital adjunct — for severe benzodiazepine-refractory DTs. Growing evidence for phenobarbital monotherapy or early adjunct (Tidwell et al.).
  • Adjuncts: IV thiamine 100–500 mg before any dextrose (prevent Wernicke's); folate; magnesium; multivitamins.

⚠️ Wernicke Encephalopathy Prevention

Always administer thiamine before dextrose in patients with alcohol use disorder. IV thiamine 100 mg (minimum) — some protocols use 500 mg IV TID × 3 days for suspected Wernicke's. Glucose administration before thiamine depletes residual stores and can precipitate acute Wernicke's (ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion).

Differential Diagnosis Framework

Step 1: PTH-Mediated vs. Non–PTH-Mediated

Check intact PTH and PTHrP simultaneously.

  • PTH ↑ or inappropriately normal: Primary hyperparathyroidism (most common outpatient cause; usually mild); tertiary hyperparathyroidism (CKD).
  • PTH ↓ + PTHrP ↑: Humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy (most common inpatient cause of severe hypercalcemia; lung squamous cell, renal cell, breast).
  • PTH ↓ + PTHrP ↓ + 1,25(OH)₂D ↑: Granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis, TB, fungal), lymphoma (ectopic 1-alpha-hydroxylase).
  • PTH ↓ + PTHrP ↓ + 25(OH)D ↑: Vitamin D toxicity.
  • PTH ↓ + PTHrP ↓ + both vitamin D forms ↓: Osteolytic metastases, multiple myeloma, milk-alkali syndrome, thiazide diuretics.

Calcium 16.7 mg/dL in This Patient — What's Driving It?

This patient has multifocal pneumonia (CT chest: upper-lobe ground-glass opacities). In a patient with alcohol use disorder and upper-lobe pulmonary disease, the differential for hypercalcemia should prominently include:

  • Granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis, mycobacterial, fungal) — upper lobe predilection, ectopic 1,25-OH₂D production from activated macrophages.
  • Occult malignancy — lung cancer (squamous or small cell) with PTHrP secretion.
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism — though PTH-mediated Ca rarely exceeds 14 mg/dL.

Workup: Intact PTH, PTHrP, 1,25(OH)₂D, 25(OH)D, ACE level, SPEP, urine UPEP, CBC differential (lymphoma), consider bronchoscopy/BAL if no clear source.

Hypoxemia in This Clinical Context

SpO₂ 84% on room air at presentation is severe hypoxemia. In a patient with alcohol use disorder and multifocal pneumonia, consider:

  • Aspiration pneumonia/pneumonitis — upper-lobe pattern in chronic alcohol users who vomit and aspirate during intoxication or withdrawal
  • ARDS — acute pancreatitis is a classic precipitant of ARDS (pulmonary lipase injury, systemic inflammation)
  • Community-acquired pneumonia — alcohol users have ↑ risk of S. pneumoniae, Klebsiella pneumoniae, anaerobes
  • COVID-19 — documented negative in this case

Evidence-Based Management

Priority 1: Alcohol Withdrawal

  • CIWA-based symptom-triggered benzodiazepines (lorazepam IV in setting of hepatic disease)
  • IV thiamine 500 mg TID × 3 days (Pabrinex regimen; then 100 mg daily PO)
  • Folate, MVI, magnesium repletion
  • ICU/step-down level care given DTs
  • Consider phenobarbital if benzodiazepine requirements escalating (>40 mg diazepam equivalents/hour)
  • Propofol infusion for refractory DTs in intubated patients

Priority 2: Hypercalcemia Crisis Management

Stepwise Treatment for Severe Hypercalcemia (>14 mg/dL)

  1. IV Normal Saline: 200–500 mL/hr initially — restores GFR, enhances urinary Ca excretion. Do not add furosemide until adequately volume-expanded (old "saline-furosemide" approach largely abandoned).
  2. Bisphosphonate: Zoledronic acid 4 mg IV over 15–30 min (onset 24–48 hrs, peak 4–7 days, duration weeks). Superior to pamidronate for severe hypercalcemia. Adjust dose for renal impairment.
  3. Calcitonin: 4 IU/kg SC/IM q12h — rapid onset (4–6 hrs), modest effect (↓ Ca ~1–2 mg/dL), tachyphylaxis within 48 hrs. Use as bridge while waiting for bisphosphonate effect.
  4. Glucocorticoids: Effective for granulomatous/lymphoma-mediated hypercalcemia (inhibit ectopic 1-alpha-hydroxylase). Prednisone 40–60 mg/day or hydrocortisone 200–300 mg/day IV.
  5. Dialysis: For refractory hypercalcemia with renal failure or cardiac instability.
  6. Denosumab: 60–120 mg SC — for bisphosphonate-refractory or contraindicated (severe renal impairment).

Priority 3: Treat Underlying Cause

  • Treat pneumonia (coverage for aspiration pathogens: piperacillin-tazobactam or ampicillin-sulbactam + azithromycin)
  • Complete hypercalcemia workup (PTH, PTHrP, vitamin D levels, ACE, SPEP) to identify etiology
  • If sarcoidosis confirmed → steroids are both anti-inflammatory and calcium-lowering
  • AKI management: IV fluids, avoid nephrotoxins, monitor UOP

Clinical Pearls

⚠️Calcium >14 mg/dL is a medical emergency. The "bones, stones, groans, and psychic moans" mnemonic helps recall manifestations: bone pain/pathologic fractures, nephrolithiasis/polyuria, constipation/nausea/pancreatitis, and confusion/depression/coma. This patient has groans (nausea/vomiting) and psychic moans (altered mental status — complicated by concurrent withdrawal).
💡Do not attribute altered mental status in a withdrawing alcohol-dependent patient to withdrawal alone. This patient's calcium of 16.7 mg/dL independently causes encephalopathy, making the clinical picture more complex. Both must be treated simultaneously.
🔬Upper-lobe pulmonary infiltrates + severe hypercalcemia = sarcoidosis until proven otherwise. Activated alveolar macrophages in sarcoidosis express 1-alpha-hydroxylase, converting 25(OH)D → 1,25(OH)₂D → increased GI calcium absorption and bone resorption. Glucocorticoids treat both the lung inflammation and the hypercalcemia.
💊The saline-furosemide strategy for hypercalcemia (previously a standard recommendation) has largely fallen out of favor. Aggressive furosemide use before adequate volume repletion can worsen AKI. The focus should be on saline hydration, with furosemide reserved for volume overload prevention after adequate repletion.
🧠DT mortality is 5–15% without treatment but <1% with appropriate benzodiazepine management. Risk factors for DTs: prior DT episodes, longer duration/higher quantity of alcohol use, concurrent medical illness, older age, and seizures during the current withdrawal episode.
