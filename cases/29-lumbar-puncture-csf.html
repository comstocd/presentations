Lumbar Puncture & CSF Interpretation

Lumbar puncture is an essential diagnostic and therapeutic procedure. The ability to perform it safely, interpret opening pressure correctly, and extract maximum diagnostic information from a CSF specimen distinguishes a skilled clinician from one who merely orders the test. Know the indications, know the contraindications, know the tubes, and know what each result means.

Indications

Diagnostic Indications
  • Suspected bacterial meningitis
  • Suspected viral encephalitis (HSV, arboviral, enteroviral)
  • Suspected fungal meningitis (Cryptococcus)
  • Suspected TB meningitis
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) with negative CT head
  • CNS malignancy (leptomeningeal carcinomatosis)
  • Autoimmune encephalitis (anti-NMDA, LGI1, CASPR2)
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome (albuminocytologic dissociation)
  • Multiple sclerosis (oligoclonal bands, IgG index)
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus (opening pressure, clinical response)
  • Neurosyphilis (VDRL in CSF)
  • CNS vasculitis
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH) — diagnosis and therapeutic
Therapeutic Indications
  • Idiopathic intracranial hypertension — large volume LP (20–40 mL) to relieve pressure and assess visual fields response
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus — high-volume LP (30–50 mL) as diagnostic and therapeutic trial; gait improvement supports shunt candidacy
  • Intrathecal drug delivery: chemotherapy (methotrexate, cytarabine), antibiotics (rare), analgesics
  • Cryptococcal meningitis — serial LPs to manage elevated ICP when shunting not feasible

Do Not Delay Antibiotics for LP in Suspected Bacterial Meningitis

If bacterial meningitis is suspected, start dexamethasone and antibiotics immediately. Do not delay treatment while waiting for CT, LP setup, or any other evaluation. Each hour of delay increases mortality and morbidity exponentially. Blood cultures take 2 minutes to draw; obtain them, then give antibiotics. LP can follow within hours — the CSF will still be informative for cell count, glucose, and protein even after antibiotic administration. The CSF culture may become negative after 2–4 hours of antibiotics, but clinical management should never wait for an LP.

Contraindications

Absolute Contraindications
  • Infection at the insertion site — skin, soft tissue, or epidural abscess overlying the LP site; seeding of the subarachnoid space is catastrophic
  • Signs of transtentorial herniation — Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, respiratory depression), unequal pupils, posturing; LP in this setting causes immediate herniation and death
  • Known obstructive hydrocephalus with impending herniation
Relative Contraindications (Proceed With Caution)
  • Coagulopathy: INR >1.5; platelets <50,000; therapeutic anticoagulation. Correct before LP when possible. If urgent meningitis, risk-benefit favors LP.
  • Thrombocytopenia: Transfuse to ≥50,000 before LP; some experts require ≥80,000–100,000
  • Space-occupying lesion on CT (tumor, abscess) with mass effect — herniation risk; may need neurosurgery input
  • Suspected spinal epidural abscess — LP may spread infection; MRI spine first
  • Prior lumbar spine surgery — distorted anatomy; fluoroscopy or CT guidance may be needed
  • Severe spinal stenosis or deformity

When to CT First

Head CT before LP is not universally required. In a patient who is alert, has no focal neurological deficits, and has no papilledema, LP can be performed safely without prior CT. Mandating CT before every LP delays life-saving treatment in meningitis unnecessarily.

Perform CT Head Before LP (IDSA / AAN Recommendations)

CT is indicated before LP in any of the following:

  • Immunocompromised patient (HIV, organ transplant, malignancy, chronic steroids)
  • History of CNS disease (tumor, stroke, focal infection)
  • New-onset seizure within 1 week
  • Papilledema on fundoscopic exam
  • Abnormal level of consciousness (GCS <15)
  • Focal neurological deficit (cranial nerve palsy, hemiparesis, aphasia, visual field defect)

If any of these are present: draw blood cultures, start dexamethasone + antibiotics, obtain CT head, then LP when safe. Never withhold antibiotics while waiting for CT.

A Normal CT Does Not Guarantee LP Safety

CT is insensitive for early cerebral edema and early herniation. A patient with papilledema and a normal CT can still herniate after LP due to elevated ICP not yet visible on imaging. Clinical signs of herniation (Cushing's triad, pupil asymmetry, posturing) are absolute contraindications to LP regardless of CT findings.

Technique

Patient Positioning

Lateral Decubitus (Fetal Position)
  • Preferred for measuring opening pressure (patient must be relaxed; legs extended)
  • Knees to chest; neck flexed; back at edge of bed; hips and shoulders stacked vertically
  • Most important: the spine must be parallel to the bed surface and the hips squared (not rotated)
  • An assistant holds the patient in position
Seated (Upright) Position
  • Patient seated on edge of bed, feet on stool, leaning over a pillow on a table
  • Easier to identify midline in obese patients
  • Cannot accurately measure opening pressure (CSF column height is different in seated position)
  • If opening pressure needed: perform in lateral decubitus, or reposition after CSF obtained

Landmark Identification

  • The spinal cord ends at L1–L2 in adults; LP must be performed below this level to avoid cord injury
  • Identify the L3–L4 or L4–L5 interspace (most common; both are safe): draw an imaginary line connecting the iliac crests (Tuffier's line) — this crosses the spine at approximately L3–L4 or L4
  • Alternative: L2–L3 (acceptable if L3–L4 is difficult); L5–S1 (often tried in obese patients)
  • Ultrasound guidance increases first-pass success, reduces traumatic taps, and is strongly recommended in obese patients, those with prior lumbar surgery, or after multiple failed attempts at landmark technique

Procedure Steps

  1. Position patient; identify and mark target interspace
  2. Sterile prep (chlorhexidine or betadine); sterile drape; sterile gloves, mask, and cap
  3. Anesthetize skin and deeper tissues with 1% lidocaine; wait 2–3 minutes
  4. Insert spinal needle (20–22 gauge; 25–27 gauge in outpatients for headache prevention) with bevel parallel to dural fibers (bevel up = parallel to longitudinal dural fibers → separates rather than severs fibers → lower rate of post-LP headache)
  5. Advance slowly; feel for a "pop" as needle passes through ligamentum flavum, then dura; remove stylet to check for CSF flow
  6. Attach manometer and three-way stopcock; measure opening pressure with patient relaxed and legs extended
  7. Collect CSF in four labeled tubes (see below)
  8. Replace stylet before withdrawing needle (reduces post-LP headache by preventing CSF leak along the needle tract)
  9. Apply pressure and bandage; patient may sit up after procedure

Bevel Orientation Matters

Insert the spinal needle with the bevel oriented parallel to the long axis of the spine (bevel up, bevel facing the ceiling in lateral decubitus). This causes the needle to separate dural fibers longitudinally rather than transect them transversely, resulting in a smaller, more self-sealing defect and significantly lower rates of post-LP headache. This is one of the most reliably effective, evidence-based technique modifications.

Opening Pressure Requirements

Opening pressure is only valid when measured in the lateral decubitus position with the patient fully relaxed and legs extended. Crying, breath-holding, Valsalva, flexed legs, or muscle tension all falsely elevate the pressure. If the patient is not relaxed, wait and recheck. A falsely high opening pressure leads to unnecessary treatment; a falsely low pressure misses elevated ICP. Converting cm H2O to mmHg: divide by 1.36 (normal ICP ≤15 mmHg = 20 cm H2O).

Traumatic Tap vs. Subarachnoid Hemorrhage

The Most Important CSF Distinction in Emergency Medicine

A patient with the "worst headache of their life" and a bloody LP may have a traumatic tap (common, benign) or SAH (life-threatening). Misclassifying SAH as a traumatic tap leads to missed diagnosis, aneurysm rebleeding, and death. Misclassifying a traumatic tap as SAH leads to unnecessary angiography, surgery, and complications. Getting this right matters enormously.

Xanthochromia by Spectrophotometry: The Gold Standard

Visual inspection of xanthochromia (yellow tinge of the supernatant after centrifugation) has poor inter-observer reliability. Spectrophotometry at 415 nm detects oxyhemoglobin and bilirubin with far greater sensitivity and objectivity. If SAH is seriously considered and RBCs are present in CSF, request spectrophotometry specifically — not just "xanthochromia" which implies visual inspection only. The specimen must be protected from light and sent fresh to the lab immediately. False negative spectrophotometry is most likely if LP is performed within 2 hours (insufficient time for oxyhemoglobin conversion) or before 6False negative spectrophotometry can occur if the LP is performed within 2 hours of the bleed (not enough time for hemoglobin breakdown to bilirubin).ndash;12 hours (insufficient bilirubin accumulation); the bilirubin signal is the more specific indicator of true hemorrhage.

Post-LP Headache (PDPH)

Characteristics

  • Onset: 24–48 hours after LP (rarely immediate)
  • Classic feature: positional — worse when upright, relieved within 15–30 minutes of lying flat
  • Typically bifrontal or occipital; may radiate to neck; associated with nausea, photophobia, and diplopia (cranial nerve VI palsy from ICP changes)
  • Caused by CSF leak through the dural defect → intracranial hypotension → traction on pain-sensitive structures

Prevention

  • Use smaller-gauge needle (22–25 gauge vs. 20 gauge; smaller = less PDPH)
  • Use atraumatic (pencil-point) needle tip (Sprotte or Whitacre) — reduces PDPH rate from ~20% to ~5%; strongest evidence-based prevention strategy
  • Bevel parallel to dural fibers (reduces dural fiber transection)
  • Replace stylet before needle withdrawal (reduces CSF leak along needle tract)
  • Bed rest after LP: evidence does not support mandatory bed rest; ambulation does not increase PDPH risk
  • Hydration: no evidence for IV hydration preventing PDPH; encourage oral fluids

Clinical Pearls

Never delay antibiotics for LP in suspected bacterial meningitis. Blood cultures take 2 minutes. Give dexamethasone and antibiotics immediately after blood cultures are drawn. LP can follow within hours — cell count, protein, and glucose remain informative even after antibiotics. Gram stain and culture sensitivity fall after 2–4 hours of antibiotics, but the clinical syndrome and CSF biochemistry remain interpretable. A dead patient cannot benefit from a culture result.
A normal CT does not guarantee LP safety. CT is insensitive for early cerebral edema and impending herniation. The clinical examination — papilledema, Cushing's triad, pupil asymmetry, posturing — is what determines LP safety, not the CT. If clinical signs of herniation are present, LP is absolutely contraindicated regardless of CT findings.
Xanthochromia by spectrophotometry, not visual inspection, distinguishes SAH from traumatic tap. Visual inspection has poor inter-observer reliability. Spectrophotometry at 415 nm detects both oxyhemoglobin and bilirubin sensitively and objectively. Request it specifically when SAH is in the differential. Send the specimen immediately protected from light. False negatives occur if LP is within 2 hours of the bleed — if strongly suspected, repeat LP or CT angiography.
Albuminocytologic dissociation in GBS: high protein, normal cells. In Guillain-Barré syndrome, CSF protein is markedly elevated (often >100–500 mg/dL) while the cell count is normal or minimally elevated. This combination — classically taught as albuminocytologic dissociation — is present in only 50–66% of GBS patients at the time of LP and is not required for diagnosis. A normal LP does not exclude GBS.
Use an atraumatic needle tip and parallel bevel orientation. The two most effective technique modifications to reduce post-LP headache: (1) atraumatic (pencil-point) needle tip reduces PDPH from ~20% to ~5%, and (2) bevel parallel to dural fibers minimizes dural defect size. If your institution stocks only cutting needles, orient the bevel parallel. If atraumatic needles are available, use them by default for elective LP.
HSV encephalitis PCR can be negative in the first 24–72 hours. HSV PCR of CSF has sensitivity approaching 98% but only after 72 hours of symptoms. In the first 24–72 hours, a negative HSV PCR does not exclude HSV encephalitis. If clinical suspicion is high (fever, temporal lobe abnormality on MRI, behavioral change, seizures), start empiric acyclovir and repeat LP at 3–7 days. Withholding acyclovir for a negative early PCR is a dangerous decision.
