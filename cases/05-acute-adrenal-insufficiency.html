Acute Adrenal Insufficiency

A patient on chronic hydrocortisone travels to Florida, develops an acute illness, receives a single IV hydrocortisone dose in the ED, is discharged, and deteriorates at home on a reduced dose — illustrating the high stakes of stress dosing and sick-day rules.

Case Narrative

Opening Quote

"Considering the possibility of adrenal insufficiency is of crucial importance in critically ill patients. If the diagnosis is missed, the patient will probably die." — Wolfgang Oelkers, M.D. NEJM 1996.

Friday, July 14

Patient develops acute illness while visiting family in Florida. Known history of adrenal insufficiency, prescribed hydrocortisone 40 mg AM / 20 mg PM (total 60 mg/day — suggesting secondary or primary AI on established replacement).

Tuesday, July 18

Patient calls answering service (4 days into illness). After-hours physician advises ER evaluation.

Wednesday, July 19

Florida ER visit. Given 100 mg IV hydrocortisone → patient "immediately felt better." Discharged with hydrocortisone prescription. Felt better until the following Tuesday.

Following Tuesday

Feeling as bad as the prior Friday. Calls answering service again. Tapered to 20 mg BID (40 mg/day) — felt terrible. Back to symptoms.

Key Insight: Tachycardia Despite Normal BP

Resting tachycardia (HR 120) in a patient with known AI who is deteriorating after steroid dose reduction, without fever or alternative explanation, is a hallmark of relative adrenal insufficiency. Cortisol normally supports cardiac contractility and vascular tone. Its deficiency causes compensatory tachycardia preceding frank hypotension.

Adrenal Axis Physiology

The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis is the master stress-response system:

  • CRH (hypothalamus) → ACTH (pituitary) → Cortisol + Aldosterone + DHEAS (adrenal cortex)
  • Normal cortisol production: ~5–10 mg/day basal; up to 100–300 mg/day during maximal physiologic stress
  • Cortisol actions: gluconeogenesis, anti-inflammation, vascular reactivity (maintains catecholamine receptor sensitivity), immune modulation
  • Aldosterone: mineralocorticoid — sodium retention, potassium excretion (regulated by RAAS independently of ACTH in primary AI)

This Patient: Likely Secondary AI

Prescribed hydrocortisone 40 mg AM + 20 mg PM (total 60 mg/day) — this is higher than typical physiologic replacement for primary AI (15–25 mg/day). The absence of electrolyte abnormalities mentioned, normal BP, and no hyperkalemia suggest secondary AI, where aldosterone is preserved. His ACTH would be expected to be low-normal from chronic glucocorticoid suppression.

Diagnosis & Testing

When to Test

  • Unexplained fatigue, weight loss, anorexia, nausea/vomiting
  • Hyponatremia (SIADH-like in secondary AI; hypovolemic in primary AI)
  • Hypoglycemia, eosinophilia, hypercalcemia
  • Hyperpigmentation (primary AI)
  • Hypotension unresponsive to pressors ("vasoplegic" shock)
  • History of steroid use, pituitary disease, or autoimmune disorders

Cosyntropin (ACTH) Stimulation Test

Standard-Dose Cosyntropin Stimulation Test (250 mcg IV)
  • Measure baseline cortisol, then give cosyntropin 250 mcg IV/IM
  • Measure cortisol at 30 and 60 minutes
  • Normal response: Peak cortisol ≥18–20 mcg/dL (institutional thresholds vary)
  • Abnormal response (AI): Peak cortisol <18 mcg/dL
  • Limitations: May miss early secondary AI (adrenals not yet atrophied); falsely normal in early pituitary disease
  • Low-dose test (1 mcg) is more sensitive for secondary AI — used in some centers

Adrenal Crisis: Recognition & Emergency Management

⚠️ Adrenal Crisis — Do Not Delay Treatment for Testing

Classic presentation: profound hypotension + abdominal pain + fever + vomiting in a patient with known or suspected AI. Treatment must not wait for cortisol results — empiric hydrocortisone saves lives.

Endocrine Society / NICE Emergency Protocol

  1. Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV bolus immediately (or IM if no IV access)
  2. Normal saline 1 L IV over 30–60 minutes (hypovolemia from mineralocorticoid deficiency)
  3. Continuous hydrocortisone infusion 200 mg over 24 hours (OR 50 mg IV q6h)
  4. Dextrose-containing IV fluids if hypoglycemia present
  5. Identify and treat the precipitating cause (infection, trauma, surgery)
  6. Monitor glucose, electrolytes, blood pressure q4–6h
  7. If BP improves → taper to normal replacement over 24–48 hours as tolerated

Why Did 100 mg IV Hydrocortisone Work Immediately?

IV hydrocortisone achieves supra-physiologic plasma cortisol levels within minutes, restoring vascular tone (catecholamine sensitization), cardiac contractility, and anti-inflammatory effects. The patient's prompt response to 100 mg IV (with immediate symptomatic improvement) is diagnostic confirmation of adrenal insufficiency.

This Patient's Error: Premature Dose Reduction

The patient was discharged from the Florida ED with resumption of his baseline dose (40 mg AM + 20 mg PM) after acute illness — and then subsequently reduced to 20 mg BID. Neither step accounted for the ongoing physiologic stress demand. The correct approach: maintain stress dosing (triple-dose) until fully recovered, then taper to baseline over several days.

Patient Education: Emergency Action Plan

  • Emergency injection kit: All AI patients should carry prefilled hydrocortisone 100 mg IM syringe for use if unable to take oral medications or are unwell and cannot reach medical care
  • Medical alert bracelet/card — "Adrenal Insufficiency — Needs Hydrocortisone in Emergency"
  • Written sick-day protocol — clearly documented with 2×, 3× dosing rules
  • When to go to the ER: Vomiting that prevents oral medications, severe symptoms not improved after 2 hours of increased doses, altered consciousness

Clinical Pearls

⚠️Never reduce hydrocortisone during active illness. Physiologic cortisol requirements increase dramatically during infection, surgery, trauma, and psychological stress — up to 10× the basal rate. Premature tapering is a common and potentially fatal error.
💡Resting tachycardia with normal blood pressure in a patient with AI = relative adrenal insufficiency. Cortisol potentiates catecholamine-mediated vasoconstriction; its deficiency causes peripheral vasodilation → compensatory tachycardia. Hypotension is a late and often pre-terminal sign.
🩺The immediate, dramatic response to IV hydrocortisone is virtually diagnostic of adrenal insufficiency — mimicking no other condition. This patient's "immediately felt better" after 100 mg IV is textbook adrenal crisis presentation and treatment response.
🔬The 250 mcg cosyntropin test may miss early secondary AI (within weeks of pituitary damage, the adrenal cortex has not yet atrophied and can still respond to pharmacologic ACTH). The 1 mcg (low-dose) test is more sensitive in this setting. Morning cortisol <3 mcg/dL on its own is diagnostic of AI without further testing.
💊Mineralocorticoid replacement (fludrocortisone 0.05–0.2 mg/day) is only needed in PRIMARY adrenal insufficiency. Secondary AI preserves RAAS-driven aldosterone secretion (ACTH has minimal effect on aldosterone in the zona glomerulosa). Not giving fludrocortisone in secondary AI is correct — the distinction matters practically.
