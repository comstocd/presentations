Spontaneous Renal Subcapsular Hematoma & Retroperitoneal Hemorrhage

35-year-old male post-lithotripsy with rapidly dropping hemoglobin, flank pain, and a large left renal subcapsular hematoma (Wünderlich syndrome) — managed with interventional radiology versus surgery.

Case Timeline

Background

35-year-old male with renal stones + left ureteral stent → admitted for complicated UTI → lithotripsy + stent exchange → postoperative pyelonephritis (resolved) → 2 days later: gross hematuria + acute urinary retention → Foley placed → continued bloody urine.

Prior Overnight

Sudden 10/10 back pain radiating to abdomen + "pop in his back." BP 166/118, HR 140. Percocet + oxycodone without relief. TTP at rib angles mid-thoracic. CXR/XR abdomen ordered. WBC 15.68, H/H 9.8/29.9. Frank hematuria with clots.

AM Labs

H/H drops to 7.8/22.9. Closely monitored.

CT Abdomen w/o Contrast

CRITICAL finding: Large left renal subcapsular hematoma (6 cm thick) + left retroperitoneal hemorrhage + hyperdense hemorrhage in left renal collecting system. Right renal angiomyolipoma (AML) 6.1 cm — unchanged. H/H drops further to 7.1 g/dL. Multiple pRBC transfusions given.

ICU Transfer

Transferred to ICU. CT angiogram considered if Hgb continues to drop. IR (Dr. Abdelbakia) accepts transfer.

Follow-up CT w/ Contrast (9/14)

No active contrast extravasation. IR recommends observation — suspecting self-tamponade.

Follow-up CT w/ Contrast (9/16)

Hematoma now 16.1 × 12.8 × 18.1 cm — increased from 14.2 × 10.6 × 16.2 cm. Mass effect on left kidney. Delayed nephrogram suggesting renal compression.

Renal Subcapsular Hematoma: Wünderlich Syndrome

Wünderlich syndrome (1856) refers to spontaneous, non-traumatic renal hemorrhage — most commonly presenting as a renal subcapsular or perinephric hematoma. It is a urologic emergency.

Post-ESWL Renal Hematoma

Subcapsular hematoma is a known complication of extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy (ESWL) — occurring in 0.5–4% of procedures. Risk factors: hypertension, obesity, coagulopathy, large stone burden. The shock waves cause cavitation injury to renal parenchyma and microvasculature. Presentation is typically 24–72 hours post-procedure (delayed rupture of subcapsular collection).

Classic Triad of Wünderlich Syndrome (Lenk's Triad)

1
Acute flank pain
2
Flank mass (palpable)
3
Hypovolemic shock

The full triad is present in only ~40% of cases. Isolated flank pain + hemodynamic instability + dropping Hgb is the most common presentation.

Imaging Findings & Interpretation

CT Without Contrast (Initial)

  • Hyperdense crescent of blood (50–80 HU) in subcapsular space — crescentic, conforming to renal margin
  • Retroperitoneal fat stranding, hematoma extension
  • Right AML 6.1 cm: fat-containing mass — characteristic of AML; fat = negative HU values on CT
  • Left renal stone 13 mm
  • No hydronephrosis
  • Small volume ascites; small left pleural effusion

CT Angiography (CTA) — When and Why

  • CTA identifies active arterial extravasation (blush on arterial phase) — indicates ongoing bleeding requiring intervention
  • In this case: initial CTA showed NO active extravasation → conservative management trialed
  • Growing hematoma on follow-up CT despite no active blush suggests oozing rather than arterial bleeding — still mandates intervention if hemodynamically unstable

The Right Renal AML — A Separate but Important Finding

Right renal AML at 6.1 cm. AMLs >4 cm have ~50% lifetime risk of spontaneous rupture (Wünderlich syndrome). Current guidelines (AUA/EAU) recommend prophylactic embolization for sporadic AMLs >3–4 cm, even asymptomatic. This patient has an indication for elective right renal AML embolization after recovery from the current left-sided hemorrhage.

Management Algorithm

Hemodynamic Assessment First

Unstable → Emergency

  • Active resuscitation (massive transfusion protocol if needed)
  • Emergent angiography + embolization (first choice)
  • If IR not available/fails: emergency nephrectomy

Stable → Conservative / Selective

  • Serial Hgb q6–8h
  • Bed rest, hold anticoagulants/antiplatelets
  • CTA if Hgb drops significantly
  • IR embolization for active extravasation or expanding hematoma
  • Surgery if embolization fails or unavailable

Interventional Radiology: Angioembolization

  • Selective renal artery embolization — identifies bleeding vessel(s) and occludes with coils, gelfoam, or Onyx
  • Nephron-sparing compared to surgical nephrectomy
  • Success rate 80–95% for hemostasis in renal hemorrhage
  • Post-embolization syndrome (fever, pain, nausea) — expected; manage supportively
  • Risk of post-embolization hypertension (Goldblatt kidney if substantial renal mass embolized)

Surgical Options (if embolization fails)

  • Partial nephrectomy (nephron-sparing) — preferred if tumor-related AML
  • Radical nephrectomy — for extensive hemorrhage, failed embolization, or malignancy
  • This patient has contralateral AML — nephron preservation is especially important

Clinical Pearls

💡Post-ESWL flank pain + dropping hemoglobin = subcapsular hematoma until proven otherwise. CT scan (non-contrast initially; add contrast if hemodynamically stable) is the diagnostic standard. Do not attribute all post-lithotripsy pain to "stone-related colic."
⚠️An expanding hematoma on serial imaging — even without active contrast extravasation — indicates ongoing bleeding and typically requires intervention. The hematoma in this case grew from 14.2 cm to 16.1 cm (in one axis) between 9/14 and 9/16 CT scans, signifying continued oozing despite initial conservative management.
🫀The right renal AML at 6.1 cm is a ticking time bomb. AUA guidelines recommend prophylactic embolization for sporadic AMLs ≥3 cm. This finding should trigger outpatient follow-up with urology for elective embolization after recovery.
🔬Spontaneous renal hematoma should always prompt evaluation for underlying lesion — 57–61% are caused by AML, 26–34% by renal cell carcinoma. CT with and without contrast (or MRI) after hematoma resolution (6–8 weeks) is recommended to characterize the underlying renal parenchyma.
