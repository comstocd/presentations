Adrenal Incidentaloma & COPD Screening

63-year-old male presenting for routine PCP follow-up reveals two important findings: undiagnosed COPD pattern (24 pack-years, daily inhaler use, productive cough) and an adrenal mass discovered incidentally.

Presentation

Chief Complaint

PCP visit for: pinched nerve follow-up, medication refills, and left index finger wart removal.

A 63-year-old male presents for a routine primary care visit. Closer questioning reveals: morning productive cough, exertional dyspnea on stairs, daily albuterol/ipratropium inhaler use (1–2×/week), and 24 pack-year smoking history (quit 3 years ago). He reports the inhaler as prescribed ~5 years ago and denies a formal COPD or asthma diagnosis. He also has low back pain with right-sided sciatica (2/10).

PMH

  • Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus
  • GERD
  • Depression / Anxiety
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Hypertension
  • Low back pain / intermittent right sciatica

Medications

  • Alogliptin + metformin + pioglitazone
  • Pantoprazole
  • Aripiprazole, buspirone, divalproex sodium
  • Clonazepam, hydroxyzine
  • Atorvastatin
  • Lisinopril
  • Albuterol/ipratropium inhaler (PRN)
  • Cortisone injections (Orthopedics)

Social History

Lives with partner. Smokes marijuana 1×/week. Denies alcohol. Quit tobacco 3 years ago (started age 36, 1 PPD = 24 pack-years).

Two Parallel Clinical Questions

This case illustrates how a "routine" PCP visit can surface two significant diagnoses: (1) likely undiagnosed COPD requiring spirometry and appropriate LAMA-based therapy, and (2) an adrenal mass found on imaging that requires systematic evaluation per the Endocrine Society 2016 guideline.

Part I — Undiagnosed COPD

GOLD 2024 Diagnostic Criteria for COPD
  • Post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC <0.70 on spirometry confirms airflow obstruction
  • Chronic respiratory symptoms (cough, sputum, dyspnea)
  • History of exposure to risk factors (smoking >10 pack-years, biomass, occupational)
  • Diagnosis cannot be made by symptoms alone — spirometry is required

This Patient's Pre-Test Probability for COPD

The combination of morning productive cough + exertional dyspnea + 24 pack-years + daily bronchodilator use for 5 years constitutes a high clinical probability of undiagnosed COPD. The prior COPD Epidemiology Estimates (CPED) questionnaire predicts a score >5 = high probability requiring spirometry.

Lung Cancer Screening Eligibility

USPSTF 2021 Recommendation (Grade B)

Annual low-dose CT chest for adults aged 50–80 with a ≥20 pack-year history who currently smoke or quit within the past 15 years. This patient (63 years old, 24 pack-years, quit 3 years ago) qualifies. The PCP appropriately recommended LDCT.

Next Steps for COPD Evaluation

  • Spirometry with post-bronchodilator testing — the only way to confirm COPD
  • If confirmed, GOLD severity classification (GOLD 1–4 by FEV₁% predicted)
  • Symptom burden: mMRC dyspnea scale ≥2 or CAT score ≥10 indicates high-symptom group
  • Initiate LAMA (tiotropium or umeclidinium) as first-line maintenance therapy — superior to LABA alone or SABA-only management per GOLD 2024
  • Albuterol PRN for rescue; educate on proper MDI/DPI technique
  • Smoking cessation reinforcement (already quit — positive)
  • Influenza, RSV, and pneumococcal vaccination

Note on Cortisone Injections

Iatrogenic Adrenal Suppression Risk

This patient receives cortisone injections from Orthopedics. Repeated intra-articular or epidural corticosteroid injections can cause hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis suppression. Document dose, frequency, and timing. This is relevant to adrenal incidentaloma interpretation — exogenous glucocorticoids can cause adrenal atrophy bilaterally and may suppress ACTH, confounding autonomy testing.

Part II — Adrenal Incidentaloma

An adrenal incidentaloma is an adrenal mass ≥1 cm discovered incidentally during imaging performed for another indication. Prevalence increases with age: ~0.2% at age 20, ~7% at age 70 (autopsy series). The two critical questions are: (1) Is it functional? and (2) Is it malignant?

CT Imaging Characteristics (from the Case)

Unenhanced CT attenuation: 5 Hounsfield Units (HU) → <10 HU is diagnostic of a lipid-rich adenoma (benign). Mass 2.8 × 3.5 cm. Homogeneous enhancement; rapid contrast washout >50% at 10 minutes — consistent with adrenal adenoma.

Interpretation: This patient's mass is radiographically benign — <10 HU, <4 cm, rapid washout, homogeneous. However, functional evaluation is still mandatory per Endocrine Society guidelines regardless of imaging appearance.

Functional Evaluation

Endocrine Society 2016: Required Testing for All Adrenal Incidentalomas

Mandatory Screening Panel
  • 1 mg overnight dexamethasone suppression test (DST) — screens for subclinical hypercortisolism (MACS). Give 1 mg dexamethasone at 11 PM; check 8 AM cortisol: abnormal if >1.8 mcg/dL (>50 nmol/L).
  • Plasma free metanephrines (or 24-hour urine metanephrines/catecholamines) — screens for pheochromocytoma. Must be ruled out before any surgical or invasive procedure.
  • Aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR) — only if patient is hypertensive or hypokalemic, to screen for primary aldosteronism.
  • DHEAS — low DHEAS suggests chronic ACTH suppression (useful adjunct in MACS evaluation).

Interpreting the Functional Tests

ConditionKey TestPositive ThresholdAdditional Workup if Positive
MACS / Subclinical Cushing's 1 mg overnight DST Post-DST cortisol >1.8 mcg/dL 24-hr urinary free cortisol, late-night salivary cortisol, ACTH level, 8 mg DST
Pheochromocytoma Plasma free metanephrines >3× ULN = very high probability; 1–3× = intermediate CT/MRI characterization, genetic testing (SDH, VHL, RET mutations)

⚠️ Pheochromocytoma: Rule Out First

This patient is on pioglitazone, aripiprazole, and receives cortisone injections — all of which can affect cortisol testing. More critically, pheochromocytoma must be excluded biochemically before any biopsy or surgery, as manipulation of an unrecognized pheo can precipitate hypertensive crisis, arrhythmia, and death.

Malignancy Risk Assessment

Risk Factors for Adrenocortical Carcinoma (ACC)

  • Size >4 cm (sensitivity ~90% for ACC if >6 cm; less predictive at 4–6 cm)
  • Lipid-poor (HU >10) or heterogeneous appearance
  • Irregular borders, calcification, necrosis
  • Personal or family history of non-adrenal malignancy → suspect metastasis
  • Family history of MEN2, Li-Fraumeni, Lynch, VHL, Carney complex

In This Patient

Mass is 2.8 × 3.5 cm (≤4 cm), HU = 5 (benign), rapid washout, homogeneous — very low probability of malignancy. No known primary malignancy. No family history mentioned.

Endocrine Society 2016: When to Resect

  • Functional tumor (any type except MACS, which may be managed conservatively)
  • CT characteristics suspicious for ACC (size >4 cm; HU >10 with concerning features)
  • Not recommended: Fine-needle biopsy for primary adrenal lesions — cannot distinguish adenoma from ACC histologically; reserved for suspected metastatic disease

Follow-Up Strategy (Benign-Appearing, Non-Functional)

  • Repeat CT adrenal protocol at 6 and 12 months if imaging is indeterminate
  • For clearly benign lesions (<10 HU, <4 cm, no growth): no routine imaging follow-up after initial characterization per updated guidance
  • Repeat hormonal testing annually for 4–5 years (development of hormonal hypersecretion may occur over time)
  • Consider stopping follow-up imaging if stable at 12 months and <4 cm with benign characteristics

Management Summary

COPD Workup

  • ✅ Order spirometry (post-bronchodilator)
  • ✅ Low-dose CT chest (USPSTF lung CA screening)
  • ✅ Consider LAMA initiation (tiotropium)
  • ✅ mMRC and CAT symptom scores
  • ✅ Vaccinations (influenza, PCV15/20, RSV)
  • ✅ Reinforce smoking cessation

Adrenal Incidentaloma

  • ✅ 1 mg overnight DST
  • ✅ Plasma free metanephrines
  • ✅ ARR (patient is hypertensive)
  • ✅ DHEAS
  • ✅ Document cortisone injection history
  • ✅ Follow-up imaging if indeterminate
  • ⚠️ Surgery only if functional or suspicious

Clinical Pearls

💡An HU ≤10 on unenhanced CT is essentially diagnostic of a benign lipid-rich adenoma — the negative predictive value for malignancy is >99%. However, this does not eliminate the need for hormonal testing.
⚠️Pheochromocytoma must always be excluded biochemically before any invasive procedure on an adrenal mass. The diagnosis can be completely silent clinically — up to 50% of pheos are found incidentally.
💡MACS (Mild Autonomous Cortisol Secretion) — formerly "subclinical Cushing's" — is found in ~5–30% of adrenal incidentalomas. It is associated with hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, and osteoporosis even without classic Cushingoid features. This patient's diabetes and HTN make MACS evaluation particularly important.
🫁Albuterol/ipratropium PRN for 5 years in a 24 pack-year smoker with morning productive cough and exertional dyspnea is de facto COPD management without a formal diagnosis. Spirometry is required — not optional — to diagnose, stage, and appropriately treat COPD per GOLD 2024.
💊Pioglitazone (a TZD) in a patient with adrenal incidentaloma and suspected MACS: exogenous glucocorticoids and metabolic syndrome overlap. Pioglitazone can cause fluid retention and is relatively contraindicated in heart failure (NYHA III–IV). Reassess the need for pioglitazone if MACS is confirmed.
