AKI in Liver Failure: Hepatorenal Syndrome

A systematic approach to AKI in the cirrhotic patient — from prerenal azotemia through the diagnosis and management of hepatorenal syndrome (HRS-AKI), including terlipressin, albumin, and liver transplantation.

AKI: Definitions & Classification

KDIGO 2012 AKI Definition

Any of the following within a specified timeframe:

  • Increase in serum creatinine (SCr) ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours
  • Increase in SCr ≥1.5× baseline within 7 days
  • Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for ≥6 hours

Important: AKI is a clinical diagnosis, not a structural one. Parenchymal damage need not be present. AKI can range from asymptomatic lab changes to lethal multiorgan failure.

Prerenal Azotemia: The Foundation

Prerenal azotemia is the most common form of AKI. It reflects inadequate renal plasma flow and reduced intraglomerular hydrostatic pressure — without intrinsic parenchymal damage. The key feature: rapidly reversible upon restoration of renal perfusion.

Mechanisms of Prerenal AKI

  • Hypovolemia (hemorrhage, GI losses, diuretics)
  • Decreased cardiac output (HF, tamponade)
  • NSAIDs → ↓prostaglandin → afferent arteriole constriction
  • ACE-I/ARBs → ↓angiotensin II → efferent arteriole dilation → ↓GFR
  • Renal artery stenosis (bilateral)

Note: FENa is unreliable in cirrhosis and HRS (sodium avid state masquerades as prerenal even with parenchymal injury).

Prolonged Prerenal AKI → Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)

If renal hypoperfusion is not corrected, ischemic tubular cell injury occurs → ATN. The transition from prerenal to ATN is clinically important: ATN requires longer recovery time, is not reversible with simple volume repletion, and in cirrhosis may progress to HRS.

AKI in Cirrhosis: Why It's Different

The Hemodynamic Paradox of Cirrhosis

Cirrhotic patients have a hemodynamic profile that mimics hypovolemia despite total-body volume overload:

  • Portal hypertension → increased nitric oxide production in splanchnic vasculature → splanchnic arterial vasodilation
  • ↓ Effective arterial blood volume (EABV) → activation of RAAS, SNS, and ADH
  • RAAS/SNS activation → sodium/water retention → ascites, edema
  • Renal vasoconstriction → ↓ RBF and GFR → HRS
  • The kidney is structurally normal — the injury is functional and hemodynamic

Hepatorenal Syndrome: Pathophysiology & Diagnosis

ICA (International Club of Ascites) 2015 Diagnostic Criteria for HRS-AKI
  • Cirrhosis with ascites
  • AKI (SCr ↑ ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48h OR ≥50% from baseline within 7 days)
  • No improvement after: 2 days of diuretic withdrawal AND volume expansion with albumin 1 g/kg/day (max 100 g/day)
  • No current or recent nephrotoxic drugs
  • No macroscopic signs of structural kidney injury (no proteinuria >500 mg/day, no hematuria >50 RBC/hpf, normal renal ultrasound)
  • No shock

HRS Phenotypes (Updated 2015 ICA Terminology)

HRS-AKI (formerly Type 1)

  • Rapid progression: SCr doubling to >2.5 mg/dL in <2 weeks
  • Usually precipitated by SBP, variceal bleed, sepsis
  • Median survival without treatment: 2 weeks
  • Treated with vasoconstrictors + albumin

HRS-CKD (formerly Type 2)

  • Slower, progressive renal dysfunction
  • Main manifestation: refractory ascites
  • Median survival ~6 months
  • Bridge to liver transplantation

Evidence-Based Management

Step 1: Identify and Treat Precipitants

  • Rule out SBP: diagnostic paracentesis (PMN ≥250/mm³ = SBP → treat with cefotaxime + albumin)
  • Hold diuretics, NSAIDs, nephrotoxins, aminoglycosides
  • Blood cultures if infection suspected
  • Consider variceal bleeding as precipitant

Step 2: Volume Challenge with Albumin

All patients with cirrhotic AKI should receive IV albumin 1 g/kg/day (max 100 g) for 2 days before labeling HRS. This serves both therapeutic and diagnostic purposes. If SCr does not improve → HRS diagnosis confirmed.

Why albumin? Crystalloids worsen ascites without improving EABV (distributes freely). 25% albumin (human serum albumin) expands intravascular space preferentially and has non-oncotic effects (reduces systemic inflammation, oxidative stress).

Step 4: Definitive Therapy — Liver Transplantation

The definitive treatment of HRS is orthotopic liver transplantation (OLT). Renal function typically recovers post-transplantation as hepatic function is restored. Patients with HRS should be referred urgently for transplant evaluation (UNOS MELD-Na score prioritization).

Albumin in SBP: Prevention of HRS (SORT trial, NEJM 1999)

Albumin 1.5 g/kg at SBP diagnosis + 1 g/kg on day 3 significantly reduced HRS development (10% vs 33%) and 3-month mortality (10% vs 29%) compared to antibiotics alone. This is one of the highest-impact interventions in hepatology.

Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)

  • CRRT or hemodialysis as bridge to OLT — does not treat HRS, only provides support
  • Patients with HRS not on transplant list: RRT rarely beneficial as long-term bridge; goals-of-care discussion critical

Clinical Pearls

💡FENa is unreliable in cirrhosis because the kidney is already in a sodium-avid state (RAAS activated). Even ATN may show FENa <1% in cirrhosis. Use clinical context, response to albumin challenge, and absence of structural kidney injury to diagnose HRS.
🔬Always perform diagnostic paracentesis in any cirrhotic patient admitted with AKI or clinical deterioration — SBP can present without fever, abdominal pain, or leukocytosis. PMN ≥250/mm³ is diagnostic. Empiric albumin + antibiotics while awaiting culture is appropriate given the mortality stakes.
⚠️Avoid nephrotoxins aggressively in cirrhosis: NSAIDs (afferent vasoconstriction), aminoglycosides (tubular toxicity), IV contrast (use N-acetylcysteine and minimize volume), and ACE-I/ARBs (reduce efferent tone needed to maintain GFR). These patients have almost no reserve to compensate.
💊Terlipressin (FDA-approved September 2022 in the USA as Terlivaz) is now the preferred first-line vasoconstrictor for HRS-AKI based on the CONFIRM trial. Key contraindications include respiratory failure (worsens hypoxia by pulmonary vasoconstriction), known coronary artery disease, and peripheral vascular disease.
🏥MELD-Na score drives organ allocation in the USA. HRS-AKI dramatically increases MELD-Na — a patient going from baseline MELD 15 to creatinine 2.5+ may suddenly have MELD 25–30, significantly accelerating their transplant timeline. Early transplant team involvement is critical.
