Air Embolism: Venous & Arterial

An uncommon but potentially catastrophic event resulting from air entry into the vasculature. Occurs most commonly in the setting of surgery, trauma, vascular interventions, and barotrauma. Requires both a communication between air and vasculature and a pressure gradient favoring air entry.

Definition & Mechanisms

Definition

Air embolism is an uncommon but potentially catastrophic event that occurs as a consequence of the entry of air into the vasculature. It requires two conditions to be met simultaneously:

  • Direct communication between a source of air and the vasculature
  • A pressure gradient favoring the passage of air into the circulation

Common Causes

CategoryExamples
SurgeryNeurosurgery (sitting position), cardiac surgery, laparoscopy, orthopedics, ENT
Vascular interventionsCentral line placement/removal, PICC, dialysis catheter, IV infusion, endoscopy
TraumaPenetrating chest or neck injury, blunt trauma with lung laceration
BarotraumaMechanical ventilation (especially with high pressures), diving (decompression sickness)
ObstetricDelivery, caesarean section, orogenital sex during pregnancy

Two Types: Venous vs. Arterial

  • Venous air embolism (VAE): Air enters the venous circulation, travels to the right heart, and lodges in the pulmonary circulation — causing obstructive shock and right heart failure
  • Arterial air embolism (AAE): Results from (1) incomplete filtration of venous air by the lung, (2) direct introduction of air into the arterial system, or (3) paradoxical embolization through a patent foramen ovale (PFO) or ASD

Volume Matters

Small amounts of air (<50 mL) are generally absorbed and clinically silent. Large volumes (>200–300 mL) entering rapidly can be immediately fatal by causing air lock in the right ventricle, preventing ejection. The rate of entry is as important as the total volume.

Venous Air Embolism

Clinical Spectrum

SeverityFeatures
MinorCommon; minimal or no symptoms; transient and self-resolving
Moderate-SevereDyspnea (almost universal); substernal chest pain; lightheadedness; dizziness
Life-ThreateningAcute right-sided heart failure; sense of impending doom; sudden loss of consciousness; hemodynamic collapse (obstructive shock); cardiac arrest

Signs & Symptoms

  • Gasp, cough, or sucking noise at entry site
  • Mill wheel murmur — a churning, machinery-like murmur heard over the precordium; classic but not always present
  • Tachypnea, tachycardia, or bradycardia
  • Hypotension, wheezing, crackles
  • Elevated jugular venous pressure (JVP)
  • Hypoxemic respiratory failure

Pathophysiology

Air in the right heart creates mechanical obstruction (air lock) preventing right ventricular ejection → acute RV failure → reduced left ventricular preload → obstructive shock. Simultaneously, air in the pulmonary vasculature increases dead space and causes V/Q mismatch → hypoxemia. Paradoxical embolism to the coronary arteries can cause myocardial ischemia.

Arterial Air Embolism

Clinical Presentation

Varies by organ affected. The brain is most commonly involved.

  • Neurological: Acute altered mental status, focal neurological deficits, stroke-like presentation; in severe cases, loss of consciousness, coma, cardiac arrest
  • Cardiac: Chest pain, arrhythmia, myocardial ischemia (coronary air embolism)
  • Cutaneous: Livedo reticularis; crepitus over superficial vessels; bubbles visible within retinal arteries on fundoscopy
  • General: Wheeze from pulmonary involvement

Key Distinction: VAE vs. AAE Positioning

Positioning differs critically between venous and arterial air embolism. Getting this wrong can worsen the clinical situation. See the Positioning section below.

Differential Diagnosis

There are no pathognomonic features of air embolism that distinguish it from other causes of acute decompensation other than the demonstration of air on imaging. All causes of acute pulmonary, cardiovascular, or neurological decompensation must be considered:

SystemDifferential Diagnoses
PulmonaryPulmonary embolism (thrombotic), tension pneumothorax, massive hemoptysis, acute severe asthma, ARDS
CardiovascularAcute MI, cardiac tamponade, aortic dissection, acute right heart failure from other cause, dysrhythmia
NeurologicalIschemic stroke, hemorrhagic stroke, hypertensive encephalopathy, seizure, hypoglycemia
Other embolismFat embolism (long bone fracture), amniotic fluid embolism, septic embolism, cholesterol embolism

Imaging to Confirm

Air embolism can be confirmed by demonstrating intravascular air on imaging. However, because air is rapidly absorbed from the circulation, air embolism is typically a retrospective clinical diagnosis based on high index of suspicion and exclusion of other life-threatening processes. Do not delay treatment while awaiting imaging confirmation in a deteriorating patient.

Initial Supportive Therapy

Immediate Assessment: ABC

  • Airway: Assess stability; intubate early if there is any concern for airway compromise
  • Breathing: Supplemental oxygen; mechanical ventilation if needed
  • Circulation: Volume resuscitation, vasopressors, ACLS if cardiac arrest
High-Flow Oxygen: A Critical First Step

Administer 100% FiO2 immediately via non-rebreather mask or mechanical ventilation. This serves two purposes:

  • Treats hypoxemia from V/Q mismatch and right heart failure
  • Promotes air absorption: By denitrogenating the blood (washing out nitrogen), high FiO2 creates a large diffusion gradient that accelerates reabsorption of trapped air from the vasculature

Start with the highest tolerable FiO2 to achieve the highest possible oxygen saturation.

Stop the Source

Immediately identify and eliminate the air entry source: clamp or remove the offending catheter, pack the wound, flood the surgical field with saline, or remove the needle. This is the single most important initial intervention.

Positioning

Positioning Differs by Type — This Is a High-Stakes Decision

Venous Air Embolism

Left lateral decubitus (Durant maneuver) + head-down (Trendelenburg)

  • Left lateral decubitus: traps air in the right atrium, away from the tricuspid valve and pulmonary outflow tract, preventing air lock at the right ventricular outflow
  • Trendelenburg: increases venous pressure in the upper body, slowing further air entry; promotes air movement away from pulmonary outflow
  • Alternative: left lateral decubitus alone, or Trendelenburg alone
  • Do NOT leave the patient supine — supine position allows air to accumulate at the RV outflow tract
Arterial Air Embolism

Supine position

  • Head-down positions are contraindicated in arterial embolism — they promote migration of air to the brain
  • Supine position minimizes cerebral air migration
  • If HBO is available, transfer immediately (see Definitive Therapy)

Act Before Imaging When Unstable

Air is rapidly absorbed from the circulation. In a deteriorating patient, do not delay positioning, high-flow O2, and source control while waiting for imaging confirmation. The diagnosis is clinical in the acute setting. Imaging confirms and characterizes once the patient is stabilized.

Definitive Therapy

Hyperbaric Oxygen (HBO)

HBO: Indications & Timing
  • HBO is not routinely administered for minor air embolism
  • When available, HBO should be given to patients with:
    • Hemodynamic or cardiopulmonary compromise
    • Neurological deficits
    • Other evidence of end-organ damage
  • Timing: Administer within the first 4–6 hours after symptom onset for best outcomes
  • Continued benefit reported up to 30 hours after symptom onset in published case reports, though this is not established by controlled trials
  • Mechanism: HBO compresses residual air bubbles by Boyle's Law; markedly accelerates nitrogen reabsorption; treats cerebral and systemic hypoxia
  • Weigh benefit vs. risk of transport in unstable patients; stabilize before transferring

Aspiration of Air (Central Venous Catheter)

In patients with a pre-existing central venous catheter positioned at the right atrium/SVC junction, aspiration of air directly from the right ventricle may be attempted. This is a last resort in extremis — the catheter tip must be in the correct position, and real-time guidance (fluoroscopy or echo) improves success.

Closed-Chest Cardiac Massage

Chest compressions during CPR can help mechanically disrupt large air bubbles in the right heart, breaking them into smaller emboli that can be absorbed. This is a last resort measure in cardiac arrest secondary to air embolism, used alongside standard ACLS.

Clinical Pearls

The mill wheel murmur is highly characteristic but frequently absent. A churning, machinery-like precordial murmur caused by air in the right heart chambers — when present, it is strongly suggestive of VAE, but it is heard in only a minority of confirmed cases in clinical practice. Its absence does not exclude VAE. Precordial Doppler is far more sensitive and is used intraoperatively in high-risk cases (sitting neurosurgery).
Central line removal is as dangerous as insertion. VAE is a well-recognized complication of both CVC placement AND removal. On removal, occlude the insertion site immediately; have the patient perform Valsalva (or place on ventilator with positive pressure) to increase venous pressure at the moment of catheter withdrawal.
PFO transforms VAE into AAE. Approximately 25–30% of the population has a PFO. In VAE with increased right atrial pressure, a right-to-left shunt can develop, allowing venous air to bypass the pulmonary filter and reach the arterial circulation. This is paradoxical embolism — screen with bubble echocardiography.
Air is rapidly absorbed — don't wait for imaging in a crashing patient. The clinical window to image intravascular air is narrow. Initiate positioning, 100% O2, and source control based on clinical suspicion. Imaging confirms but should not delay treatment.
HBO improves outcomes in arterial air embolism even when delayed. While optimal results are seen within 4–6 hours, published case reports document benefit when treatment is delayed beyond that window. The evidence base is observational and largely case-based; if HBO is unavailable locally, consider transfer after stabilization rather than withholding treatment.
