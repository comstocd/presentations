RG Vascular Code: Chronic Limb-Threatening Ischemia, DKA, and a Code Blue

65-year-old male admitted for DKA with glucose 873 and metabolic encephalopathy, found to have chronic limb-threatening ischemia of the left lower extremity, ultimately requiring below-knee amputation. Died intraoperatively from cardiac arrest with suspected PE. Case raises critical questions about anticoagulation choice, code status, thrombolytic selection, and system delays in resuscitation.

Suspected Cause of Death: Massive PE or Obstructive Shock

The intraoperative course — acute cardiovascular collapse after limb surgery, difficulty ventilating, and rapid hemodynamic failure — is consistent with massive PE or catastrophic obstructive shock. The patient had multiple PE risk factors: known prior PE/DVT, popliteal DVT, immobility, malignant obesity, postoperative state, and cancer of anticoagulation management decisions throughout the admission.

Peripheral Artery Disease & Chronic Limb-Threatening Ischemia

This Patient's Disease Burden

  • Duplex findings: (1) aortoiliac inflow disease, (2) total occlusion of left SFA and left popliteal artery, (3) severe bilateral tibial disease
  • Clinically: absent pedal and popliteal pulses bilaterally; 2+ femoral pulses bilaterally; left foot cooler, mottled, ultimately necrotic heel
  • No ABI obtainable (pain prevented BP cuff tolerance)
  • MRI: extensive myositis of uncertain etiology — raising question of ischemic myositis vs. infectious myositis vs. rhabdomyolysis-related

PAD Overview

Clinical Spectrum of PAD (ACC/AHA 2005 Distribution in Patients ≥50)
  • Asymptomatic: 20–50%
  • Atypical leg pain: 40–50%
  • Classic claudication: 10–35%
  • Threatened limb (chronic limb-threatening ischemia): 1–2%

Risk factors: older age, smoking, HTN, male sex, known atherosclerosis elsewhere, family history, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, homocysteinemia. This patient carried virtually every major risk factor.

Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI)

  • ABI ≤0.90 = diagnostic of PAD
  • ABI <0.40 = severe disease / rest pain territory
  • ABI >1.40 = non-compressible vessels (calcified arteries — common in diabetics; may require toe-brachial index instead)
  • Unable to obtain here due to severe pain — duplex arterial study used instead

Why Mechanical Thrombectomy Was Not an Option for Popliteal DVT

  • Popliteal vein is posterior and difficult to access, increasing procedural risk
  • Popliteal DVT clots are smaller and more distal — thrombectomy less effective vs. proximal (iliofemoral) clots
  • Anticoagulation is preferred for popliteal and below-knee DVT — less invasive, lower complication risk
  • Catheter-directed thrombolysis remains an option for proximal (iliofemoral) DVT with high thrombus burden

Chronic Limb-Threatening Ischemia (CLTI): Key Principles

CLTI is associated with high amputation risk and mortality. Treatment is based on effective revascularization to relieve pain, heal wounds, and preserve limb function. In this patient, multiple factors precluded standard revascularization: morbid obesity, recent AKI precluding contrast, COPD/hypoxemia, recent rhabdomyolysis, and his being a poor surgical candidate overall. When revascularization is not feasible, amputation becomes the limb-sparing (and ultimately life-sparing) option — but must be weighed against perioperative risk, which in this patient was extremely high.

ACC/AHA Recommendation: UFH for Acute Limb Ischemia

The ACC/AHA guidelines recommend unfractionated heparin for acute limb ischemia due to its rapid onset, titrability, and complete reversibility with protamine — critical given the perioperative setting and the need to hold anticoagulation around surgery. DOACs are not specifically recommended for acute ischemic limb and are harder to reverse rapidly. For chronic PAD management, antiplatelet therapy (aspirin or clopidogrel) remains the mainstay; anticoagulants are added only for concurrent AF, VTE, or high-risk PAD (COMPASS criteria).

The Code: Suspected PE During BKA

Intraoperative Arrest

During transfer from the OR table to the bed following BKA completion, the patient developed acute hypotension and had no palpable pulse. CPR was initiated. IV epinephrine was administered. Brief ROSC achieved, but hemodynamic instability recurred. The patient became progressively difficult to ventilate with declining saturations — a clinical picture highly consistent with massive PE causing obstructive shock and acute cor pulmonale.

Why Suspect PE?

  • Known prior PE (on anticoagulation at home)
  • Active popliteal DVT (confirmed 10/10)
  • Prolonged immobility (>4 weeks bedridden before admission, then extended inpatient stay)
  • Morbid obesity — major VTE risk factor
  • Perioperative state — highest VTE risk period
  • Anticoagulation had been held pre-operatively (standard for surgical management)
  • Sudden cardiovascular collapse immediately post-op; difficulty ventilating (RV failure → reduced LV preload → reduced CO)

The Teaching Question: Number One Risk Factor for PE

Q: What is the #1 risk factor for pulmonary embolism?

A: Prior pulmonary embolism.

A history of VTE (PE or DVT) confers the highest individual risk for recurrent PE. This patient had documented prior PE/DVT and was on anticoagulation for it — yet the perioperative period (anticoagulation held) combined with active DVT and immobility created the perfect storm for recurrent, fatal PE. VTE is the third most common life-threatening cardiovascular disease in the United States.

Thrombolytics: Alteplase vs. Tenecteplase for Massive PE

Alteplase (tPA)

Alteplase: Overview
  • Recombinant tissue plasminogen activator (tPA)
  • Converts plasminogen to plasmin → lyses fibrin
  • Half-life: initial <5 min; terminal 72 min
  • Cleared by liver
Alteplase: Dosing for Massive PE
  • FDA-approved dose: 100 mg IV over 2 hours
  • Start heparin after infusion when aPTT ≤2× normal
  • Available as lyophilized powder in 50 mg and 100 mg vials
  • Reconstituted in sterile water; compatible with NS and D5W at 1 mg/mL
  • Stable 8 hours at room temperature

Evidence: TNK vs. Alteplase in PE

TriNetX Retrospective Cohort (2024)
  • TNK-treated: 266 cases (29 organizations); Alteplase-treated: 22,864 cases
  • Propensity-matched to 283 patients per cohort
  • 30-day mortality: TNK 19.4% vs. alteplase 19.8% (RR 0.982; 95% CI 0.704–1.371) — no significant difference
  • Intracranial hemorrhage and transfusion rates too infrequent to analyze
  • Conclusion: TNK exhibits similar mortality to alteplase in PE with hemodynamic instability; prospective evaluation needed
PEITHO Trial: TNK in Submassive PE
  • TNK vs. placebo in acute PE with RV dysfunction (submassive)
  • TNK improved hemodynamics but at increased risk of intracranial hemorrhage: 6.3% vs. 1.2% with placebo
  • No difference in long-term functional outcomes
  • Key lesson: thrombolytics in submassive PE carry significant ICH risk — reserve for massive PE (hemodynamic instability) unless benefit clearly outweighs risk

Bottom Line on Thrombolytic Choice

For massive PE in cardiac arrest, current AHA/ESC guidelines support systemic thrombolysis with alteplase (100 mg over 2 hours). Tenecteplase is emerging as a practical alternative given its single bolus administration, similar efficacy, and lower cost — particularly advantageous during active resuscitation when setting up a 2-hour infusion is logistically challenging. Both agents carry similar bleeding risk. TNK is not yet FDA-approved for PE but off-label use is increasing.

System Issue: Delay in Thrombolytic Administration

What Happened

There was an estimated 15-minute delay in administering thrombolytics during this code blue, attributed to: multiple pharmacy calls without orders in place, multiple conflicting orders in the system, and time required for pharmacy to prepare the drug. In cardiac arrest, every minute without ROSC reduces survival by approximately 10%. A 15-minute delay is catastrophic.

Why Tenecteplase Might Have Reduced This Delay

Alteplase for PE requires a 100 mg vial reconstituted in sterile water and infused over 2 hours — a multi-step preparation that takes time under normal circumstances and even longer in emergency conditions. Tenecteplase, by contrast, is a single weight-based IV bolus over 5 seconds, requiring only one vial reconstitution. In active CPR, this difference in preparation time may be clinically meaningful.

Systems-Level Improvements

  • Pre-written code blue thrombolytic order sets that can be activated with a single order (activating all downstream pharmacy preparation steps simultaneously)
  • Pharmacy code bags pre-stocked with thrombolytics for rapid deployment, analogous to crash carts
  • Clear institutional protocol for massive PE / PEA arrest: which thrombolytic, what dose, who orders, who prepares, who administers, and what the trigger criteria are
  • Simulation training for code teams on PE arrest management, including thrombolytic administration workflow
  • Consider TNK as the institutional default for PE arrest — simpler preparation reduces delay

Alteplase: Team-Based Prescribing

Alteplase requires careful coordination across prescriber, pharmacist, and nurse. The pharmacist performs reconstitution and dose verification; the nurse administers and monitors for bleeding; the prescriber must be immediately available for any concerns. The complexity of this workflow is a patient safety vulnerability in cardiac arrest settings. Interprofessional simulation and pre-authorized standing orders are the mitigation strategies.

Code Status & Goals of Care

Key Facts from This Case

  • eMOLST established as of 10/6/2024
  • Multiple family members aware of patient wishes
  • Code status changed at some point during the admission (noted in the case)
  • Family delivered word during active CPR to withhold further resuscitation — CPR was stopped; patient expired 12:04 PM

Teaching Points

  • Goals of care conversations must happen before the OR. This patient had an eMOLST but underwent major surgery (BKA). The code status and resuscitation preferences in the perioperative setting should have been explicitly re-confirmed with the patient and family before the procedure, documented in the anesthesia and surgical consent.
  • Perioperative DNR/DNI is a distinct conversation from general code status. Many institutions require specific documentation if a patient with DNR wishes to have that order suspended during anesthesia (standard practice) or maintained (rare but possible).
  • Family communication during a code is extraordinarily difficult. The information they receive, the time pressure, and the emotional state all compromise decision-making. Having clear documented wishes before the procedure reduces the burden on the family and the team.
  • NYS eMOLST: A legally valid medical order that travels with the patient across care settings. It supersedes a verbal code status. If an eMOLST is in place, it should be immediately visible in the chart and present in the OR.

Clinical Pearls

Prior PE is the #1 risk factor for recurrent PE. Every patient with known VTE on anticoagulation who undergoes a procedure requiring anticoagulation interruption needs an explicit bridging plan. This patient had active DVT, was morbidly obese, and had a prior PE — each independently increasing his perioperative PE risk. The decision to hold anticoagulation before BKA without a bridging strategy in a patient with active popliteal DVT and prior PE deserves scrutiny.
UFH is the correct anticoagulant for acute limb ischemia, not apixaban or rivaroxaban. DOACs have no established role in acute ischemic limb management. UFH is rapidly titratable, fully reversible with protamine, and the ACC/AHA-endorsed agent. The switch from Eliquis to Xarelto citing the COMPASS trial was a misapplication of evidence — COMPASS studied chronic PAD prevention (rivaroxaban 2.5 mg BID + aspirin), not acute ischemic limb management.
DKA and HHS frequently coexist in T2DM. Glucose >600, osmolality >320, but also pH <7.30 and ketosis = mixed picture. Management priorities: aggressive IV fluids first, insulin after K&sup+; is ≥3.5 mEq/L, identify the precipitant. In this patient, medication noncompliance drove a 4–6 week deterioration before admission.
Thrombolytics in massive PE: don't let logistics kill the patient. The 15-minute delay in thrombolytic administration highlights a systems failure, not an individual failure. Institutions should have pre-approved standing order sets, pharmacy code bags with thrombolytics, and simulation-trained teams. Tenecteplase's single-bolus administration may offer a practical advantage during active resuscitation.
Code status must be re-addressed before surgery in high-risk patients. An eMOLST established during a hospitalization should be explicitly reviewed before any major procedure. The goals of care in the perioperative context — including what resuscitation measures are acceptable if the patient arrests on the table — must be documented and communicated to the entire OR team before the first incision.
Morbid obesity dramatically increases perioperative risk. This patient's body habitus (BMI ~49) complicated nearly every aspect of his care: limited lung auscultation, inability to obtain ABI, contrast risk from AKI, difficulty with vascular access and monitoring, high surgical risk, and impaired pharmacokinetics of all anticoagulants. Weight-based dosing of UFH (80 units/kg bolus, 18 units/kg/hr) and anti-Xa monitoring are essential in morbid obesity.
