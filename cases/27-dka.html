Diabetic Ketoacidosis

DKA is an acute, life-threatening metabolic emergency defined by the triad of hyperglycemia, high anion gap metabolic acidosis, and ketonemia. It occurs predominantly in Type 1 DM but is increasingly seen in Type 2. Mortality is Mortality is <1% with appropriate management but riseslt;1% in uncomplicated adult DKA with timely management but rises sharply with delayed recognition, cerebral edema (predominantly a pediatric complication), or missed precipitant. The protocol is precise: fluids first, insulin after potassium is safe, and never stop insulin before starting dextrose.

The Triad to Memorize

Hyperglycemia + High anion gap metabolic acidosis + Ketonemia/ketonuria. Note: glucose can be <200 mg/dL in euglycemic DKA (SGLT2 inhibitor use, starvation, pregnancy, alcohol). Never exclude DKA solely on the basis of a normal glucose in the right clinical context.

Pathophysiology

DKA results from absolute or relative insulin deficiency combined with excess counter-regulatory hormones (glucagon, cortisol, catecholamines, growth hormone). This creates a perfect metabolic storm:

ProcessMechanismResult
HyperglycemiaDecreased glucose uptake (no insulin); increased hepatic gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis (glucagon excess)Osmotic diuresis → polyuria, polydipsia, dehydration, electrolyte wasting
KetogenesisWithout insulin, adipose tissue undergoes uncontrolled lipolysis → free fatty acids flood the liver → beta-oxidation → acetyl-CoA accumulation → ketone body synthesis (β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate, acetone)High anion gap metabolic acidosis; fruity breath (acetone)
Counter-regulatory excessGlucagon activates hepatic ketogenesis and gluconeogenesis; cortisol and catecholamines amplify lipolysis and insulin resistanceAmplifies both hyperglycemia and ketosis; explains why DKA persists even when glucose starts falling with treatment
Osmotic diuresisGlycosuria draws water and electrolytes (Na, K, Cl, Mg, PO4) into urineTotal body water deficit 3–6 L; total body K deficit 3–5 mEq/kg despite potentially elevated serum K
Transcellular K shiftAcidosis drives K out of cells (H+ in, K+ out); insulin deficiency prevents cellular K uptakeSerum K may be normal or high despite massive total body K depletion; serum K will fall precipitously with insulin and fluid therapy

Why Potassium Is the Most Dangerous Electrolyte in DKA

Total body potassium is depleted in all patients with DKA from osmotic diuresis. However, serum K may be normal or even elevated due to transcellular shift from acidosis. When insulin is given, K shifts back into cells rapidly. If serum K is already low before insulin starts, insulin will drive it into fatal hypokalemia (<2.5 mEq/L) causing cardiac arrhythmia. This is why the protocol requires: check K first, replace to ≥3.5 mEq/L before starting insulin, and replace aggressively throughout.

Always Find the Precipitant

Treating the DKA without identifying and treating the precipitant leads to recurrence. Order blood cultures, urinalysis, chest X-ray, EKG, and troponin in every DKA patient. In patients with abdominal pain, remember that DKA itself causes abdominal pain (from gastric dysmotility and acidosis) — but if abdominal pain is severe or does not improve with DKA treatment, pursue further evaluation for a surgical abdomen.

Clinical Presentation

Symptoms
  • Polyuria, polydipsia (osmotic diuresis)
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • Generalized weakness, fatigue
  • Weight loss (catabolism)
  • Altered mental status (mild to coma in severe DKA)
  • Symptoms of precipitant (cough, dysuria, chest pain)
Signs
  • Kussmaul respirations — deep, rapid, sighing breaths; respiratory compensation for metabolic acidosis
  • Fruity breath — acetone (not all clinicians can detect this)
  • Tachycardia, hypotension (volume depletion)
  • Dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor
  • Fever (if infection) or hypothermia (severe acidosis)
  • Diffuse abdominal tenderness (DKA-related vs. surgical)
  • Decreased reflexes (hypokalemia)

Fluid Resuscitation

Fluids First — Before Insulin

Volume resuscitation is the first and most urgent intervention in DKA. Fluids alone lower glucose (dilution, improved renal clearance), improve perfusion, and correct acidosis. Starting insulin before adequate volume resuscitation worsens hypokalemia and may impair cerebral perfusion. In severe DKA, initial fluid resuscitation takes priority even before the first insulin dose.

ADA Fluid Protocol
  1. Initial bolus: 1 L of 0.9% NS (normal saline) over the first hour. In hemodynamic compromise, give more rapidly.
  2. Subsequent fluids based on corrected sodium and hemodynamic status:
    • Corrected Na low or normal: 0.9% NS at 250–500 mL/hr
    • Corrected Na high: 0.45% NS (half-normal saline) at 250–500 mL/hr
  3. When glucose reaches 200 mg/dL: Switch to D5W + 0.45% NS (or D5 + 0.9% NS) at 150–250 mL/hr to prevent hypoglycemia while continuing insulin to clear ketones
  4. Total fluid deficit in DKA is typically 3–6 L; replace over 24–48 hours; avoid over-resuscitation causing cerebral edema or pulmonary edema

NS vs. Balanced Crystalloids in DKA: TOPS & FLUID Trials

Emerging evidence (TOPS trial, 2021) suggests balanced crystalloids (PlasmaLyte, LR) may resolve DKA faster than NS, with less hyperchloremic acidosis and fewer electrolyte disturbances. The ADA currently recommends NS as the standard but acknowledges balanced crystalloids are a reasonable alternative. Large volumes of NS cause hyperchloremic non-anion gap metabolic acidosis, which can complicate interpretation of resolution criteria. Consider switching to balanced crystalloid after initial resuscitation if hyperchloremia is a concern.

Potassium Replacement

The Most Critical Safety Step in DKA Management

Check serum potassium before starting insulin. If K <3.5 mEq/L, hold insulin and replace potassium first. Giving insulin with hypokalemia can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmia. This rule has no exceptions.

Potassium Replacement Protocol
Serum K (mEq/L)Action
<3.5Hold insulin. Replace K at 40 mEq/hr IV until K ≥3.5. Recheck K q1–2h. Start insulin only when K ≥3.5.
3.5–5.0Start insulin. Add 20–40 mEq K per liter of IV fluid. Recheck K q2–4h.
>5.0Start insulin. Hold IV potassium supplementation. Recheck K q2h; begin replacing when <5.0.

Use KCl (potassium chloride) as the primary replacement salt. Potassium phosphate can be used for a portion of replacement if phosphate is also low (replaces both simultaneously). Maximum safe IV KCl rate via peripheral IV: 10–20 mEq/hr. Rates >20 mEq/hr require central access and continuous cardiac monitoring.

Insulin Protocol

Standard IV Insulin Infusion Protocol

ADA Insulin Infusion Protocol
  1. Confirm K ≥3.5 mEq/L before starting insulin (see above).
  2. Optional bolus: Regular insulin 0.1 units/kg IV push. Many institutions omit the bolus (no demonstrated benefit and increases hypoglycemia/hypokalemia risk); not recommended in children.
  3. Infusion: Regular insulin 0.1 units/kg/hr IV continuous infusion. Use ideal body weight in obese patients.
  4. Target glucose fall: 50–75 mg/dL/hr. If glucose does not fall by ≥50 mg/dL in the first hour, double the infusion rate.
  5. When glucose reaches 200 mg/dL:
    • Reduce insulin infusion to 0.02–0.05 units/kg/hr
    • Add dextrose to IV fluids (D5W + 0.45% NS) to maintain glucose 150–200 mg/dL
    • Do not stop insulin until DKA is resolved — ketosis requires ongoing insulin even after glucose normalizes

Never Stop Insulin Just Because Glucose Is Normal

This is the most common DKA management error. Insulin is required to suppress ketogenesis — not just to lower glucose. When glucose reaches 200 mg/dL, the correct action is to add dextrose to the IV fluids AND reduce (not stop) the insulin infusion. Stopping insulin prematurely allows ketone production to resume, prolonging DKA. The insulin infusion should continue until the anion gap is closed and pH is ≥7.30, regardless of the glucose level.

Subcutaneous Insulin as an Alternative (Mild-Moderate DKA)

For mild-to-moderate DKA (pH ≥7.00, HCO3 ≥10, alert patient tolerating fluids orally), rapid-acting subcutaneous insulin (lispro or aspart) given every 1–2 hours has been shown comparable to IV insulin infusion in selected single-center studies and may allow management in a step-down unit. Evidence is based on small trials (Umpierrez 2004) and should be applied with clinical judgment. Not appropriate for severe DKA, altered mental status, hemodynamic instability, or inability to tolerate oral fluids.

Bicarbonate: When & When Not

Bicarbonate Is Not Routinely Recommended in DKA

The ADA does not recommend routine bicarbonate administration in DKA. Insulin and fluids correct the acidosis by stopping ketone production and allowing bicarbonate to regenerate. Exogenous bicarbonate may paradoxically worsen intracellular and CNS acidosis (CO2 crosses BBB faster than HCO3), increase hypokalemia, slow ketone clearance, and cause metabolic alkalosis overshoot.

Bicarbonate: The Only Accepted Indication
  • Severe life-threatening acidemia: pH <6.9
  • Hyperkalemia with EKG changes (bicarbonate shifts K into cells as temporizing measure)
  • Hemodynamic instability attributed to severe acidosis (catecholamine resistance)

Dose if used: Sodium bicarbonate 100 mEq in 400 mL sterile water + 20 mEq KCl over 2 hours. Repeat pH assessment in 2 hours. Stop when pH ≥7.0.

Note: Evidence for bicarbonate even in pH <6.9 is limited; it remains controversial. Some experts recommend a single dose with close monitoring rather than repeated administration.

Phosphate

Total body phosphate is depleted in DKA from osmotic diuresis, but serum phosphate is usually normal or elevated on presentation due to transcellular shift (acidosis, catabolism). With insulin therapy and volume resuscitation, phosphate shifts back into cells, and serum levels fall.

  • Routine phosphate replacement is not recommended (clinical trials show no outcome benefit)
  • Replace if: Serum phosphate <1.0 mg/dL with symptoms (muscle weakness, hemolytic anemia, respiratory failure), or in patients with cardiac dysfunction, anemia, or respiratory compromise where hypophosphatemia may cause clinical deterioration
  • Dose: Potassium phosphate 20–40 mmol IV over 4–12 hours (replaces both K and PO4 simultaneously — account for K contribution in total K replacement calculations)
  • Avoid aggressive phosphate replacement in renal failure (hyperphosphatemia risk)

Resolution Criteria & Transition to SQ Insulin

DKA Resolution Criteria (All Three Required)
  • Blood glucose <200 mg/dL
  • Serum bicarbonate ≥15 mEq/L
  • Venous pH ≥7.30
  • And/or: Anion gap ≤12 mEq/L (some guidelines add this as a fourth criterion)

Glucose normalizes first; pH and anion gap closure lag. Do not transition to SQ insulin based on glucose alone — wait for biochemical resolution of ketoacidosis.

Transition to Subcutaneous Insulin

  1. Patient must be able to eat and drink
  2. DKA resolution criteria met (above)
  3. Give first SQ dose of long-acting insulin (glargine or detemir) 2 hours before stopping the insulin infusion — this "overlap" prevents rebound ketosis from the gap between stopping IV insulin (half-life ~5 minutes) and onset of SQ basal insulin (~2 hours)
  4. For known diabetic: resume their home insulin regimen; adjust doses based on this admission
  5. For new-onset diabetes: consult endocrinology; initiate basal-bolus insulin regimen; provide diabetes education before discharge
  6. Discharge planning: identify and address precipitant; ensure insulin supply and supplies; outpatient follow-up within 1 week

The 2-Hour Overlap Rule

Stopping the IV insulin infusion without giving SQ insulin first leads to rebound hyperglycemia and recurrent ketosis within 1–2 hours. Glargine or detemir must be administered at least 1–2 hours before the infusion is stopped. This is a protocol step that is frequently missed on overnight transitions — document it explicitly in orders and the care plan.

Cerebral Edema

The Most Feared Complication of DKA Treatment

Cerebral edema is rare and occurs predominantly in children and adolescents; it is uncommon in adults with DKA. In pediatric series, incidence is approximately 0.5–1% of DKA episodes, with mortality reported at 20–40% in modern case series (older literature cited higher rates). It typically develops 4–12 hours after DKA treatment is initiated, not at presentation. Risk is associated with aggressive early fluid and insulin therapy, though the precise mechanism remains debated.

Risk Factors

  • Age <20 years (especially young children)
  • New-onset diabetes presenting in DKA
  • Severe DKA (low pH, high BUN at presentation)
  • Rapid glucose fall (>100 mg/dL/hr)
  • Large, rapid fluid resuscitation (especially hypotonic fluids)
  • Bicarbonate administration
  • Failure of serum Na to rise as glucose falls (suggests osmotic shift into CNS)

Recognition & Management

FeatureDetail
Warning signsSudden worsening headache; change in mental status; pupil changes; bradycardia with hypertension (Cushing's triad); incontinence; declining level of consciousness despite improving labs
Immediate managementReduce IV fluid rate; elevate head of bed 30°; mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg IV over 20 minutes (first-line); hypertonic saline (3% NaCl 5–10 mL/kg over 30 min) as alternative or adjunct
Do notDo not delay treatment for CT scan if clinical picture is consistent; CT may be normal early in cerebral edema
DispositionICU; neurosurgery consultation; ICP monitoring in refractory cases

Prevention

  • Target glucose fall of 50–75 mg/dL/hr (not faster)
  • Monitor neurological status hourly during treatment
  • Ensure serum Na rises appropriately as glucose falls (corrected Na should increase by ~1.6 mEq for every 100 mg/dL fall in glucose)
  • Avoid hypotonic fluids in the first 4–6 hours
  • Avoid bicarbonate unless pH <6.9

Euglycemic DKA

A Diagnosis That Is Frequently Missed

Euglycemic DKA (euDKA) is defined as DKA with glucose <250 mg/dL. It meets all other DKA criteria (HAGMA, ketonemia) but presents with near-normal glucose, leading to delayed or missed diagnosis. The glucose is low because ketone production occurs without adequate glucose production, or because glucose has been lowered by prior insulin, caloric restriction, or renal clearance.

SGLT2 Inhibitors: Perioperative & Illness Protocol

SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozin, ertugliflozin) must be held 3–4 days before elective surgery and during any acute illness, prolonged NPO status, or hospital admission. Continuing them perioperatively or during critical illness is associated with euglycemic DKA. Any patient on an SGLT2 inhibitor who presents with HAGMA and ketonemia has euglycemic DKA until proven otherwise, regardless of their glucose level.

Clinical Pearls

Check potassium before insulin, every time. If K <3.5 mEq/L, hold insulin and replace first. This is the most critical safety step in DKA management. Total body K is always depleted; serum K may be misleadingly normal or high due to acidosis. Once insulin drives K back into cells, life-threatening hypokalemia can develop within minutes to hours. The protocol sequence is: fluids → check K → replace K if needed → then insulin.
Never stop insulin just because glucose is 200. Insulin is required to suppress ketogenesis, not just lower glucose. When glucose reaches 200 mg/dL, add dextrose to the IV fluids and reduce (not stop) the insulin infusion. Continue until the anion gap is closed and pH ≥7.30. Stopping insulin prematurely is the most common cause of prolonged DKA and re-ketosis during treatment.
The 2-hour overlap when transitioning to SQ insulin is non-negotiable. The IV insulin infusion has a half-life of ~5 minutes. Stopping it without pre-giving basal insulin leaves the patient with no insulin coverage for 2+ hours, long enough to restart ketogenesis. Administer glargine or detemir at least 2 hours before stopping the infusion. Document this explicitly in the transition order. It is most often missed on overnight handoffs.
Think SGLT2 inhibitor in any unexplained HAGMA with normal or low glucose. Euglycemic DKA is missed because clinicians anchor on hyperglycemia. Any patient on an SGLT2 inhibitor with an anion gap acidosis and ketones has euglycemic DKA until proven otherwise — even if their glucose is 140 mg/dL. Check their medication list.
Abdominal pain in DKA: treat the DKA first. DKA itself causes nausea, vomiting, and diffuse abdominal pain from gastric dysmotility and the acidotic state. If abdominal pain resolves with DKA treatment, no further abdominal workup is needed. If severe abdominal pain persists or worsens despite adequate DKA management, pursue CT abdomen for a surgical cause (pancreatitis, ischemia, perforation). Elevated lipase in DKA is common and does not necessarily indicate acute pancreatitis.
Cerebral edema presents as neurological deterioration during treatment, not at onset — and it is predominantly a pediatric complication. In adults it is rare; in children it remains the leading cause of DKA-related death. The child or young adult who was alert on admission but develops sudden headache, confusion, or pupil changes 4–8 hours into treatment has cerebral edema until proven otherwise. Act immediately: reduce fluid rate, elevate HOB, give mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg IV. Do not wait for CT to treat.
