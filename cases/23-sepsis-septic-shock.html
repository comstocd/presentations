Sepsis & Septic Shock

Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. Septic shock is a subset with circulatory, cellular, and metabolic abnormalities associated with substantially increased mortality. Recognition, risk stratification, and time-sensitive bundle execution are the core competencies every IM resident must own.

Definitions: Sepsis-3 (2016)

Why Sepsis-3 Replaced SIRS

The old SIRS-based definition (2 of 4: fever, tachycardia, tachypnea, leukocytosis) was overly sensitive — up to 70% of ICU patients met SIRS criteria without infection. Sepsis-3 shifted focus to organ dysfunction as the defining feature, better reflecting the biology and identifying patients at highest risk of death.

Sepsis (Sepsis-3)

Life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection.

Operationally defined as:

  • Suspected or confirmed infection
  • Acute change in SOFA score ≥2 points attributable to infection

A SOFA score ≥2 is associated with an in-hospital mortality >10%.

Septic Shock (Sepsis-3)

Subset of sepsis with circulatory, cellular, and metabolic abnormalities associated with greater mortality risk than sepsis alone.

Operationally defined as all three:

  • Sepsis (above)
  • Vasopressor requirement to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg
  • Serum lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation

In-hospital mortality: historically reported at ~30–40%; varies substantially by cohort, institution, and patient population.

qSOFA: Bedside Screening (Not Diagnostic)

qSOFA Criteria (1 point each)
  • Respiratory rate ≥22 breaths/min
  • Altered mentation (GCS <15)
  • Systolic BP ≤100 mmHg

qSOFA ≥2 in a patient with suspected infection should prompt further evaluation for sepsis and consideration of ICU-level care. qSOFA alone should not be used to define sepsis — it is a screening tool, not a diagnostic one. It does not replace clinical judgment or SOFA-based organ dysfunction assessment. Use full SOFA for formal diagnosis.

Recognition & Screening

Clinical Presentation

Sepsis does not always look dramatic. High-risk presentations include:

  • Altered mental status in an elderly patient with no other obvious cause
  • Unexplained hypotension or tachycardia
  • Leukocytosis or leukopenia in a patient with known infection source
  • Lactic acidosis on routine labs
  • Acute organ dysfunction (rising creatinine, bilirubin, dropping platelets) in the setting of infection

High-Risk Populations at Lourdes: Know Your Index of Suspicion

Elderly patients, immunocompromised hosts (cirrhosis, DM, ESRD, malignancy, chronic steroids), and patients with indwelling hardware (PICC, HD catheter, Foley, vascular grafts) may not mount a fever or leukocytosis. Hypothermia, normal WBC, or subtle mental status changes may be the only clues. A normal temperature does not exclude sepsis.

The Hour-1 Bundle (Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2018)

Time Is Tissue: Every Hour Matters

Observational data suggest that delays in appropriate antibiotic therapy are associated with increased mortality in septic shock — one influential study estimated approximately 7% increased mortality per hour of delay (Kumar et al., 2006), though this figure is context-dependent and derived from a single retrospective cohort. Bundle compliance is associated with improved outcomes in observational and quality-improvement studies, though these associations are confounded and should not be interpreted as guaranteed causal effects. The principle stands: earlier recognition, earlier source control, and earlier appropriate antibiotics improve outcomes.

Hour-1 Bundle: All Five Within 60 Minutes of Recognition
  1. Measure lactate. Remeasure if initial lactate >2 mmol/L to assess clearance.
  2. Obtain blood cultures before administering antibiotics. At least 2 sets (aerobic + anaerobic). Do not delay antibiotics >45 minutes waiting for cultures.
  3. Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics. Empiric coverage based on suspected source, local antibiogram, and host factors (immunocompromised, recent antibiotics, known colonization).
  4. Administer 30 mL/kg crystalloid for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/L. Reassess after bolus.
  5. Apply vasopressors if patient is hypotensive during or after fluid resuscitation to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg.

Fluid Resuscitation

Initial Resuscitation: 30 mL/kg

The 30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus is guideline-recommended for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/L. Use IBW in morbid obesity. Balanced crystalloids (lactated Ringer's, PlasmaLyte) are preferred over normal saline based on SMART and SALT-ED trial data showing lower rates of AKI and need for renal replacement therapy.

Fluid Responsiveness: Don't Just Keep Pushing

After initial resuscitation, reassess before giving more fluid. Approximately 50% of hemodynamically unstable ICU patients are fluid-unresponsive — additional fluid causes harm (pulmonary edema, abdominal compartment syndrome, worsening organ function) without improving cardiac output.

FACTT & Conservative Fluid Strategy After Resuscitation

The FACTT trial demonstrated that a conservative fluid strategy after initial resuscitation in ARDS patients reduced ventilator days and ICU length of stay without increasing organ failure. Once the patient is resuscitated (lactate clearing, MAP ≥65 on low vasopressors), shift to a conservative fluid strategy. De-resuscitation (active diuresis) in the recovery phase further improves outcomes.

Vasopressor Selection

First-Line: Norepinephrine

Norepinephrine (Levophed) is the vasopressor of choice for septic shock per Surviving Sepsis 2021 guidelines. Primarily α1-adrenergic (vasoconstriction) with modest β1 effect (mild inotropy). Dose: 0.01–3 mcg/kg/min. Preferred over dopamine (higher arrhythmia risk, no mortality benefit, increased 28-day mortality in cardiogenic shock subgroup per SOAP II trial).

MAP Target: 65 mmHg Is Enough for Most

The SEPSISPAM trial compared MAP targets of 65–70 vs. 80–85 mmHg. Higher MAP target did not reduce 28-day mortality in the overall population but did reduce need for renal replacement therapy in patients with chronic hypertension. Individualize: target MAP 65 mmHg for most; consider 70–75 in known hypertensives or evidence of end-organ hypoperfusion at 65.

Antibiotic Strategy

Timing: The Most Important Variable

Delays in appropriate antibiotic therapy are associated with worse outcomes in septic shock (Kumar et al., 2006). Target administration within 1 hour of sepsis recognition as a practical operational goal. The SSC does not specify a sub-1-hour threshold; the key principle is that antibiotics should not be meaningfully delayed for any reason other than an imminent blood culture draw.

De-escalation: As Important as Initiation

  • Narrow therapy within 48–72 hours based on culture results and clinical response
  • Discontinue antibiotics if infection is ruled out (procalcitonin-guided protocols reduce duration)
  • Standard course for most bacterial infections causing sepsis: 7 days (IDSA/SSC guidance)
  • Bacteremia (non-endocarditis): 14 days for Staph aureus; 7–14 days for gram-negatives; 14–28 days for fungemia
  • Procalcitonin: levels >0.5 ng/mL suggest bacterial infection; serial decline guides safe discontinuation

Procalcitonin-Guided Antibiotic Stewardship

PRORATA and SAPS trials demonstrated procalcitonin-guided antibiotic discontinuation reduces antibiotic duration by ~2 days without increasing mortality. SSC 2021 recommends using PCT to support de-escalation decisions. A declining PCT (fall >80% from peak, or absolute <0.5 ng/mL) in a clinically improving patient supports stopping antibiotics. Do not use PCT alone — always integrate with clinical picture.

Lactate & Clearance

Why Lactate Matters

Lactate elevation (>2 mmol/L) in sepsis reflects tissue hypoperfusion and anaerobic metabolism. It is both a risk-stratification tool and a therapeutic target. Note: lactate >2 mmol/L alone does not define septic shock — the Sepsis-3 definition requires both vasopressor dependence to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg and lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation. A patient with lactate ≥4 mmol/L and normal blood pressure does not formally meet septic shock criteria, but does carry high mortality risk and warrants aggressive evaluation and treatment as presumptive occult shock.

Lactate Clearance as a Resuscitation Target
  • Remeasure lactate 2 hours after initial resuscitation
  • Lactate clearance ≥10% at 2 hours is associated with improved outcomes (LACTATE trial)
  • Target normalization of lactate (<2 mmol/L) as a resuscitation endpoint
  • Lactate clearance-guided resuscitation is non-inferior to ScvO2-guided resuscitation (LACTATE trial vs. ProCESS/ARISE/ProMISe)
  • Persistent lactate elevation despite resuscitation should prompt reassessment: undrained source, ongoing hemorrhage, mesenteric ischemia, vasopressor-induced vasoconstriction, or epinephrine use (raises lactate via glycogenolysis)

Causes of Elevated Lactate Beyond Shock

  • Type A (hypoxic): Sepsis, cardiogenic shock, hemorrhagic shock, mesenteric ischemia — most clinically significant
  • Type B (non-hypoxic): Liver failure (impaired clearance), metformin toxicity, thiamine deficiency, epinephrine, salicylates, HIV medications, malignancy, seizures
  • Always correlate with clinical context — a patient on metformin with AKI may have elevated lactate from biguanide accumulation, not tissue hypoperfusion

Corticosteroids in Septic Shock

SSC 2021 Recommendation

Consider hydrocortisone 200 mg/day IV (50 mg q6h or 200 mg continuous infusion) in adult patients with septic shock who are not responding adequately to fluids and vasopressors.

  • Based on ADRENAL and APROCCHSS trials
  • ADRENAL: hydrocortisone reduced vasopressor duration and ICU LOS but not 90-day mortality in the overall population
  • APROCCHSS: hydrocortisone + fludrocortisone reduced 90-day mortality (43% vs 49.1%; p=0.03)
  • SSC recommends hydrocortisone alone (not fludrocortisone) for most; fludrocortisone can be considered
  • Duration: typically 5–7 days; taper if clinically stable; do not abruptly discontinue
  • Do NOT use cosyntropin stimulation test to guide steroid use — no longer recommended

Practical Trigger

A practical threshold: norepinephrine ≥0.25 mcg/kg/min after adequate fluid resuscitation for ≥4 hours without improvement. At that point, add hydrocortisone 200 mg/day. Vasopressor-sparing effect typically evident within 24–48 hours.

What to Avoid

  • EGDT (early goal-directed therapy) as originally described — ProCESS, ARISE, and ProMISe trials showed no benefit over usual care; abandoned as a protocol; individual elements (cultures, fluids, vasopressors, lactate) remain evidence-based
  • Routine CVP targets — CVP does not predict fluid responsiveness; targeting CVP 8–12 is no longer recommended
  • Routine blood transfusion to Hgb >10 — TRICC trial supports restrictive strategy (transfuse at Hgb <7 in most; <8 in cardiac disease or active ischemia)

Special Populations

PopulationKey Modifications
Cirrhosis Baseline hypotension and elevated lactate complicate interpretation. Higher index of suspicion for SBP, spontaneous bacteremia, hepatic hydrothorax infection. SBP-specific: albumin 1.5 g/kg IV on day 1 + 1 g/kg on day 3 with antibiotic initiation reduces hepatorenal syndrome risk (Sort NEJM 1999) — this is an SBP-specific intervention, not a general sepsis recommendation. Vasopressin often more effective than NE due to relative vasopressin deficiency in cirrhosis. Avoid nephrotoxins aggressively.
ESRD / Dialysis Oliguria is not a reliable resuscitation endpoint. Fluid resuscitation can precipitate pulmonary edema rapidly. Catheter-related BSI is common. Dose adjustments for renally cleared antibiotics. Consider emergent dialysis for fluid overload if unresponsive to diuretics.
Immunocompromised / Neutropenic Anti-pseudomonal coverage mandatory. Lower threshold for anti-fungal. Fever may be blunted. Consider PCP, CMV, invasive fungal infection. Avoid G-CSF in established sepsis (no mortality benefit per SSC).
Elderly Atypical presentations (hypothermia, confusion without fever). Lower baseline MAP — individualize target. High risk of fluid overload. Polypharmacy complicates antibiotic selection (QT prolongation, drug interactions).
Pregnancy Group A Strep and E. coli most common. Physiologic leukocytosis/tachycardia makes interpretation harder. Avoid fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines. Emergent delivery may be required for source control (septic abortion, chorioamnionitis).

Clinical Pearls

Elevated lactate signals risk even without hypotension. Septic shock requires both vasopressor-dependent hypotension and lactate >2 mmol/L after adequate resuscitation — lactate alone does not meet the formal definition. However, a patient with lactate ≥4 mmol/L and normal blood pressure carries very high mortality risk and should be treated with the same urgency as overt shock. Normal BP does not mean normal perfusion. Check lactate in all patients with suspected sepsis, not just those who are hypotensive.
Blood cultures before antibiotics, but do not delay antibiotics for cultures. Each set of blood cultures takes 2–5 minutes to draw. If cultures cannot be obtained quickly, give antibiotics and culture when feasible. The yield of blood cultures drawn after antibiotics drops significantly, but the clinical cost of delaying antibiotics in septic shock exceeds the diagnostic cost of post-antibiotic cultures. The practical rule: draw cultures, then treat — never the reverse.
Norepinephrine first. Dopamine is associated with higher arrhythmia rates and worse outcomes in certain shock subtypes (SOAP II trial). It is generally avoided in septic shock. There are rare clinical scenarios — such as bradycardia-predominant shock — where it has been considered, but norepinephrine remains the preferred agent across nearly all situations. When NE alone is insufficient, add vasopressin as the second agent rather than escalating to dopamine.
After the bolus, assess fluid responsiveness before giving more. The 30 mL/kg initial bolus is appropriate. Continuing to give fluid beyond that without assessing response (passive leg raise, PPV, clinical reassessment) causes harm. ARDS, AKI, and abdominal compartment syndrome are all fluid overload complications. Volume of resuscitation is independently associated with mortality above a threshold.
De-escalate early and aggressively. Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics are necessary upfront. But continuing unnecessary antibiotics beyond 48–72 hours when cultures are negative or narrow organisms are identified drives resistance, C. diff, and adverse drug effects. A procalcitonin-guided protocol can reduce antibiotic duration by 2 days with no mortality increase. Stewardship is a resuscitation-phase responsibility, not just a pharmacy or ID issue.
Source control is not optional. A patient with intra-abdominal sepsis who receives perfect antibiotics and vasopressor management but has an undrained abscess will die from that abscess. Source control — drainage, debridement, line removal — must be actively sought and executed in parallel with medical resuscitation, not deferred until the patient is "stable."
