Delirium

Delirium is an acute disturbance in attention and awareness with additional cognitive dysfunction, representing a medical emergency that is frequently missed, undertreated, and associated with increased mortality, prolonged hospitalization, long-term cognitive decline, and institutionalization. It is not a normal part of aging, not inevitable, and often preventable.

Definition & DSM-5 Criteria

DSM-5 Diagnostic Criteria for Delirium (All Five Required)
  1. Disturbance in attention (reduced ability to direct, focus, sustain, and shift attention) and awareness (reduced orientation to the environment)
  2. The disturbance develops acutely (hours to days) and represents a change from baseline; it tends to fluctuate in severity throughout the day
  3. An additional disturbance in cognition (memory deficit, disorientation, language, visuospatial ability, or perception)
  4. The disturbances are not better explained by a pre-existing, established, or evolving neurocognitive disorder (dementia)
  5. The disturbance is a direct physiological consequence of another medical condition, substance intoxication or withdrawal, or multiple etiologies

The Three Essential Features to Memorize

Acute onset, fluctuating course, and inattention. These three features distinguish delirium from dementia (chronic, non-fluctuating, attention relatively preserved early) and from psychiatric illness (no acute onset, attention intact, no medical trigger). If a patient cannot recite the months of the year backwards or cannot maintain focus during your history, that is inattention — assess further.

Subtypes

Hyperactive Delirium (~25%)
  • Agitation, restlessness, combativeness
  • Pulling at lines and tubes
  • Yelling, thrashing
  • Hallucinations (visual > auditory)
  • Easily recognized — the patient who is "difficult"
  • Associated with alcohol/sedative withdrawal, medications
Hypoactive Delirium (~50%) — The Dangerous One
  • Somnolence, withdrawal, psychomotor slowing
  • Minimal spontaneous movement or speech
  • Appears "calm" or "resting comfortably"
  • Frequently missed and mistaken for depression, fatigue, or expected illness behavior
  • Associated with worse outcomes than hyperactive
  • More common in elderly, hepatic encephalopathy, opioid toxicity
Mixed Delirium (~25%)

Features of both hyperactive and hypoactive delirium, fluctuating between them throughout the day. Most common subtype in ICU patients. The patient who was agitated overnight and now seems "better" in the morning may simply be in the hypoactive phase of mixed delirium.

Hypoactive Delirium Is Missed >70% of the Time

Studies using systematic screening tools consistently show hypoactive delirium goes unrecognized by nurses and physicians in the majority of cases — recognition rates without systematic screening are often below 30%. The quiet, withdrawn patient does not trigger concern the way an agitated patient does. Active delirium screening with a validated tool (CAM or CAM-ICU) on every patient, every shift, is the most reliable way to identify hypoactive delirium.

Consequences of Delirium

  • Mortality: 10–26% during hospitalization; 22–76% at 6 months; independent predictor of death after adjustment for severity of illness
  • Long-term cognitive decline: Delirium is associated with accelerated progression to dementia in cohort studies; some analyses report 2–8-fold increased risk of dementia at follow-up, though causal direction and confounding by shared risk factors remain debated
  • Hospital LOS: Adds average 5–10 days to hospitalization
  • Functional decline: Loss of ADLs that may not recover; increased institutionalization
  • Post-ICU: Post-intensive care syndrome (PICS) — cognitive, psychological, and physical impairments persisting months to years after ICU discharge
  • Cost: Estimated at $164 billion annually in the US in older analyses (direct costs alone); current estimates vary but remain in the tens of billions; this is one of the most costly hospital-acquired conditions

Pathophysiology

Delirium results from widespread disruption of cerebral network connectivity, particularly the prefrontal cortex and its connections mediating attention and executive function. Multiple neurobiological mechanisms converge:

MechanismDetail
NeuroinflammationSystemic inflammation (cytokines: IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) crosses the blood-brain barrier → microglial activation → neuronal dysfunction. Explains why sepsis, surgery, and any severe illness can precipitate delirium.
Neurotransmitter imbalanceReduced acetylcholine (ACh) — the predominant hypothesis; excess dopamine; reduced GABA; excess glutamate. ACh deficiency impairs attention circuits; explains why anticholinergic medications are a major risk factor and why physostigmine can temporarily reverse some delirium.
Oxidative stressHypoxia, ischemia, mitochondrial dysfunction → reactive oxygen species → neuronal injury. Relevant in sepsis, respiratory failure, cardiac surgery.
Neuroendocrine dysregulationElevated cortisol (stress response) suppresses hippocampal neurogenesis and impairs memory consolidation. HPA axis dysregulation contributes to cognitive dysfunction.
Sleep-wake cycle disruptionLoss of circadian rhythm → melatonin dysregulation → altered arousal and attention. ICU environment (light, noise, continuous interventions) perpetuates this.
Cerebral blood flow alterationsHypoperfusion (hypotension, hypoxia, anemia) impairs neuronal energy metabolism; particularly damaging in patients with pre-existing cerebrovascular disease.

Risk Factors & Precipitants

Delirium results from the interaction of predisposing factors (baseline vulnerability) and precipitating factors (acute insults). A highly vulnerable patient (elderly, demented, deaf) may develop delirium from a mild insult (one sleeping pill); a low-vulnerability patient may require multiple severe insults.

Predisposing Factors (Baseline Vulnerability)
  • Advanced age (>65) — strongest predictor
  • Pre-existing cognitive impairment / dementia
  • Severe underlying illness (high APACHE, SOFA)
  • Functional dependence (ADL impairment)
  • Sensory impairment (vision, hearing)
  • Dehydration / malnutrition at baseline
  • Prior history of delirium
  • Depression or other psychiatric illness
  • Alcohol use disorder
  • Sleep deprivation at baseline
Precipitating Factors (The "MOVE IT" Mnemonic)
  • Medications — see table below
  • Oxygen deficiency (hypoxia, hypercapnia, anemia)
  • Vascular (MI, stroke, PE, hypotension)
  • Electrolytes (Na, Ca, Mg, glucose, uremia)
  • Infection (UTI, pneumonia, sepsis, meningitis)
  • Trauma / Pain / Surgery / Immobility

Additional: urinary retention, constipation, fecal impaction, sleep deprivation, environmental factors (ICU, restraints), withdrawal (alcohol, benzos, opioids)

Assessment Tools: CAM & CAM-ICU

Confusion Assessment Method (CAM)

The gold standard bedside tool for diagnosing delirium outside the ICU. Validated against DSM criteria; sensitivity 94%, specificity 89% when administered correctly. Requires a brief cognitive assessment (e.g., digit span, days of week backwards) to assess Feature 2.

CAM Diagnostic Algorithm: Delirium = Features 1 + 2 + (3 OR 4)
  1. Feature 1: Acute onset and fluctuating course
    "Is there evidence of an acute change in mental status from baseline? Does the abnormal behavior fluctuate during the day?"
  2. Feature 2: Inattention (Required)
    "Does the patient have difficulty focusing attention?" — Test: digits forward/backward, months of year backwards, squeeze hand when you say the letter "A" in a random letter sequence
  3. Feature 3: Disorganized thinking
    "Does the patient have disorganized or incoherent thinking?" — Rambling speech, illogical flow, unpredictable topic switching, unclear or irrelevant answers
  4. Feature 4: Altered level of consciousness
    Anything other than alert and appropriate: hyperalert, lethargic, stuporous, or comatose

Delirium present if: Feature 1 AND Feature 2 AND (Feature 3 OR Feature 4)

CAM-ICU

Adapted for mechanically ventilated, non-verbal patients. Replaces verbal cognitive testing with non-verbal tasks. Also uses a 4-feature structure. First assess level of arousal with the Richmond Agitation-Sedation Scale (RASS):

RASS Scale
ScoreTermDescription
+4CombativeViolent, immediate danger to staff
+3Very agitatedPulls/removes tubes; aggressive
+2AgitatedFrequent nonpurposeful movement; fights ventilator
+1RestlessAnxious but movements not aggressive

-1DrowsyNot fully alert; sustained awakening >10 sec to voice
-2Light sedationBriefly awakens to voice (<10 sec)
-3Moderate sedationMovement or eye opening to voice; no eye contact
-4Deep sedationNo response to voice; movement to physical stimulus
-5UnarousableNo response to voice or physical stimulus

CAM-ICU cannot be assessed if RASS ≤−3 (deep sedation/unarousable). In these patients, delirium assessment is deferred until sedation is lightened. Target RASS 0 to −1 for most mechanically ventilated patients (PADIS guidelines).

Other Tools

  • 3D-CAM: 3-minute diagnostic interview version of CAM for non-ICU settings; faster than full CAM
  • ICDSC (Intensive Care Delirium Screening Checklist): 8-item nurse-administered tool; alternative to CAM-ICU
  • 4AT: Brief assessment tool (alertness, AMT4, attention, acute change); validated in ED and general medical settings; takes <2 minutes

Differential Diagnosis

ConditionKey Distinguishing Features
Dementia Chronic, progressive; no acute onset; attention relatively preserved early; no fluctuation; no clear medical precipitant. Note: Dementia is the #1 predisposing factor for delirium — both can coexist.
Depression Psychomotor slowing and withdrawal can mimic hypoactive delirium; onset over weeks-months not hours-days; attention and orientation usually intact; no fluctuation; no medical trigger. CAM negative.
Psychosis (primary) Usually younger patient; hallucinations often auditory > visual; no medical trigger; attention relatively intact; chronic or episodic (not acute and fluctuating in the delirium sense). Rule out medical cause first.
Non-convulsive status epilepticus (NCSE) Can present identically to delirium; altered consciousness without overt convulsions; EEG required to diagnose; consider in any refractory or unexplained delirium, especially post-seizure or after generalized convulsion
Encephalitis / Meningitis Fever, headache, meningismus, CSF pleocytosis; viral encephalitis (HSV) can present acutely with behavioral change; LP required when CNS infection is on differential
Stroke / CNS lesion Focal neurological deficits; acute onset but typically non-fluctuating; right parietal or thalamic strokes can cause confusion without focal deficits — MRI brain may be required
Wernicke's encephalopathy Triad of encephalopathy, oculomotor dysfunction, ataxia; thiamine deficiency; give thiamine empirically in any AUD or malnourished patient with delirium
Drug intoxication / withdrawal Alcohol withdrawal, benzodiazepine withdrawal, opioid intoxication, anticholinergic toxidrome; toxicology screen and medication review essential

Workup

The workup for delirium is a search for the underlying cause(s). There is no lab test that diagnoses delirium itself — it is a clinical diagnosis. The evaluation is guided by history, physical exam, and identification of likely precipitants.

Minimum Evaluation in Every Patient

  • Medication reconciliation — the single highest-yield intervention; review every medication for deliriogenic potential; check for recent additions or dose changes
  • Vitals review — hypoxia, hypotension, fever, tachycardia, hypertensive emergency
  • BMP / CMP — sodium, glucose, creatinine/BUN (uremia), calcium, liver enzymes
  • CBC — infection (leukocytosis/leukopenia), anemia
  • Urinalysis — UTI (but beware: asymptomatic bacteriuria does not cause delirium; only treat if symptomatic)
  • Blood cultures — if fever or signs of infection
  • Chest X-ray — pneumonia, pulmonary edema
  • Pulse oximetry / ABG — hypoxia, hypercapnia
  • Toxicology screen — if substance use disorder or unexplained presentation
  • Thyroid function — if not recently checked; hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism both cause delirium

Directed Testing Based on Clinical Suspicion

SuspicionTest

Asymptomatic Bacteriuria ≠ Cause of Delirium

One of the most common overdiagnosis errors in inpatient medicine. A positive UA with bacteria in an elderly patient with delirium does not mean UTI is causing the delirium. Asymptomatic bacteriuria is present in 25–50% of elderly women at baseline. Treating it with antibiotics does not improve delirium and drives resistance. Only treat if there are localizing urinary symptoms (dysuria, urgency, frequency) or clear evidence of urosepsis. Continue the delirium workup to find the real cause.

Non-Pharmacologic Management

Non-Pharmacologic Interventions Are First-Line — Not Optional

The Hospital Elder Life Program (HELP) RCT demonstrated that multicomponent non-pharmacologic strategies reduced delirium incidence by approximately one-third in at-risk hospitalized patients, and subsequent quality improvement studies support this benefit. No medication has been shown to reliably prevent delirium. Non-pharmacologic care is not "soft medicine" — it is the evidence-based standard.

SensoryEnsure glasses and hearing aids are in use; adequate lighting; speak clearly and slowly; minimize competing noiseSensory deprivation and misperception drive delirium; correcting it is modifiable

Hydration & NutritionEnsure adequate oral fluid intake; early enteral nutrition in ICU; treat constipation; minimize fasting periodsDehydration and malnutrition perpetuate delirium; constipation and urinary retention are often missed precipitants

Physical Restraints Worsen Delirium

Physical restraints are associated with increased agitation, longer delirium duration, pressure injuries, aspiration, DVT, and death. They do not prevent patients from falling or removing lines in the long run — patients in restraints injure themselves trying to escape. Restraints should be a last resort for immediate safety only, with a documented clinical rationale, time limit, and reassessment every shift. One-to-one sitter, family presence, and de-escalation are preferable alternatives.

Pharmacologic Management

No Medication Is FDA-Approved for Delirium

Despite widespread use, no medication has been shown in high-quality RCTs to reduce delirium duration, severity, or mortality. Pharmacologic treatment is reserved for patients whose delirium poses a safety risk to themselves or others (severe agitation, pulling at life-sustaining devices), or for symptom relief when non-pharmacologic measures are insufficient. Treat the underlying cause first.

AgentDoseRoleKey Considerations
Haloperidol (Haldol) 0.5–2 mg PO/IV/IM q4–8h PRN; start low in elderly Most-used agent for acute agitation in delirium; controls hyperactive symptoms Does not shorten delirium duration (HOPE-ICU, MIND-USA trials); prolongs QTc — baseline ECG, avoid if QTc >500; EPS risk (acute dystonia, akathisia); avoid in Lewy body dementia (severe EPS, neuroleptic malignant syndrome); IV route prolongs QTc more than PO/IM
Quetiapine (Seroquel) 12.5–50 mg PO BID–TID PRN; PO only Atypical antipsychotic; sedating; useful in hyperactive delirium with sleep disruption Less EPS than haloperidol; still prolongs QTc; metabolic side effects with prolonged use; no IV formulation; used in Parkinson's (lower EPS risk among atypicals); in Lewy body dementia, clozapine is actually the safest antipsychotic if one is required — quetiapine has lower EPS risk than typical agents but is not risk-free in LBD; MIND-USA showed no benefit over placebo for delirium outcomes in ICU
Olanzapine (Zyprexa) 2.5–5 mg PO/IM QD–BID Atypical; sedating; used in agitated delirium IM available; less QTc prolongation than haloperidol; metabolic effects; avoid in elderly with aspiration risk (sedating); avoid in Lewy body
Melatonin 0.5–3 mg PO QHS Sleep-wake cycle regulation; delirium prevention Very safe; modest supportive evidence for prevention in surgical and medical patients across several small trials; reasonable low-risk adjunct for sleep promotion; not for acute agitation management
Dexmedetomidine 0.2–1.5 mcg/kg/hr IV infusion ICU sedation associated with less delirium than benzodiazepines; useful in hypoactive delirium in ventilated patients MENDS and SEDCOM trials: dexmedetomidine > lorazepam/midazolam for delirium-free days in ICU; bradycardia and hypotension; requires ICU setting; not for acute agitation outside ICU
Benzodiazepines Variable Avoid in most delirium — worsen and prolong it. Specific indications: alcohol/sedative withdrawal delirium (first-line); seizure; extreme agitation threatening airway; terminal agitation at end of life (comfort-focused care) In non-withdrawal delirium: benzodiazepines increase delirium severity and duration, increase aspiration risk, and may cause paradoxical agitation in elderly. Do not use PRN lorazepam for "agitation" in a delirious patient unless withdrawal is confirmed or seizure is occurring.

Avoid Benzodiazepines in Non-Withdrawal Delirium

Lorazepam (Ativan) is among the most commonly prescribed and most harmful pharmacologic interventions for delirium outside of withdrawal. It is consistently associated with worsening delirium in non-withdrawal settings. The delirious elderly patient who receives "Ativan 0.5 mg IV for agitation" at 2 AM is often more confused and more dangerous by 4 AM. Outside of alcohol or sedative withdrawal delirium, seizure, and terminal agitation in the actively dying patient, benzodiazepines should be avoided as first-line treatment.

ICU Delirium: PADIS Guidelines & ABCDEF Bundle

PADIS Guidelines (2018)

The Society of Critical Care Medicine's Pain, Agitation/Sedation, Delirium, Immobility, and Sleep Disruption (PADIS) guidelines represent the comprehensive evidence-based framework for managing delirium in the ICU. Key recommendations:

  • Screen for delirium every shift using CAM-ICU or ICDSC
  • Target light sedation (RASS 0 to −1) rather than deep sedation
  • Use dexmedetomidine over benzodiazepines when sedation is required in mechanically ventilated patients
  • Do not use haloperidol or atypical antipsychotics routinely to prevent or treat ICU delirium (MIND-USA)
  • Mobilize patients early and progressively
  • Promote sleep by clustering care and minimizing unnecessary nighttime interventions
ABCDEF Bundle: ICU Liberation
  • A — Assess, prevent, and manage pain (use NRS or CPOT; treat pain first before sedation)
  • B — Both spontaneous awakening trials (SAT) and spontaneous breathing trials (SBT) daily
  • C — Choice of sedation and analgesia (analgesia-first; dexmedetomidine or propofol over benzos; target lightest effective sedation)
  • D — Delirium assess, prevent, and manage (CAM-ICU every shift; multicomponent non-pharmacologic bundle)
  • E — Early mobility and exercise (physical and occupational therapy from day 1 of ICU)
  • F — Family engagement and empowerment (open visitation; family at bedside; family-centered rounds)

Full ABCDEF bundle implementation is associated with a 68% lower odds of delirium, shorter mechanical ventilation, lower 7-day mortality, and higher likelihood of discharge home vs. institution (Pun et al., Crit Care Med 2019).

Special Populations

PopulationKey Considerations
Elderly (>65) Highest risk group; start all medications at lowest possible dose; avoid anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, meperidine, H2 blockers (Beers Criteria); delirium may be the only presenting sign of serious illness (MI, PE, sepsis); family history is critical for establishing baseline
Dementia Hardest to distinguish from delirium; baseline cognitive testing from family/caregiver is essential; CAM should be applied against the patient's own cognitive baseline; hypoactive delirium superimposed on dementia is almost universally missed; any acute behavioral change in a dementia patient is delirium until proven otherwise
Lewy Body Dementia (LBD) Profound sensitivity to antipsychotics — even low-dose haloperidol can cause severe, irreversible parkinsonism or neuroleptic malignant syndrome. If an antipsychotic is truly required, clozapine is the safest option in LBD; quetiapine is sometimes used but carries risk. Ask about fluctuating cognition, visual hallucinations, and parkinsonism before prescribing any antipsychotic.
Parkinson's Disease High delirium risk; dopamine agonists themselves can cause psychosis and delirium; avoid D2 blockers (typical antipsychotics, metoclopramide, prochlorperazine); quetiapine or clozapine are safest if antipsychotic needed; ensure PD medications are not held (missed doses cause acute deterioration)
Post-operative Pain is a major unrecognized precipitant; adequate analgesia is delirium prevention; regional anesthesia may reduce delirium compared to general anesthesia in hip surgery; avoid prophylactic melatonin has modest evidence; avoid routine prophylactic antipsychotics (no evidence)
End-of-life / Palliative Terminal delirium (last hours to days of life) is common and often irreversible. Goals of care discussion with family is essential. Low-dose haloperidol or chlorpromazine for symptom relief; benzodiazepines (midazolam) may be appropriate for refractory terminal agitation in the dying patient when comfort is the goal.

Clinical Pearls

Hypoactive delirium kills quietly. The agitated patient gets attention; the quiet, withdrawn patient gets missed. More than half of all delirium is hypoactive or mixed. Any patient who seems "more tired than usual" or "just not themselves" deserves a CAM assessment. A patient who stares at the ceiling and does not engage is not resting — they may be delirious.
Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) is contraindicated in hospitalized elderly patients. It is the most anticholinergic over-the-counter medication available. Ordered routinely for sleep or pruritus, it is a leading cause of iatrogenic delirium. Use melatonin 0.5–3 mg for sleep. Use hydroxyzine cautiously (also anticholinergic but less so than diphenhydramine). Document "no diphenhydramine" as a clinical preference in high-risk patients.
The midnight agitation call: don't reflex to lorazepam. When called about an agitated patient at 2 AM, the instinct is to order Ativan. In a non-withdrawing patient, lorazepam will make the delirium worse by morning. First: go see the patient. Check for urinary retention (straight cath if needed), pain, hypoxia, hypoglycemia, and a full medication list. Then try reorientation and de-escalation. If pharmacotherapy is truly needed, haloperidol 0.5–1 mg is preferable to lorazepam in the non-withdrawing patient.
A positive urine culture in a delirious elderly patient is not the diagnosis. Asymptomatic bacteriuria is present in up to 50% of elderly women. Attributing delirium to "UTI" without localizing urinary symptoms leads to unnecessary antibiotics, missed real diagnoses, and C. diff. Complete the full workup regardless of the UA result.
Screen every patient, every shift, with a validated tool. Clinical impression alone misses >70% of hypoactive delirium. A brief CAM takes 2–3 minutes. Institutionalizing systematic delirium screening (like systematic pain scoring) is the most impactful systems intervention for reducing delirium burden at the unit level. Delirium prevalence data is also a quality metric.
Family is medicine. A familiar voice, a familiar face, and a gentle hand are the most powerful delirium interventions available. Families should be encouraged to stay, talk to the patient, bring familiar objects, and participate actively in reorientation. Open visitation policies in the ICU are associated with reduced delirium rates. Never view family presence as an inconvenience — it is a clinical intervention.
