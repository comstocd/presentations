Syncope vs. Seizure: Differentiating Transient Loss of Consciousness

44-year-old male with 3-year history of nocturnal events and known bradycardia presenting with 20–30 minute LOC episode at work, forehead laceration, and bilateral wrist abrasions.

Presentation

Key History Points

  • 44-year-old male, found by EMS after LOC at work while seated watching a video
  • Does not recall the event; estimates 20–30 minutes of lost time (EMS response + regaining consciousness)
  • Forehead laceration + bilateral ulnar wrist abrasions (suggests sudden postural loss)
  • 3-year history of similar nocturnal events — previously unworked up
  • Known bradycardia, HR typically 40s — prior diagnosis, no pacemaker, no beta-blockers
  • No prior seizure diagnosis, no antiepileptics, no EtOH, no tobacco
  • Cannabis ~½ oz/week
  • Sleeps ~6 hours/night
  • Creates acrylic artwork recreationally

The Critical Question

Is this cardiac syncope (dangerous, mortality-linked) or seizure (requires neurologic evaluation and driving restrictions) — or a benign vasovagal episode? The known bradycardia in the 40s and prolonged LOC duration are red flags that elevate this above a routine syncopal evaluation.

Syncope vs. Seizure: Definitions & Mechanisms

Syncope (ESC 2018 Definition)

Syncope: Diagnostic Criteria
  • Transient, self-limited loss of consciousness (TLOC)
  • Associated with loss of postural tone
  • Rapid onset, short duration, spontaneous complete recovery
  • Mechanism: Global cerebral hypoperfusion — either bilateral cortical hypoperfusion or reticular activating system hypoperfusion
  • No resuscitative measures required
  • Normal neurological function at baseline (no postictal period)

Seizure (ILAE Definition)

Transient occurrence of signs and/or symptoms due to abnormal excessive or synchronous neuronal activity in the brain. Not all seizures cause LOC; generalized tonic-clonic seizures do and may mimic syncope. Key distinction: seizures typically have a postictal phase (5–60+ minutes of confusion, fatigue, headache), tongue biting, urinary incontinence, and stereotyped prodrome (aura).

⚠️ The 20–30 Minute LOC Duration is NOT Typical Syncope

True syncope typically lasts seconds, rarely beyond 1–2 minutes. A prolonged LOC of 20+ minutes suggests either: (1) prolonged postictal state after seizure, (2) a cardiac arrhythmia with prolonged cerebral hypoperfusion, or (3) trauma-related LOC from the fall. This duration mandates thorough cardiac and neurologic evaluation before attributing to vasovagal syncope.

Risk Stratification Tools

San Francisco Syncope Rule (SFSR)

High risk if any of CHESS:

  • CHF history
  • Hematocrit <30%
  • ECG abnormal (new changes, non-sinus rhythm, QTc >500)
  • Shortness of breath
  • SBP <90 at triage

Sensitivity ~98% for 7-day serious outcome. This patient's bradycardia and ECG status are key inputs.

ROSE Rule (Risk Stratification of Syncope)

High risk if any of BRACES:

  • BNP ≥300
  • Rhythm: bradycardia <50
  • Anemia: Hgb <9
  • Chest pain
  • ECG: Q waves (non-inferior)
  • Saturation ≤94%

This patient's HR 40s baseline meets "Rhythm" criterion — HIGH risk by ROSE.

Diagnostic Workup

Immediate (All Patients with TLOC)

  • 12-lead ECG — evaluate for AV block, QTc prolongation, delta waves (WPW), Brugada pattern, epsilon waves (ARVC), ST changes
  • Orthostatic vitals — lying → sitting → standing at 1, 3, 5 minutes; drop ≥20 mmHg systolic = orthostatic hypotension
  • Glucose, electrolytes, CBC — metabolic causes
  • Troponin — cardiac event triggering syncope or resulting from it

For This High-Risk Patient

  • Continuous cardiac monitoring (telemetry) — mandatory given bradycardia HR 40s
  • Echocardiogram — structural disease, EF, valvulopathy
  • Ambulatory monitoring — if no arrhythmia found on telemetry: 30-day event monitor or implantable loop recorder (ILR) given recurrent unexplained events
  • EEG — given 3-year nocturnal event history raising seizure suspicion
  • MRI Brain — if seizure is suspected or neurologic findings present
  • Electrophysiology (EP) study — if structural or arrhythmic cause suspected and non-invasive workup negative; assesses for sinus node dysfunction, AV nodal disease
  • Tilt table test — if vasovagal suspected after cardiac workup negative

Implantable Loop Recorder (ILR)

For patients with recurrent unexplained syncope where initial evaluation is negative, ILR (e.g., Reveal LINQ) provides up to 3 years of continuous cardiac rhythm monitoring. The PICTURE registry demonstrated ILR identifies a cause in ~78% of patients with unexplained syncope within 12 months. Given this patient's 3-year history of events, an ILR may be the most efficient diagnostic strategy after initial workup.

Management by Likely Etiology

If Cardiac Arrhythmia (Sinus Node Dysfunction / AV Block) Confirmed

  • Permanent pacemaker implantation (PPM) — Class I indication for symptomatic sinus bradycardia with documented correlation, or Mobitz II/complete AV block
  • Avoid AV nodal blocking agents (beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin)

If Seizure Confirmed

  • Neurology referral; antiepileptic therapy selection based on seizure type and EEG pattern
  • Driving restriction counseling — NYS requires 6–12 months seizure-free before driving
  • Avoid triggers (sleep deprivation, cannabis can affect seizure threshold)

If Vasovagal Syncope Confirmed (Diagnosis of Exclusion)

  • Education, increased salt/fluid intake, physical counterpressure maneuvers
  • Midodrine or fludrocortisone if recurrent and disabling
  • Beta-blockers no longer recommended (ISSUE-3 trial showed no benefit)

Clinical Pearls

💡The 1-minute rule: LOC lasting >1–2 minutes is very unlikely to be simple vasovagal syncope and mandates immediate evaluation for arrhythmia, seizure, or structural cardiac disease. A 20–30 minute episode in a patient with resting HR of 40 bpm is a cardiac emergency until proven otherwise.
🫀Ictal asystole is a rare but real phenomenon — cardiac asystole triggered by seizure activity (particularly temporal lobe). Patients may be worked up for cardiac arrhythmia when seizure is the primary cause. ILR with simultaneous EEG recording (e.g., Holter + video EEG) can identify this overlap.
🌙Nocturnal events in an adult suggest: nocturnal frontal lobe epilepsy (NFLE), REM sleep behavior disorder, sleep-related cardiac arrhythmia, or nocturnal seizures from any cause. NFLE episodes are often brief (<2 min), hypermotor, and occur in clusters. They are frequently misdiagnosed as parasomnias.
🧠Bilateral ulnar wrist abrasions from a bracing fall are more consistent with syncope (patient maintained reflexes and reached out to break fall) than generalized tonic-clonic seizure (which typically causes complete postural loss without protective response). However, partial seizures or the early tonic phase can still produce protective responses.
💊Cannabis use: △9-THC can cause vasovagal syncope via peripheral vasodilation and bradycardia (cannabinoid receptor activation). However, this is more typically associated with acute intoxication. A 1-week use pattern is less likely to explain recurring events over 3 years.
