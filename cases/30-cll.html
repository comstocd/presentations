Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia

CLL is the most common leukemia in adults in Western countries. Most patients are asymptomatic at diagnosis and many never require treatment. When treatment becomes necessary, the landscape has shifted decisively from chemoimmunotherapy to targeted agents — BTK inhibitors and BCL2 inhibitors — with profound improvements in outcomes. Every internist encounters CLL; knowing when to watch, when to refer, and what the targets mean is essential.

Epidemiology & Overview

  • Most common leukemia in adults in Western countries
  • Median age at diagnosis: ~72 years; rare before age 40
  • Male predominance (~60%)
  • Incidence: approximately 20,000 new cases per year in the US (I believe this is approximate; verify from current SEER data)
  • ~70% of patients are asymptomatic at diagnosis — found incidentally on CBC
  • Course is highly variable: some patients never require treatment; others have aggressive disease requiring early intervention
  • Median survival has improved dramatically with targeted therapy; many patients with low-risk CLL have near-normal life expectancy

CLL Is Not an Emergency — But It Requires a Framework

The most important initial decisions in CLL are: (1) confirm the diagnosis with flow cytometry, (2) exclude mantle cell lymphoma, (3) assess prognostic markers before any treatment is contemplated, and (4) resist the urge to treat based on lymphocyte count alone. Most patients benefit from initial observation.

Two Core Pathobiological Mechanisms

BCL2 Overexpression → Impaired Apoptosis

BCL2 (B-cell leukemia/lymphoma 2) is the master anti-apoptotic protein. CLL cells overexpress BCL2, preventing programmed cell death and allowing malignant B cells to accumulate.

Therapeutic target: Venetoclax (BCL2 inhibitor) directly inhibits this protein, restoring apoptosis in CLL cells.

Constitutive BCR Signaling → Proliferation

Constitutive activation of the B-cell receptor (BCR) pathway drives CLL cell proliferation via downstream kinases including Bruton tyrosine kinase (BTK) and PI3Kδ.

Therapeutic targets: BTK inhibitors (ibrutinib, acalabrutinib, zanubrutinib) block BTK; PI3K inhibitors (idelalisib, duvelisib) block the PI3K arm.

Morphology on Peripheral Smear

  • Small, mature lymphocytes with condensed chromatin ("soccer ball" or "cracked mud" chromatin) and scant cytoplasm
  • Smudge cells (Gumprecht nuclear shadows) — fragile CLL cells ruptured during slide preparation; classic finding on peripheral smear; more smudge cells generally correlates with better prognosis

Prognostic Markers

Prognostic Testing: Informative at Diagnosis, Essential Before Treatment

Comprehensive prognostic testing is not required at diagnosis if the patient is asymptomatic and will be observed. However, it is essential before initiating any treatment, as results determine appropriate therapy selection (particularly del(17p)/TP53 status, which precludes chemoimmunotherapy).

Clinical Presentation

Incidental Discovery (Most Common)

Approximately 70% of patients are asymptomatic at diagnosis, identified by unexplained lymphocytosis on routine CBC. The absolute lymphocyte count (ALC) is typically >5 × 10&sup9;/L and may be markedly elevated (>100 × 10&sup9;/L) without symptoms.

Richter Transformation: Always Consider in Sudden Clinical Change

Any CLL patient with sudden constitutional symptoms, rapidly enlarging lymph nodes, markedly elevated LDH, or new extranodal disease should be evaluated urgently for Richter transformation. PET-CT is useful to identify the highest-metabolic-activity node for targeted biopsy. Transformation to DLBCL carries a median survival of approximately 1 year even with treatment.

MBL (monoclonal B-cell lymphocytosis) <5 × 10&sup9;/L No lymphadenopathy, organomegaly, cytopenias, or disease-related symptoms Observe; progresses to CLL requiring treatment at approximately 1–2% per year; annual CBC

Excluding Mantle Cell Lymphoma

CD5+ B-Cell Lymphoma: Always Exclude MCL

Both CLL and mantle cell lymphoma (MCL) are CD5+ B-cell malignancies and can appear similar on initial flow cytometry. MCL is an aggressive lymphoma requiring prompt treatment; CLL is often observed. Distinguishing features: CLL is CD23+, cyclin D1−, FMC7−/dim, CD20 dim; MCL is CD23−/dim, cyclin D1+, t(11;14) by FISH, FMC7+, CD20 bright. FISH for t(11;14) and/or cyclin D1 staining should be performed whenever the immunophenotype is atypical or CD23 is negative.

Required Diagnostic Workup

  • Peripheral blood flow cytometry — necessary and often sufficient for CLL diagnosis
  • CBC with differential — ALC, degree of anemia, thrombocytopenia
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel — LDH (Richter concern), uric acid, hepatic function
  • Direct antiglobulin test (DAT) — screen for autoimmune hemolytic anemia
  • Quantitative immunoglobulins — establish baseline for hypogammaglobulinemia monitoring
  • Bone marrow biopsy — not required for CLL diagnosis; used to evaluate unexplained cytopenias (distinguish immune from marrow infiltration) or before treatment in selected cases
  • CT scan — not routinely required at diagnosis; used for staging and treatment planning

Prognostic Testing (Before Treatment)

  • FISH panel: del(17p), del(11q), del(13q), trisomy 12
  • TP53 sequencing (detects mutations even without del(17p) on FISH)
  • IGHV mutation status
  • Beta-2-microglobulin
  • CpG-stimulated metaphase karyotype (detects complex karyotype)

Staging Limitations

Rai and Binet staging are based on clinical examination and CBC alone and were developed before modern molecular markers. They remain useful for broadly communicating disease burden and prognosis but do not capture the key prognostic information provided by IGHV status, del(17p)/TP53, and cytogenetics. Staging alone should not drive treatment decisions without the full molecular context.

When to Treat

Watch and Wait Is the Standard for Asymptomatic CLL

Multiple randomized trials have shown that early treatment of asymptomatic CLL does not improve survival compared to treatment at the time of disease progression. Asymptomatic patients, regardless of stage or lymphocyte count, should be observed. Early treatment exposes patients to side effects without benefit. The absolute lymphocyte count alone — no matter how high — is not an indication for treatment.

NCCN Treatment Indications (Active Disease)

Treatment is indicated when any of the following are present:

  • Significant B symptoms (drenching night sweats, weight loss ≥10% in 6 months, fever >38°C for ≥2 weeks without infection)
  • Progressive or symptomatic splenomegaly (>6 cm below left costal margin)
  • Progressive or symptomatic lymphadenopathy (>10 cm in longest diameter, or rapidly progressive)
  • Progressive anemia and/or thrombocytopenia due to marrow infiltration (Rai stage III/IV, Binet C)
  • Steroid-refractory autoimmune hemolytic anemia or immune thrombocytopenia
  • Rapid lymphocyte doubling time — doubling in <6 months (with ALC >30 × 10&sup9;/L); or doubling in <2 months
  • Symptomatic or threatened end-organ dysfunction from CLL infiltration

Not an indication: Elevated ALC alone, positive prognostic markers without symptoms, or patient/physician anxiety about a high WBC.

First-Line Treatment

The Shift to Targeted Therapy

First-line treatment for CLL has shifted decisively from chemoimmunotherapy (FCR, BR) to targeted agents. BTK inhibitors and BCL2 inhibitors are now the preferred first-line options for virtually all patients. Chemoimmunotherapy retains a role in a narrow group: young, fit patients with mutated IGHV and no del(17p)/TP53, where FCR can provide long-term remissions — but this population is small and this approach is less commonly chosen as targeted options mature.

With del(17p) / TP53 Mutation — Same Targeted Agents, No Chemoimmunotherapy

del(17p) and TP53 mutations confer resistance to all chemoimmunotherapy regimens. The same BTK inhibitor and BCL2 inhibitor-based regimens are recommended as for standard-risk CLL. Chemoimmunotherapy (FCR, BR, chlorambucil-based) is not appropriate in this population. Outcomes with targeted agents are similar regardless of del(17p) status, though long-term follow-up data continue to mature.

No Proven Advantage to Sequencing Order

There is currently no evidence that starting with a BTK inhibitor before a BCL2 inhibitor (or vice versa) improves overall outcomes. Both classes are available at relapse if the other was used first-line. Patient-specific factors — comorbidities, preference for continuous vs. time-limited therapy, TLS risk, AF history, and bleeding risk — should drive the choice.

MRD-Guided Therapy: An Emerging Paradigm

Measurable residual disease (MRD) refers to CLL cells detectable below the level of standard morphologic assessment, measured by flow cytometry or PCR. Achieving undetectable MRD (uMRD) is associated with prolonged progression-free survival. The FLAIR trial (Munir et al., NEJM 2025) demonstrated that MRD-guided ibrutinib–venetoclax was superior to both ibrutinib monotherapy and FCR, allowing individualized treatment duration based on uMRD status — a potential pathway to treatment-free remission for selected patients.

Role of Chemoimmunotherapy in 2025

FCR (fludarabine, cyclophosphamide, rituximab) may still be considered in young (<65), fit patients with mutated IGHV and no del(17p)/TP53, where long-term remissions (>10 years) are observed in a subset. However, this population is narrow, and increasing evidence supports targeted agents even here due to superior tolerability. Ibrutinib and obinutuzumab-chlorambucil are category 2A options for frail/elderly patients. Chemoimmunotherapy is never appropriate for del(17p)/TP53-mutant disease.

BTK Inhibitor Resistance Mutations

Resistance to covalent BTK inhibitors (ibrutinib, acalabrutinib, zanubrutinib) most commonly occurs via BTK C481S mutation, which prevents covalent drug binding. Pirtobrutinib is a noncovalent (reversible) BTK inhibitor that retains activity against C481S-mutant BTK and has demonstrated meaningful response rates in patients who have progressed on covalent BTK inhibitors. It is an important addition to the relapsed/refractory armamentarium.

Complications & Supportive Care

Infectious Complications

  • Hypogammaglobulinemia is common and worsens with treatment; monitor IgG levels; intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) replacement is indicated for recurrent serious bacterial infections (sinopulmonary) with documented IgG <500 mg/dL — does not improve survival but reduces infection frequency
  • Opportunistic infections increase with targeted and chemoimmunotherapy: PCP prophylaxis with TMP-SMX (or dapsone/inhaled pentamidine if sulfa allergy) during treatment; antiviral prophylaxis (acyclovir/valacyclovir) for herpes reactivation; anti-fungal prophylaxis in selected high-risk patients
  • Hepatitis B reactivation risk: screen all patients with HBsAg, anti-HBc, and anti-HBs before treatment; anti-HBc-positive patients require monitoring or prophylactic antiviral therapy during treatment
  • COVID-19 and respiratory viruses: impaired humoral response to vaccination; may not mount adequate antibody response; consider pre-exposure prophylaxis strategies in high-risk patients

Autoimmune Complications

  • AIHA: Treat with corticosteroids (prednisone 1 mg/kg); rituximab for steroid-refractory; CLL-directed therapy may be needed if AIHA is refractory
  • ITP: Similar approach to AIHA; IVIG for acute severe thrombocytopenia; eltrombopag or romiplostim for refractory ITP; distinguish from marrow infiltration with bone marrow biopsy
  • Pure red cell aplasia (PRCA): Rare; treat with cyclosporine

Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS) with Venetoclax

Venetoclax carries significant TLS risk due to rapid apoptosis of CLL cells. A mandatory dose ramp-up over 5 weeks is required (20 → 50 → 100 → 200 → 400 mg daily), with TLS risk assessment at each dose level based on ALC and lymph node size. High-risk patients require hospitalization for initial doses. Prophylaxis: allopurinol, aggressive hydration, electrolyte monitoring, uric acid monitoring.

Vaccination & Preventive Care

  • Vaccinate before treatment if possible (better immune response)
  • Inactivated vaccines recommended: influenza annually, pneumococcal (PCV20 or PCV15 + PPSV23), COVID-19, shingles (recombinant Shingrix preferred over live ZVL)
  • Live vaccines (MMR, varicella, yellow fever) generally avoided during treatment
  • Age-appropriate cancer screening should continue

Clinical Pearls

The lymphocyte count is almost never the reason to treat. CLL patients with ALC of 200,000/μL who are asymptomatic should be observed, not treated. The watch-and-wait strategy has been validated in multiple randomized trials showing no survival benefit to early treatment. The decision to treat is based on symptoms, cytopenias from marrow infiltration, and rapidly progressive disease — not a number on the CBC.
Always exclude mantle cell lymphoma before calling it CLL. Both are CD5+ B-cell malignancies. MCL is aggressive and requires immediate treatment; CLL is often observed for years. Check CD23, cyclin D1, and FISH for t(11;14) whenever the immunophenotype is atypical, CD23 is negative, or CD20 is brightly expressed. Misclassifying MCL as CLL is a serious diagnostic error.
del(17p) and TP53 mutations must be checked before any treatment decision. These mutations confer resistance to all chemoimmunotherapy. A patient treated with FCR who has del(17p) will not respond and will be exposed to significant toxicity. BTK or BCL2 inhibitor-based therapy is the only appropriate choice in this setting. TP53 sequencing detects mutations even when FISH for del(17p) is negative — both tests are needed.
Ibrutinib's cardiovascular toxicity is a real clinical problem. Atrial fibrillation occurs in approximately 10–16% of patients on ibrutinib, with rates increasing with age and duration of therapy. Hypertension, ventricular arrhythmias, and bleeding (through BTK-mediated platelet inhibition) are also recognized. Second-generation agents (acalabrutinib, zanubrutinib) have substantially improved cardiovascular safety profiles and are generally preferred for new patients requiring a BTK inhibitor.
Smudge cells on peripheral smear point toward CLL. The fragility of CLL cells during blood smear preparation produces characteristic Gumprecht nuclear shadows (smudge cells). Their presence in a patient with lymphocytosis should prompt flow cytometry. A high smudge cell count has been associated with more indolent disease in some series — a counterintuitive but clinically useful observation.
Sudden clinical deterioration in a CLL patient = Richter transformation until proven otherwise. A patient with stable, indolent CLL who develops rapidly enlarging lymph nodes, fever, night sweats, and a markedly elevated LDH has Richter transformation until a biopsy proves otherwise. PET-CT identifies the highest-avidity node; targeted biopsy is essential. This is a medical urgency — transformation to DLBCL carries a poor prognosis and requires prompt oncology referral.
