Answer
Most likely etiology: ETEC (Enterotoxigenic E. coli) — the leading cause of traveler's diarrhea, accounting for ~30–40% of cases. Produces heat-labile (LT) and/or heat-stable (ST) enterotoxins that drive secretory diarrhea via cAMP/cGMP. Not covered by standard GI multiplex PCR panels.
  • Azithromycin 1g single dose or 500mg daily × 3 days — preferred in most regions due to rising FQ resistance; also covers Campylobacter
  • Rifaximin 200mg TID × 3 days — non-invasive; stays in gut; ideal for non-bloody watery diarrhea; not effective if invasive organism suspected
  • Loperamide can be co-administered for symptom control in non-bloody, non-febrile illness
  • Ciprofloxacin — still used but FQ resistance in Campylobacter exceeds 25% in many regions; no longer first-line empirically
Answer
Do NOT start antibiotics. Antibiotic use in STEC infection is associated with increased risk of Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS). Proposed mechanism: antibiotics lyse bacteria → massive Shiga toxin release → systemic absorption → endothelial injury → thrombotic microangiopathy.
  • Antibiotics — contraindicated; avoid FQs, TMP-SMX, and beta-lactams
  • Loperamide — contraindicated; prolongs mucosal toxin contact time
  • IV fluid resuscitation — cornerstone of management; maintain perfusion to protect kidneys
  • Monitor CBC, creatinine, urinalysis q24–48h for HUS triad: microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, AKI
  • Nephrology consult if creatinine rising or thrombocytopenia develops
  • Notify public health — STEC is a reportable condition; outbreak investigation may be warranted
Answer
There is no single correct answer — this is a stewardship judgment call. Consider:
  • Specimen quality matters: Was the PCR performed on a well-collected, promptly transported specimen? A transport delay (as in this case) raises the possibility of false negative — though NAAT is generally robust
  • Azithromycin is a motilin receptor agonist — prokinetic effect may be perpetuating diarrhea in the absence of a confirmed target; completing the course may worsen symptoms
  • If day 4 of 5 and clinically stable: Reasonable to complete — marginal stewardship gain from stopping one day early
  • If symptoms worsening or prolonged: Consider stopping and reassessing — continuing without a target violates core stewardship principles
  • Document your reasoning — "empiric azithromycin initiated for presumed Campylobacter; GI PCR returned negative; course completed given proximity to completion and clinical stability" is a defensible note
Answer
Worsening disturbance: Hyperchloremic Non-Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis (NAGMA). Normal saline (Na 154, Cl 154 mEq/L) delivers a large chloride load. Combined with ongoing GI bicarbonate losses from diarrhea, this further drives HCO₃ down via hyperchloremic displacement and dilution.
  • Normal saline → worsens hyperchloremia; worsens NAGMA; avoid in diarrheal illness with metabolic acidosis
  • Lactated Ringer's (Na 130, Cl 109, lactate 28 mEq/L) — lactate is metabolized to bicarbonate by the liver; lower chloride avoids worsening the acidosis; first-line for diarrheal dehydration
  • Potassium repletion: K 3.2 requires active repletion; goal ≥4.0 given ongoing losses. Each 0.1 mEq/L rise in serum K reflects ~100–200 mEq of total-body deficit at lower values
  • Check magnesium: Hypomagnesemia impairs renal potassium retention and can render K repletion ineffective; correct Mg first
  • Oral rehydration solution (ORS) remains appropriate if the patient can tolerate PO; WHO ORS uses sodium-glucose cotransport mechanism — independent of the secretory defect
Answer
This case illustrates two independent failures: transport (specimen never moved) and communication (result not relayed after it was finalized). Systems interventions should address both:
  • Automated transport confirmation: LIS alert generated if specimen is not logged at reference lab within expected window (e.g., 4 hours post-collection)
  • Weekend transport protocol: Designated backup transport for time-sensitive specimens collected Friday PM or weekends; standing arrangement with secondary reference lab
  • Provider notification loop: Automatic flag to ordering provider if specimen not received within expected timeframe — silent failures become visible
  • Patient portal notification: MyChart/portal message with expected result timeframe; automated delay alert if result not returned on schedule
  • Equity lens: A non-physician patient cannot call the reference lab, identify the failure, or arrange redirection. The safeguard must exist in the system, not rely on patient sophistication
  • Formal RCA: Every specimen transport failure should enter the patient safety reporting system; root cause analysis to identify whether this is a recurring gap vs. isolated incident
  • Stewardship implication: Diagnostic delays enable empiric antibiotic use without confirmed targets — patient safety and stewardship are inseparable in this context
Answer
Not necessarily cleared — the clinical picture is encouraging but several criteria must be explicitly met before return to direct patient care.
  • Loperamide suppression ≠ symptom-free. CDC/HICPAC and most institutional policies require symptom-free status — not pharmacologically controlled symptoms. The resident must demonstrate absence of diarrhea without relying on loperamide, or the clock has not started.
  • 48-hour minimum: CDC/HICPAC requires at least 48 hours symptom-free after the last episode of diarrhea before returning to direct patient care.
  • 72 hours at many institutions for direct patient care roles — confirm your institutional policy. Do not self-clear; Employee Health or Occupational Medicine makes this determination.
  • Formed stool is the clinical benchmark — not reduced frequency alone. Two formed BMs per day without loperamide support would be reassuring; two formed BMs on loperamide leaves the underlying status unclear.
  • Pathogen-specific escalations: If STEC, Shigella, Salmonella, or Hepatitis A were confirmed, exclusion criteria are significantly more stringent (multiple negative cultures required). Unknown etiology defaults to standard gastroenteritis protocol.
  • Documentation required: Physician clearance letter specifying the anticipated return date, uploaded to the institutional occupational health system (e.g., ReadySet). A note from the treating physician or ED physician of record within the same health system is typically acceptable.
  • Notify the rotation supervisor and GME coordinator of the expected return date in writing — protects the resident if the process encounters delays outside their control.
