Vestibular Migraine: Ruling Out Posterior Stroke

A patient presents with acute dizziness, ataxia, and nystagmus — prompting urgent evaluation to distinguish vestibular migraine from posterior circulation stroke. HINTS exam, MRI timing, and diagnostic criteria guide the workup.

Presentation

Chief Complaint

Acute onset severe dizziness, inability to walk (ataxia), and eye movement abnormalities (nystagmus) — brought in by EMS. No loss of consciousness, no headache at onset (though headache has followed in past episodes).

  • Truncal ataxia — cannot stand or walk unassisted
  • Horizontal nystagmus (present on exam)
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • No diplopia, no dysarthria, no dysphagia
  • No facial droop, no limb weakness
  • Mild photophobia

  • History of migraines with aura (visual)

Posterior Stroke: Why It's the Priority to Rule Out

Posterior Circulation Stroke — Cannot Be Missed

  • Posterior circulation territory (vertebrobasilar) supplies: cerebellum, brainstem, occipital cortex, thalami
  • Mimics: BPPV, vestibular neuritis, Ménière's disease, vestibular migraine
  • Isolated dizziness/vertigo without typical "stroke symptoms" (hemiplegia, aphasia) is the presentation in ~10% of posterior strokes
  • Untreated cerebellar stroke → mass effect → brainstem compression → herniation and death
  • Cerebellar hemorrhage: treat with emergent surgical decompression if >3 cm or hydrocephalus
  • tPA window: 3–4.5 hours from symptom onset; must not be delayed

The HINTS Exam: Ruling Out Central Pathology at Bedside

HINTS = Head Impulse · Nystagmus · Test of Skew

In patients with acute vestibular syndrome (continuous vertigo + nystagmus), HINTS outperforms early MRI/DWI for posterior stroke detection. Sensitivity ~100%, specificity ~96% (Kattah et al., Stroke 2009). Early DWI can be false negative in 17–20% of posterior fossa strokes within 24–48 hours.

ComponentPeripheral (Benign)Central (Stroke — Worry)
H — Head Impulse Test (HIT)
Rapid head rotation; observe eyes		 Abnormal (corrective saccade visible) = peripheral VOR deficit; reassuring Normal HIT (no corrective saccade) = central pathology; concerning for stroke
I — Nystagmus Type Unidirectional, horizontal, fast phase away from lesion; suppressed by fixation Direction-changing (gaze-evoked); vertical; torsional; not suppressed by fixation
TS — Test of Skew
Alternate cover test; look for vertical deviation		 No skew deviation Vertical skew (hypertropia) = central; brainstem ocular pathway dysfunction
Interpretation: HINTS "PLUS"
  • PERIPHERAL (reassuring) pattern: Abnormal HIT + Unidirectional nystagmus + No skew
  • CENTRAL (stroke) pattern: Normal HIT + Direction-changing nystagmus + Skew deviation (any ONE of these = central)
  • HINTS Plus: Add new hearing loss (unilateral, sudden) → AICA territory infarct despite otherwise peripheral-appearing HINTS

Diagnostic Criteria for Vestibular Migraine

Bárány Society / IHS Criteria for Vestibular Migraine (2012)

A. At least 5 episodes with vestibular symptoms of moderate-to-severe intensity, lasting 5 minutes to 72 hours

B. Current or previous history of migraine with or without aura (ICHD criteria)

C. One or more migraine features with at least 50% of vestibular episodes:

  • Headache with ≥2 of: unilateral, pulsating, moderate/severe, aggravated by activity
  • Photophobia AND phonophobia
  • Visual aura

D. Not better accounted for by another vestibular or ICHD diagnosis

This Patient: Does She Meet Criteria?

  • ✅ At least 2–3 prior similar episodes lasting hours (approaching 5 episode threshold)
  • ✅ History of migraine with visual aura
  • ✅ Photophobia present with current episode
  • ✅ No alternative diagnosis (posterior stroke excluded by HINTS + MRI)
  • ⚠️ May not yet meet full Bárány criteria (5 episodes) — "Probable vestibular migraine" if criteria partially met

MRI Timing in Posterior Stroke: The False Negative Problem

DWI False Negative Rate in the First 24 Hours

DWI (diffusion-weighted MRI) is the gold standard for acute ischemic stroke — but in the posterior fossa, sensitivity drops to ~72–80% within the first 24–48 hours (Chalela et al., Lancet 2007; Wintermark et al., Radiology 2015). Small brainstem and cerebellar infarcts may not be visible on early DWI. This is clinically critical: a negative MRI in the first 24 hours does NOT rule out posterior stroke if the HINTS exam suggests central pathology. Repeat DWI at 48–72 hours is recommended if clinical suspicion remains high.

When to Image and What to Order

  • Non-contrast CT head first: Rules out hemorrhage (visible immediately), large structural lesions
  • MRI brain with DWI + FLAIR + gradient echo + MRA head/neck: Standard stroke protocol
  • Best timing for posterior fossa DWI: >24–48 hours from symptom onset (higher sensitivity)
  • If HINTS suggests central pathology and early MRI is negative → admit, antiplatelet therapy, repeat MRI at 48h

Management of Vestibular Migraine

Acute Episode

  • Triptans (e.g., sumatriptan 50–100 mg PO): effective if headache present; evidence for vestibular migraine growing (Level C)
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) — if contraindications to triptans
  • Antiemetics: prochlorperazine 10 mg IV or promethazine 12.5–25 mg IV (for nausea/vomiting control)
  • Vestibular suppressants: Meclizine 25–50 mg PO TID PRN for acute dizziness (use short-term; chronic use inhibits vestibular compensation)
  • Benzodiazepines: diazepam 2–5 mg for severe acute vestibular crisis — not for chronic use
  • Dark, quiet room; IV fluids if dehydrated

Preventive Therapy (if ≥4 attacks/month or disabling)

First-Line Preventive Options

  • Propranolol 40–120 mg BID — first-line migraine prevention; also addresses vestibular component. Avoid if asthma, bradycardia.
  • Amitriptyline 10–75 mg qHS — particularly useful if comorbid insomnia or tension-type headache
  • Valproate 250–500 mg BID — Level A evidence for migraine prevention; teratogenic — discuss with women of childbearing age
  • Topiramate 25–100 mg/day — Level A; also used in vestibular migraine
  • Venlafaxine 37.5–150 mg/day — evidence for vestibular migraine specifically (Staab et al.)
  • CGRP monoclonal antibodies (erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab): highly effective for chronic migraine; evidence accumulating for vestibular migraine

OCP Consideration in Migraine with Aura

⚠️ Combined OCPs are Contraindicated in Migraine with Aura

WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria (Category 4): Combined estrogen-progestin contraceptives are contraindicated in migraine with aura due to increased ischemic stroke risk (RR ~2–3×, multiplicative with smoking). This patient should be counseled to discontinue combined OCPs and transition to progestin-only or non-hormonal contraception. This is an actionable finding from this case.

Vestibular Rehabilitation Therapy

Vestibular PT helps with chronic balance dysfunction and inter-episodic dizziness. Less clearly beneficial for vestibular migraine than for vestibular neuritis, but can complement pharmacotherapy.

Clinical Pearls

🎯HINTS is a bedside clinical exam, not a radiology order. It should be performed in the ED for ANY patient with acute vestibular syndrome (continuous vertigo + nystagmus at rest). A normal head impulse test (no corrective saccade) + direction-changing nystagmus = central pathology = cannot be discharged with vestibular neuritis diagnosis.
🧠Early MRI/DWI can be false negative for posterior stroke. A negative MRI within 24 hours does NOT rule out cerebellar or brainstem infarct. If HINTS suggests central pathology, admit and repeat MRI at 48–72 hours. This is one of the most dangerous diagnostic pitfalls in emergency neurology.
💊Vestibular suppressants (meclizine, scopolamine) suppress the vestibular system — effective for acute symptom relief, but chronic use actually inhibits the brain's natural vestibular compensation process and prolongs recovery. Limit to acute phase use (days, not weeks).
⚠️Combined oral contraceptives in women with migraine with aura carry a 3–6× increased stroke risk. This is an absolute contraindication (WHO Category 4). The relative risk compounds with smoking (up to 30×). Every patient with migraine with aura on combined OCPs should have this contraindication addressed.
💡Vestibular migraine is the most common cause of episodic vertigo in adults, and the most commonly missed vestibular diagnosis. It is vastly underdiagnosed because: (1) headache may be absent, (2) prior migraine history may not be recognized by the patient as migraine, and (3) diagnostic criteria were only formally established in 2012.
