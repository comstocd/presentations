Pain Classification

By Duration

  • Acute: <4 weeks; expected tissue healing phase; nociceptive
  • Sub-acute: 4–12 weeks; transitional zone; high risk for chronification
  • Chronic: >12 weeks; persists beyond expected healing; sensitization mechanisms predominate
  • Chronic overlapping pain conditions (COPCs): fibromyalgia, IBS, chronic fatigue, TMD, chronic headache — shared CNS mechanisms

By Mechanism

  • Nociceptive — Somatic: Tissue damage; well-localized; responds to NSAIDs/opioids; e.g., fracture, surgical wound
  • Nociceptive — Visceral: Poorly localized, crampy, referred; e.g., pancreatitis, angina
  • Neuropathic: Ectopic neural firing; burning, shooting, allodynia, hyperalgesia; e.g., diabetic neuropathy, postherpetic neuralgia. NSAIDs less effective; requires adjuvants (SNRIs, gabapentinoids, TCAs)
  • Nociplastic / Central sensitization: Altered nociceptive processing without tissue/nerve injury; e.g., fibromyalgia, CRPS. Opioids generally ineffective or harmful.

Biopsychosocial Model of Chronic Pain

Engel's Model Applied to Pain

Chronic pain is not simply a biomedical phenomenon — it is the product of interacting biological, psychological, and social factors. This has profound implications for treatment: pure pharmacotherapy (especially opioids) targeting only biological mechanisms will be inadequate for complex chronic pain.

Biological

  • Tissue pathology (arthritis, disc disease)
  • Peripheral sensitization
  • Central sensitization (spinal dorsal horn, descending modulation)
  • Genetic pain susceptibility (COMT, OPRM1 polymorphisms)
  • Prior opioid exposure → opioid-induced hyperalgesia

Psychological

  • Catastrophizing ("pain will never get better") — strongest predictor of chronic pain disability
  • Fear-avoidance behaviors
  • Depression and anxiety (bidirectional with pain)
  • Trauma / PTSD
  • Pain self-efficacy (belief in ability to manage pain)

Social

  • Socioeconomic factors: work stress, job demands, job loss, insurance access
  • Social support: isolation predicts poor outcomes
  • Disability and legal factors: medicolegal context affects pain behavior
  • Healthcare access disparities (racial, geographic)
  • Cultural pain expression norms

Pain Assessment

Beyond the VAS: Multidimensional Assessment

  • Numeric Rating Scale (NRS 0–10): Simple; unidimensional; useful for tracking
  • Brief Pain Inventory (BPI): Severity + functional interference; validated
  • PainDETECT: Screens for neuropathic component (score ≥19 = likely neuropathic)
  • PHQ-9: Depression — mandatory in chronic pain evaluation
  • GAD-7: Anxiety — comorbid anxiety predicts poor pain outcomes
  • PROMIS: Patient-Reported Outcomes Measurement Information System — increasingly used in research and clinical care

Functional Assessment (More Important than Pain Score)

Ask about function, not just pain score. "How far can you walk? Can you do your grocery shopping? Sleep through the night? Work?" Functional improvement is the treatment goal — pain reduction alone is not.

Multimodal Analgesia

WHO Analgesic Ladder — Updated Interpretation

The original WHO ladder (1986) was designed for cancer pain. For non-cancer chronic pain, the modern interpretation emphasizes:

  1. Step 1: Non-pharmacological interventions (physical therapy, CBT, acupuncture, TENS) — FIRST LINE for all chronic pain
  2. Step 2: Non-opioid pharmacotherapy (NSAIDs, acetaminophen, topical agents, adjuvants) — should be maximized before opioids
  3. Step 3: Opioids — for moderate-severe pain unresponsive to Steps 1–2; lowest effective dose, shortest effective duration; ongoing monitoring

CDC 2022 Clinical Practice Guideline — Key Recommendations

Major Shift from CDC 2016 to 2022

The 2022 guideline moved away from rigid dose thresholds (the 90 MME/day limit was widely misapplied) toward individualized, patient-centered care. It explicitly states: "The clinical practice guideline is not intended as a rule or law… not a replacement for clinical judgment."

CDC 2022: Top Recommendations

  1. Non-pharmacological therapy first: Exercise, PT, CBT, interdisciplinary pain programs are recommended before initiating opioids for chronic non-cancer pain.
  2. Non-opioid pharmacotherapy preferred for most types of chronic pain (evidence often better than opioids for long-term outcomes).
  3. If opioids prescribed: Use lowest effective dose; combine with non-opioid therapy; set realistic treatment goals (function, not pain elimination).
  4. Short-acting before long-acting: For acute/sub-acute pain — use IR opioids. Extended-release/long-acting (ER/LA) formulations are not for episodic pain.
  5. Prescription drug monitoring program (PDMP): Review before prescribing opioids; check for concurrent prescriptions.
  6. Avoid concurrent benzodiazepines + opioids: Increases overdose mortality ×3–4; avoid when possible; if both necessary, ensure lowest possible doses.
  7. Naloxone co-prescription: Prescribe for any patient on ≥50 MME/day or with concurrent CNS depressants, OUD history, or respiratory disease.
  8. OUD treatment: If OUD develops — offer/refer to buprenorphine or methadone treatment; do not withhold treatment.

Opioid Prescribing: Risk Stratification & Safety

Opioid Risk Tool (ORT)

ORT Scoring — Prior to Opioid Initiation
  • Family history of substance abuse (alcohol: 3; illegal drugs: 3; Rx drugs: 4)
  • Personal history of substance abuse (alcohol: 3; illegal drugs: 4; Rx drugs: 5)
  • Age 16–45: 1 point
  • History of preadolescent sexual abuse: 3 points
  • Psychological disease (ADD/ADHD, OCD, bipolar, schizophrenia: 2; depression: 1)
  • Score 0–3: Low risk. 4–7: Moderate. ≥8: High risk of opioid misuse.

Opioid Side Effects & Management

  • Constipation: Does NOT resolve with tolerance. Treat prophylactically. Peripherally-acting mu-opioid receptor antagonists (PAMORAs): methylnaltrexone, naloxegol, naldemedine — for opioid-induced constipation (OIC).
  • Sedation/cognitive impairment: Reduces with tolerance; driving restriction until stable dose
  • Nausea: Usually resolves in 1–2 weeks; prochlorperazine or ondansetron for acute management
  • Respiratory depression: Dose-related; compounded by CNS depressants, sleep apnea, COPD. Naloxone (0.4–2 mg IN/IV) for reversal.
  • Opioid-induced hyperalgesia (OIH): Paradoxical increase in pain sensitivity with opioid use; treat with opioid dose reduction, rotation, or cessation + non-opioid alternatives

Clinical Pearls

💡Pain catastrophizing — the tendency to magnify the threat value of pain and feel helpless — is the single strongest psychosocial predictor of pain chronification and disability. Pain Catastrophizing Scale (PCS) score >30 identifies patients who will benefit most from CBT-based pain interventions, regardless of physical findings.
⚠️Opioid-induced hyperalgesia (OIH) causes increasing pain sensitivity with escalating opioid doses — paradoxical to the desired effect. It is clinically indistinguishable from opioid tolerance. The clue: pain score worsening despite dose increases, with generalized (not localized) hyperalgesia. Treatment: opioid dose reduction or rotation; add ketamine or methadone.
🔬Gabapentinoids (gabapentin, pregabalin) are widely overprescribed for non-neuropathic pain. Evidence for gabapentin in acute surgical pain, low back pain without radiculopathy, and chronic non-neuropathic pain is weak to absent. They are Schedule V controlled substances in most US states due to misuse potential and overdose risk — especially in combination with opioids.
📊The CDC 2022 guideline removed the 90 MME/day "hard limit" from the 2016 guideline, which had led to abrupt, medically harmful dose tapers in patients with legitimate chronic pain. High-dose opioid therapy still carries risk — but tapering should be collaborative, slow, and voluntary when possible, not driven by a prescription database threshold.
🩺Treat pain by mechanism, not by severity score alone. A VAS 8/10 neuropathic pain from diabetic neuropathy will not respond to opioids — it needs duloxetine or gabapentin. A VAS 4/10 somatic pain from an inflammatory arthritis may respond beautifully to an NSAID. Mechanism-guided prescribing reduces opioid overuse and improves outcomes.
